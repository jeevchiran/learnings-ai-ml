Module 10: N-gram Language Models & Applications

Bridge from Module 9 — Sequences of Words

From meaning to probability. Module 9 gave us word embeddings — dense vectors capturing what words mean. Now we ask a different question: how probable is a sequence of words? A language model assigns probability to sentences. This lets us rank hypotheses in speech recognition ("recognize speech" vs. "wreck a nice beach"), generate text, and evaluate NLP systems.

A language model is a probability distribution over sequences of words. Given a sentence \(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n\), a language model tells us \(P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n)\) — how likely that sequence is in the language. This single capability unlocks a surprising range of applications.

Speech Recognition

\(P(\text{"I want to eat"}) > P(\text{"I wand two eat"})\) — pick the more probable transcription from among all acoustic hypotheses the recogniser generates.

Spell Correction

The noisy channel model (Module 2) required a prior \(P(w)\) over word sequences. A language model provides exactly that prior, weighting corrections by how natural they sound.

Machine Translation

Statistical MT generates many candidate translations; the language model scores each candidate by its fluency in the target language, helping select the most natural output.

N-gram Language Models — The Core Idea

How do we compute \(P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n)\)? We start with an exact formula, then introduce a practical approximation.

1
Chain Rule of Probability
Any joint probability can be decomposed exactly using the chain rule:
\[P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_n) = P(w_1) \cdot P(w_2 \mid w_1) \cdot P(w_3 \mid w_1 w_2) \cdots P(w_n \mid w_1 \ldots w_{n-1})\]
This is exact — but the conditioning history grows without bound as the sentence gets longer. A model would need to remember every possible prefix, which is computationally and statistically intractable for large vocabularies.
2
Markov Assumption
Approximate by looking back only \(k\) words instead of the entire history:
\[P(w_i \mid w_1 \ldots w_{i-1}) \approx P(w_i \mid w_{i-k} \ldots w_{i-1})\]
This gives three practically important special cases:
  • Unigram (\(k=0\)):  \(\displaystyle P(w_1 \ldots w_n) \approx \prod_i P(w_i)\) — words are independent.
  • Bigram (\(k=1\)):  \(\displaystyle P(w_1 \ldots w_n) \approx \prod_i P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})\) — each word depends only on the previous one.
  • Trigram (\(k=2\)):  \(\displaystyle P(w_1 \ldots w_n) \approx \prod_i P(w_i \mid w_{i-2}\, w_{i-1})\) — each word depends on the previous two.
3
Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)
For a bigram model, estimate probabilities by counting in the training corpus:
\[P(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}\, w_i)}{C(w_{i-1})}\]
The numerator counts how often the bigram \(w_{i-1}\, w_i\) appeared; the denominator counts how often the conditioning word \(w_{i-1}\) appeared. The ratio is the relative frequency — the MLE estimate.

Worked Example — Building a Bigram Model

Consider this tiny training corpus of four sentences:

  1. "I like cats"
  2. "I like dogs"
  3. "dogs like cats"
  4. "cats like dogs"

Adding sentence boundary tokens <s> and </s>, the bigram counts are:

</s> I like cats dogs
<s> 0 2 0 1 1
I 0 0 2 0 0
like 0 0 0 2 2
cats 1 0 1 0 0
dogs 1 0 1 0 0

Computing P("I like dogs") step by step:

\(P(\text{"I like dogs"}) = P(I \mid \langle s\rangle) \times P(\text{like} \mid I) \times P(\text{dogs} \mid \text{like}) \times P(\langle /s\rangle \mid \text{dogs})\)

\(= \dfrac{C(\langle s\rangle,\, I)}{C(\langle s\rangle)} \times \dfrac{C(I,\, \text{like})}{C(I)} \times \dfrac{C(\text{like},\, \text{dogs})}{C(\text{like})} \times \dfrac{C(\text{dogs},\, \langle /s\rangle)}{C(\text{dogs})}\)

\(= \dfrac{2}{4} \times \dfrac{2}{2} \times \dfrac{2}{4} \times \dfrac{1}{2} = 0.5 \times 1.0 \times 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.125\)

Data Sparsity & Laplace Smoothing

The Zero Problem If a bigram never appeared in training, MLE gives it probability 0. Then ANY sentence containing that bigram has probability 0 — even perfectly grammatical ones. A model that assigns zero probability to valid sentences is useless for ranking or generation. This is the data sparsity problem: no training corpus is large enough to observe every possible word pair.

The solution is smoothing — redistributing some probability mass from observed events to unobserved ones.

1
Laplace (Add-1) Smoothing
Add 1 to every bigram count (including unseen ones). To keep probabilities summing to 1, add \(|V|\) (vocabulary size) to every unigram count:
\[P_{\text{Laplace}}(w_i \mid w_{i-1}) = \frac{C(w_{i-1}\, w_i) + 1}{C(w_{i-1}) + |V|}\]
Every bigram now has at least count 1. No sentence receives probability 0.

Other Smoothing Methods

  • Add-k smoothing: Replace the 1 in Laplace with a smaller constant \(k < 1\). Hyperparameter \(k\) is tuned on a held-out set. Often works better in practice than add-1.
  • Interpolation / Backoff: Blend trigram, bigram, and unigram estimates: \(\hat{P}(w_i \mid w_{i-2}w_{i-1}) = \lambda_3 P_3 + \lambda_2 P_2 + \lambda_1 P_1\). If the trigram is unseen, back off to the bigram, then the unigram.
  • Kneser-Ney smoothing: The most widely used method in practice. Uses discounted counts and a special "continuation probability" that captures how likely a word is to appear in novel contexts, not just how frequent it is.

NLTK Code: Laplace-Smoothed Bigram Model

Python — NLTK Laplace LM 
from nltk.lm import Laplace
from nltk.lm.preprocessing import padded_everygram_pipeline
from nltk.util import ngrams

# Corpus
corpus = [
    "I like cats".split(),
    "I like dogs".split(),
    "dogs like cats".split(),
    "cats like dogs".split()
]

# Build bigram model with Laplace smoothing
train_data, vocab = padded_everygram_pipeline(2, corpus)
model = Laplace(2)
model.fit(train_data, vocab)

# Score sentences
print(model.score("cats", ["I", "like"]))   # P(cats | I like)
print(model.score("dogs", ["I", "like"]))   # P(dogs | I like)
print(model.logscore("dogs", ["like"]))     # log2 P(dogs | like)

# Generate text
from nltk.lm import MLE
train_data2, vocab2 = padded_everygram_pipeline(2, corpus)
mle_model = MLE(2)
mle_model.fit(train_data2, vocab2)
print(mle_model.generate(7, text_seed=["I"]))

Perplexity — Evaluating Language Models

How do we compare two language models without running a full downstream task? The standard intrinsic metric is perplexity: measure how surprised the model is by a held-out test set.

For a test set \(W = w_1 w_2 \ldots w_N\):

\[PP(W) = P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_N)^{-\frac{1}{N}} = \sqrt[N]{\frac{1}{P(w_1 w_2 \ldots w_N)}}\]

Expanding for bigrams:

\[PP(W) = \sqrt[N]{\prod_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})}}\]

Equivalently, using log probabilities to avoid floating-point underflow:

\[\log PP(W) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \log P(w_i \mid w_{i-1})\]

Interpreting Perplexity

Perplexity is the weighted average branching factor of the language — the number of equally likely words the model must choose among at each step. A perplexity of 100 means the model is "as confused as if it had to choose uniformly among 100 words at each position." Lower perplexity indicates a better model that assigns higher probability to real sentences. Typical values for word-level English language models range from 50 to 200 before modern neural methods.

Perplexity comparisons require identical conditions. Perplexity is only meaningfully comparable between models evaluated on the same test set with the same vocabulary. Never compare perplexity numbers across different datasets, different tokenizations, or different vocabulary sizes — the numbers will not be commensurate.

Text Generation with N-grams

Once we have a language model, we can generate text by sampling. At each step, we look at the recent context and draw the next word from the conditional distribution the model provides.

1
Greedy / Sampled Generation
For a bigram model: given the current word \(w_{i-1}\), sample \(w_i \sim P(\cdot \mid w_{i-1})\). For a trigram model: given \(w_{i-2}, w_{i-1}\), sample \(w_i \sim P(\cdot \mid w_{i-2}, w_{i-1})\). Repeat until reaching an end-of-sentence token or the desired length.
2
The Loop Problem
Simple sampling often produces repetitive text. If "like cats" is frequent and "cats like" is also frequent, the model can enter a loop: "I like cats like cats like cats...". This happens because n-gram models have no long-range memory — they cannot detect that the same phrase has already appeared nearby.
3
Remedies: Temperature and Top-k Sampling
Temperature sampling scales logits before softmax: high temperature flattens the distribution (more random), low temperature sharpens it (more deterministic). Top-k sampling restricts sampling to the k most probable next words, zeroing out the rest. Both techniques extend naturally to neural language models (GPT, etc.) and are used in production systems today.

NLTK Code: Text Generation

Python — N-gram Text Generation 
import random

def generate_text(model, seed_words, n_words=20, n=2):
    """Generate text using an n-gram model."""
    text = list(seed_words)
    for _ in range(n_words):
        # Get (n-1)-gram context
        context = tuple(text[-(n-1):]) if n > 1 else ()
        next_word = model.generate(1, text_seed=list(context))
        if next_word == "</s>":
            break
        text.append(next_word)
    return " ".join(text)

print(result)

Classical NLP Applications Overview

6a. Extractive Summarization

Extractive summarization selects the \(k\) most important sentences from a document and returns them verbatim. No new sentences are generated — the summary is literally a subset of the original text.

Three Approaches to Sentence Scoring

  • TF-IDF sentence scoring: Each sentence receives the average TF-IDF weight of its non-stop-word tokens (links directly back to Module 8). Sentences that concentrate high-TF-IDF terms score higher and are selected.
  • Position heuristic: The first and last sentences of a document tend to be the most informative — introductions state the main point, conclusions restate it. A simple rule extracts these positions first.
  • TextRank: Build a graph where nodes are sentences and edge weights are cosine similarities between sentence TF-IDF vectors. Run the PageRank algorithm on this graph. Sentences that are similar to many other sentences act as "hubs" and receive high scores — analogous to important web pages receiving many links.
Python — Extractive Summarization (spaCy) 
import spacy
from collections import defaultdict

nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")

def extractive_summary(text, n_sentences=2):
    doc = nlp(text)
    sentences = list(doc.sents)

    # Score each sentence by average TF-IDF of its tokens
    word_freq = defaultdict(int)
    for token in doc:
        if not token.is_stop and not token.is_punct:
            word_freq[token.lemma_.lower()] += 1

    scores = {}
    for i, sent in enumerate(sentences):
        score = sum(word_freq[t.lemma_.lower()] for t in sent
                    if not t.is_stop and not t.is_punct)
        scores[i] = score / len(sent)  # Normalize by length

    top_indices = sorted(scores, key=scores.get, reverse=True)[:n_sentences]
    top_indices = sorted(top_indices)  # Preserve original order
    return " ".join(sentences[i].text for i in top_indices)

text = """Natural language processing enables computers to understand text.
Deep learning has transformed many NLP tasks. Language models like GPT learn
from vast text corpora. Summarization extracts the most important sentences."""
print(extractive_summary(text, n_sentences=2))
Output: 
Natural language processing enables computers to understand text. Language models like GPT learn from vast text corpora.

6b. Machine Translation — A Historical Arc

Machine translation has undergone two paradigm shifts in seventy years. Understanding the arc reveals how each era's core ideas connect to the NLP concepts in this track.

1950s–1990s
Rule-Based Machine Translation (RBMT)
Hand-written grammar rules (phrase structure rules from Module 4) combined with bilingual dictionaries translate source sentences step by step: parse source, map to interlingua or transfer structure, generate target. RBMT systems were linguistically principled but brittle — every new language pair required years of expert effort, and the rules never covered colloquial or domain-specific language.
1990s–2015
Statistical Machine Translation (SMT)
Phrase-based SMT learned translation tables from parallel corpora (aligned source–target sentence pairs). The IBM models estimated \(P(\text{source} \mid \text{target})\) — a translation model. The full decoding objective was the noisy channel: \[\hat{T} = \arg\max_T P(\text{source} \mid T) \times P(T)\] where \(P(T)\) is a language model — exactly what this module builds. The language model ensured translations were fluent in the target language, even when the translation model chose unusual word orderings.
2015–present
Neural Machine Translation (NMT)
Encoder–decoder architectures with attention (Bahdanau et al., 2015) replaced phrase tables with learned continuous representations. Seq2Seq models encode the full source sentence into a vector; decoders generate target words one by one, attending to source positions. The 2017 Transformer architecture further replaced recurrence with multi-head self-attention, enabling parallelism and much longer context. State-of-the-art systems (Google Translate, DeepL, MarianMT, Helsinki-NLP) are all Transformer-based. Note: spaCy and NLTK do not perform translation — use MarianMT via HuggingFace or commercial APIs.
Language Models in Statistical MT. The noisy channel framing of statistical MT uses a language model as \(P(\text{target})\) — exactly what you built in this module. Even modern neural systems are trained to maximise \(P(\text{target} \mid \text{source})\) on parallel corpora, which is still a form of language modeling. The n-gram LM you understand now is the direct conceptual ancestor of GPT-style autoregressive language models.

Practice

Problem 1: Unigram Probability

Easy

Given unigram probabilities \(P(I) = 0.2\), \(P(\text{like}) = 0.3\), \(P(\text{cats}) = 0.1\), compute \(P(\text{"I like cats"})\) using the unigram model.

Now consider that in the actual corpus, "I like cats" may appear rarely as a phrase even if each word is common. What does this reveal about the unigram assumption?

Unigram probability:

\[P(\text{"I like cats"}) = P(I) \times P(\text{like}) \times P(\text{cats}) = 0.2 \times 0.3 \times 0.1 = 0.006\]

Limitation: The unigram model treats words as independent — it ignores the fact that "I" is far more likely to be followed by "like" than by "cats". The true probability \(P(\text{"I like cats"})\) computed via the chain rule using actual bigram/trigram counts could be much higher or lower than 0.006 depending on actual co-occurrence patterns. The unigram model completely discards sequential structure.

Problem 2: Laplace-Smoothed Bigram

Medium

Given corpus: "I like cats I like dogs dogs like cats".

Vocabulary: \(\{I, \text{like}, \text{cats}, \text{dogs}\}\), so \(|V| = 4\).

From the corpus: \(C(\text{like}, \text{dogs}) = 1\) and \(C(\text{like}) = 3\).

(a) Compute the MLE bigram probability \(P(\text{dogs} \mid \text{like})\).

(b) Compute the Laplace-smoothed probability \(P_{\text{Laplace}}(\text{dogs} \mid \text{like})\).

(c) Explain why Laplace probability is lower than MLE here, and when Laplace becomes especially useful.

(a) MLE:

\[P(\text{dogs} \mid \text{like}) = \frac{C(\text{like, dogs})}{C(\text{like})} = \frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333\]

(b) Laplace:

\[P_{\text{Laplace}}(\text{dogs} \mid \text{like}) = \frac{C(\text{like, dogs}) + 1}{C(\text{like}) + |V|} = \frac{1 + 1}{3 + 4} = \frac{2}{7} \approx 0.286\]

(c) Explanation: Laplace is lower than MLE for the observed bigram because probability mass has been redistributed to the three other bigrams beginning with "like" that were unseen (like-I, like-like, like-[/s]). Smoothing "steals" from observed counts to give non-zero probability to unseen events. Laplace becomes especially useful for unseen bigrams — MLE gives 0, Laplace gives \(\frac{1}{3+4} = \frac{1}{7} \approx 0.143\) — preventing the zero-probability catastrophe.

Problem 3: Perplexity and Log Probability

Hard

A language model assigns perplexity 64 to a test set of 100 words.

(a) What is the average \(\log_2\) probability per word?

(b) What total probability does the model assign to the entire 100-word test sequence?

(c) Why do NLP practitioners always work with log probabilities rather than raw probabilities?

(a) Average log probability per word:

Perplexity \(PP = 64\). By definition, \(PP = 2^{-\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \log_2 P(w_i \mid \text{context})}\).

\[\log_2(PP) = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_i \log_2 P(w_i \mid \text{context}) \implies \log_2(64) = 6\]

So the average \(\log_2\) probability per word is \(-6\) (the model assigns each word a probability of \(2^{-6} = \frac{1}{64}\) on average).

(b) Total probability of the 100-word sequence:

\[\sum_{i=1}^{100} \log_2 P(w_i \mid \text{context}) = -6 \times 100 = -600\]
\[P(W) = 2^{-600} \approx 4 \times 10^{-181}\]

This is an astronomically small number — far smaller than can be represented by any standard floating-point type (minimum positive float64 is ~\(5 \times 10^{-324}\)).

(c) Why log probabilities? Raw probabilities of sequences underflow to zero in standard 64-bit floating-point arithmetic even for modest sentence lengths (products of hundreds of small fractions). Log probabilities turn products into sums — numerically stable, faster to compute, and easier to compare. All modern NLP systems operate in log space.

Quiz

1. What does the Markov assumption allow in n-gram models?

Correct! The Markov assumption truncates the conditioning history to the last \(k\) words, making the computation tractable. The chain rule computes exact probabilities without this assumption; smoothing handles zero-frequency n-grams; perplexity is computed after the model is built.
Not quite. Option A describes the chain rule (exact, no assumption). Option C describes smoothing. Option D is perplexity evaluation. The Markov assumption specifically limits the context window to the last \(k\) words.

2. Why is lower perplexity better for a language model?

Correct! Perplexity is \(P(W)^{-1/N}\) — as the model assigns higher probability \(P(W)\) to the test sequence, the perplexity decreases. A lower perplexity means the model is less "surprised" by the test data, indicating a better fit to the true distribution of the language.
Not quite. Perplexity is inversely related to the probability the model assigns to the test text — higher model probability means lower perplexity, so lower perplexity signals better (less uncertain) predictions. Training data size and context length do not directly determine perplexity.

3. Laplace (Add-1) smoothing assigns probability to unseen bigrams by:

Correct! Laplace smoothing adds 1 to every bigram count (including unseen ones, which were 0 and become 1) and adds \(|V|\) to the unigram denominator to ensure all probabilities still sum to 1. Option A is MLE without smoothing. Option C is backoff smoothing. Option D is a neural LM.
Not quite. Option A is unsmoothed MLE — the zero problem we're solving. Option C describes backoff smoothing (a different technique). Option D is a neural language model approach. Laplace specifically adds 1 to all counts uniformly.

4. In statistical machine translation, the language model \(P(\text{target})\) serves to:

Correct! In the noisy channel formulation \(\hat{T} = \arg\max P(\text{source} \mid T) \times P(T)\), the translation model \(P(\text{source} \mid T)\) handles the word-level mapping, while the language model \(P(T)\) scores how natural and grammatically well-formed \(T\) is in the target language. Without the LM, translations might be technically accurate word-for-word but ungrammatical.
Not quite. Translation itself is handled by the translation model \(P(\text{source} \mid \text{target})\). BLEU is an evaluation metric computed after translation, not part of the model. OOV handling is a separate vocabulary problem. The language model's role is fluency — it ensures the output sounds natural in the target language.
