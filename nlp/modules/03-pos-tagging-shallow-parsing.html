Why Syntax? From Words to Structure

Normalized tokens from Module 2 still leave meaning underspecified — word order matters. Consider:

The order of words changes everything

"The dog bites the man."

"The man bites the dog."

Both sentences contain exactly the same tokens: the, dog, bites, man. A bag-of-words model assigns them identical representations. Yet they describe radically different events.

Syntax bridges this gap — capturing not just which words appear but how they are arranged and what role each plays. Syntactic analysis comes in two flavours:

Shallow parsing (this module)

Tags each token with a POS, then groups adjacent tokens into flat, non-overlapping phrase chunks. Fast and sufficient for IE and NER.

Deep parsing (Module 4)

Builds a full recursive parse tree (PP inside NP inside VP inside S). More expressive but more complex and domain-sensitive.

Plan: POS tags → statistical taggers → shallow parsing (chunking). Two widgets let you explore tagged sentences and NP chunks interactively.

Parts of Speech

A part of speech (POS), or lexical category, is the grammatical role a word plays in a sentence. Computational linguistics uses finer-grained tagsets than traditional grammar — the key distinction is between open and closed class words:

Open class (content words)

  • Nouns — objects, concepts, entities (dog, democracy, algorithm)
  • Verbs — actions, states (run, believe, tokenize)
  • Adjectives — properties of nouns (quick, neural, ambiguous)
  • Adverbs — modifiers of verbs/adjectives (quickly, very, not)

New words enter these classes constantly.

Closed class (function words)

  • Prepositions — spatial/temporal relations (in, on, after)
  • Determiners — articles and quantifiers (the, a, every)
  • Conjunctions — connectors (and, but, because)
  • Pronouns — noun substitutes (he, they, it)

These classes are fixed. English has had essentially the same prepositions for centuries.

Why ambiguity is hard

The same word can belong to different POS depending on context:

Lexical ambiguity in POS
  • "book" — NN in "Read the book." but VB in "Book the table."
  • "light" — JJ in "a light breeze", NN in "the traffic light", VB in "light the candle."
  • "can" — MD (modal) in "She can swim." but NN in "Open the can."
  • "running" — VBG (predicate) in "She is running." but NOUN (subject) in "Running is healthy."

About 11–12% of word tokens are genuinely ambiguous across major POS categories (Brown Corpus). A most-frequent-tag baseline achieves ~92% accuracy; resolving the remaining 8% requires context — what statistical taggers exploit.

Rule-Based vs. Statistical POS Taggers

Rule-based taggers

Early taggers (e.g., ENGTWOL, 1980s) used hand-crafted rule cascades — e.g., word ends in -ly → RB — to narrow down candidates.

Advantages

  • Interpretable — every decision has a human-readable rule
  • No training data needed
  • Can encode linguistic knowledge precisely

Disadvantages

  • Brittle — rules break on unseen domains
  • Expensive to write and maintain
  • Rules interact in unexpected ways
  • Poor coverage of rare edge cases

HMM taggers — the statistical approach

A Hidden Markov Model (HMM) tagger treats POS tagging as sequence labeling: observed words are the visible sequence, tags are the hidden sequence. It uses two distributions:

1
Emission probability P(word | tag)
How likely is a word given a tag? Estimated by counting: P("dog" | NN) = count(dog, NN) / count(NN).
2
Transition probability P(tagi | tagi−1)
How likely is one tag to follow another? P(VBD | NN) is high ("the dog ran"); P(DT | DT) is near zero ("the the"). Captures grammar implicitly.
3
Viterbi decoding
Brute-force search over tag sequences is exponential; Viterbi finds the optimal sequence in O(n · |T|²) via dynamic programming.

The fundamental formula the HMM maximises is:

P(t_1, \ldots, t_n \mid w_1, \ldots, w_n) \;\propto\; \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(w_i \mid t_i)\, P(t_i \mid t_{i-1})

where t_0 is a special start-of-sentence tag. Viterbi finds the highest-scoring tag assignment efficiently.

Accuracy on standard benchmarks HMM taggers trained on the Penn Treebank achieve around 96–97% per-token accuracy. Modern neural taggers — BiLSTM or transformer-based (as used in spaCy v3) — push this to approximately 97–98% on in-domain text, with better generalization to new domains and languages.

Neural taggers (modern)

A BiLSTM tagger reads the sentence bidirectionally for context-aware token representations. A transformer tagger (e.g., spaCy's en_core_web_trf) uses full-sentence attention. Both learn emission- and transition-like patterns implicitly from data.

POS Tagging in spaCy

spaCy exposes two POS levels per token: coarse universal tag (token.pos_) and fine-grained PTB tag (token.tag_). spacy.explain() returns a human-readable description.

Python — spaCy POS tagging 
import spacy

nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
doc = nlp("The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog.")

for token in doc:
    print(f"{token.text:12} {token.pos_:8} {token.tag_:8} {spacy.explain(token.tag_)}")

Shallow Parsing (Chunking)

Chunking groups consecutive tokens into non-overlapping, non-recursive phrase chunks — a flat sequence of labelled spans, one level above tokens. The three main chunk types are:

  • NP (Noun Phrase) — "the quick brown fox", "cardiovascular health"
  • VP (Verb Phrase) — "jumps over", "has been running"
  • PP (Prepositional Phrase) — "over the lazy dog", "in London"

Chunks are written with bracket notation:

Bracket notation for chunks

[NP The quick brown fox] [VP jumps] [PP over] [NP the lazy dog] .

Chunk grammar — regex over POS tags

The simplest chunker applies regex patterns over POS tag sequences. For NP:

NP: {<DT>?<JJ>*<NN.*>+}

Optional determiner, zero or more adjectives, one or more nouns (.* matches NN/NNS/NNP/NNPS). This single rule correctly identifies most English NPs.

Regex quantifiers in chunk grammars
  • ? — zero or one (optional)
  • * — zero or more (any number of adjectives, including none)
  • + — one or more (at least one noun required)
  • <NN.*> — matches any tag beginning with NN (NN, NNS, NNP, NNPS)

NLTK's RegexpParser applies this directly:

Python — NLTK RegexpParser chunking 
import nltk

grammar = "NP: {<DT>?<JJ>*<NN.*>+}"
cp = nltk.RegexpParser(grammar)

sentence = [
    ("The", "DT"), ("quick", "JJ"), ("brown", "JJ"), ("fox", "NN"),
    ("jumps", "VBZ"), ("over", "IN"),
    ("the", "DT"), ("lazy", "JJ"), ("dog", "NN")
]
result = cp.parse(sentence)
print(result)
(S (NP The/DT quick/JJ brown/JJ fox/NN) jumps/VBZ over/IN (NP the/DT lazy/JJ dog/NN))

Unmatched tokens remain bare (jumps/VBZ, over/IN). Two NP chunks are correctly identified.

import spacy

doc = nlp("The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog in the bright morning sun.")

Practice

Problem 1: POS Tag Identification

Easy

Tag each word in the sentence "The old train left the station quickly." with Penn Treebank POS tags. Choose from: DT, JJ, NN, VBD, RB, IN.

Hint: Think carefully about "old" and "train" — their POS depends on the surrounding context, not just their most common usage in isolation.

Tagged sentence:

The/DT   old/JJ   train/NN   left/VBD   the/DT   station/NN   quickly/RB

Key reasoning:

  • train → NN (not VB): preceded by the adjective "old" and determiner "The", so it is functioning as the sentence subject, a noun phrase head. If it were a verb, you would expect a noun subject before it ("They train..."), not a determiner.
  • old → JJ: modifies the noun "train"; classic adjective position.
  • left → VBD: past tense of "leave"; the main verb of the sentence.
  • quickly → RB: adverb modifying "left"; the -ly suffix is a reliable indicator.

This sentence illustrates why context is essential: "train" is a NN here, even though it is frequently a VB in other contexts (e.g., "I train every day").

Problem 2: Structural Ambiguity and Chunking

Medium

The sentence "I saw the man with the telescope." is structurally ambiguous.

(a) Identify the two possible meanings and the attachment site that creates the ambiguity.

(b) Draw the two possible NP chunk structures for the sentence.

(c) Which structure does a greedy left-to-right chunker using the rule {<DT>?<JJ>*<NN.*>+<IN><DT>?<NN.*>+} most likely produce?

(a) Two meanings:

  • PP attaches to NP: "I saw [the man with the telescope]" — the man who had the telescope. The PP "with the telescope" modifies "man".
  • PP attaches to VP: "I [saw with the telescope] [the man]" — I used the telescope to see. The PP "with the telescope" modifies the verb "saw".

The ambiguity is in PP attachment — whether "with the telescope" attaches to the noun phrase (man) or the verb phrase (saw).

(b) Two chunk structures:

Interpretation 1 (PP to NP):
I  saw  [NP the man with the telescope] .

Interpretation 2 (PP to VP):
I  saw  [NP the man]  [PP with [NP the telescope]] .

(c) Greedy left-to-right chunker: A greedy chunker extending NPs to include following PPs produces Interpretation 1: [NP the man with the telescope]. It greedily extends the NP as far to the right as the rule allows, attaching the PP to the nearest noun — "man" — before moving on. This greedy-attach behaviour is a known limitation of flat chunkers; resolving the ambiguity correctly requires a full parser with knowledge of verb semantics.

Problem 3: Tagging Accuracy and Error Compounding

Hard

A POS tagger achieves 97% per-token accuracy on a standard benchmark.

(a) A sentence has 20 tokens. What is the probability that the entire sentence is tagged correctly (every token correct)? Show your calculation.

(b) Explain why per-sentence accuracy is a more demanding evaluation metric than per-token accuracy, and why this matters for downstream tasks like parsing.

(c) Suggest two concrete techniques to improve tagging accuracy specifically on ambiguous words like "run" and "light".

(a) Sentence accuracy:

Assuming token errors are independent (a simplification — they are actually correlated, but this gives a useful lower bound):

P(\text{all 20 correct}) = 0.97^{20} \approx 0.544

Only about 54.4% of 20-token sentences are fully correctly tagged, even at 97% per-token accuracy. For a 40-token sentence the figure drops to about 30%.

(b) Why sentence accuracy is harder: Per-token accuracy allows many errors in a large corpus and still looks good in aggregate. But a parser, NP chunker, or information extraction system often needs the entire tag sequence to be correct to produce a correct downstream output. One wrong tag can cascade — a noun mistagged as a verb changes the entire phrase structure. Sentence accuracy reflects this "all-or-nothing" property of many downstream applications.

(c) Two techniques for ambiguous words:

  1. Context window features: Include the POS tags and words of surrounding tokens (e.g., ±2 window) as features. "The" before a word strongly suggests NOUN; a subject NP before it also suggests VERB. HMM bigram models already use the preceding tag; log-linear (MaxEnt) taggers can incorporate a wider window.
  2. Lexicalized/neural models that learn word-specific tag distributions: A BiLSTM or transformer reads the entire sentence bidirectionally, building a contextualized representation for each token. This allows it to learn, for instance, that "light" after "traffic" is almost always NOUN, while "light" after "let there be" is almost always VERB — patterns that a simple window of surface features misses.

Quiz

Multiple Choice — 4 Questions

Q1. Which POS tag would spaCy assign to "Running" in the sentence "Running is good for you."?

A VBG — gerund / present participle verb form
B NOUN — gerund acting as the sentence subject
C VB — base form verb
D ADJ — adjective modifying a noun
Correct. In "Running is good for you", "Running" is a gerund acting as the subject of the sentence. spaCy's coarse pos_ tag uses the word's syntactic function: here it is the subject — a NOUN. The fine-grained tag_ will be VBG (its morphological form), but the coarse pos_ reflects the syntactic role.
The answer is B. spaCy distinguishes between a word's morphological form and its syntactic role. VBG describes the form (gerund), but in this sentence "Running" is the subject, which is a noun slot. spaCy assigns NOUN to the coarse pos_ to reflect this functional role.

Q2. The HMM POS tagger relies on two probability distributions. Which are they?

A Prior probability P(tag) and posterior probability P(tag | sentence)
B Emission probability P(word | tag) and transition probability P(tagi | tagi−1)
C Bigram language model probability P(wi | wi−1) and trigram probability P(wi | wi−2, wi−1)
D TF (term frequency) and IDF (inverse document frequency)
Correct. The emission model P(word | tag) encodes how likely each word is for each tag. The transition model P(tagi | tagi−1) encodes how likely one tag is to follow another, implicitly capturing grammar. Together they define the full joint probability the Viterbi algorithm maximises.
The answer is B. HMM taggers use emission probabilities P(word | tag) — how likely a word is given a tag — and transition probabilities P(tagi | tagi−1) — how likely one tag follows another. These are quite different from language model n-grams or TF-IDF weights.

Q3. In the NP chunk grammar {<DT>?<JJ>*<NN.*>+}, what does the * symbol mean?

A Exactly one occurrence of the preceding element
B Zero or more occurrences of the preceding element
C One or more occurrences of the preceding element
D Optional — zero or one occurrence (same as ?)
Correct. In regex-style chunk grammars, * means zero or more repetitions. So <JJ>* matches any number of adjectives before the noun, including none at all — allowing "dog" (zero adjectives) and "quick brown dog" (two adjectives) to both be valid NPs.
The answer is B. In chunk grammars (and regex generally), * means zero or more. Remember: ? = optional (zero or one), * = zero or more, + = one or more. So <JJ>* allows any number of adjectives, including none.

Q4. Shallow parsing (chunking) differs from deep parsing in that shallow parsing:

A Only works on short sentences of fewer than 20 tokens
B Produces non-overlapping, non-recursive phrase chunks rather than a full parse tree
C Requires a probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG)
D Cannot identify noun phrases — only verb phrases
Correct. The defining property of shallow parsing is that it produces a flat sequence of phrase chunks — no recursion, no full tree. A PP cannot be nested inside an NP inside a VP in the shallow parse output. Deep parsing (Module 4) builds the full hierarchical tree with arbitrarily deep nesting.
The answer is B. Shallow parsing works on sentences of any length and can identify NPs. It does not require a PCFG. The key distinction is structural: shallow parsing gives flat chunks with no recursion; deep parsing gives a full hierarchical parse tree.
