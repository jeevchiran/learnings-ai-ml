Why NLP? The Pipeline Idea

Computers see text as bytes with no inherent meaning. Bridging the gap between raw character sequences and meaning is the central problem of Natural Language Processing, solved through a sequence of transformations — a pipeline — each stage building on the last.

The NLP Pipeline

Raw text → Tokenization → Normalization → POS tagging → Parsing → Semantics

Blue stages (Modules 1–2) are lexical processing: they work at the word level and produce clean, normalised tokens. Green stages (Modules 3–4) are syntactic analysis: they assign grammatical structure. Purple stages (Modules 5–10) are semantic: they assign meaning.

Lexical processing is the foundation: every later stage consumes its tokens, so errors here compound downstream ("garbage in, garbage out").

Why lexical processing matters more than it looks A 2018 study on clinical NLP found that 38% of downstream information-extraction errors could be traced back to tokenization and normalization failures — not model limitations, but pipeline foundations. Getting the first stage right pays dividends across every module that follows.
Spell checking

Edit distance — covered in this module's second half — is the algorithmic core of every spell checker.

Search & retrieval

Normalising queries and documents to the same canonical form lets "running", "runs", and "ran" all match a query for "run"; without it, recall drops sharply.

Machine translation

Inconsistent tokenization between training and inference degrades translation quality, especially for rare words and morphologically complex languages.

Sentiment analysis

A classifier trained on lower-cased text will fail on mixed-case inference input. Consistent normalisation at both stages avoids vocabulary mismatch.

Tokenization

A token is the atomic unit the pipeline works with; tokenization is the process of splitting text into tokens. Splitting on spaces sounds trivial, but the subtleties are significant. Tokens come in three granularities:

Word tokens

Most natural; works well for English but poorly for agglutinative languages (Turkish, Finnish) where one word encodes a full English sentence.

Subword tokens

Used by BERT, GPT. "unhappiness" → ["un", "##happi", "##ness"]. Balances vocabulary size with coverage by splitting rare words into known pieces.

Character tokens

Trivially small vocabulary; handles any word including misspellings. Downside: very long sequences make context modelling harder.

Tokenization Strategies

Whitespace tokenization splits on spaces and newlines. It is fast and adequate for clean, space-separated text, but fails on contractions ("don't" → one word or two?), punctuation attached to words ("end." keeps the period), and languages like Chinese that have no spaces between words.

Rule-based tokenization uses hand-crafted regular expressions and dictionaries. The NLTK word_tokenize function, for example, applies Penn Treebank–style rules that separate punctuation from words, handle contractions, and preserve certain abbreviations.

ML-based tokenization trains a sequence-labelling model on annotated data to predict token boundaries. This is the standard for languages without spaces (Chinese, Japanese) and for robustly handling social-media text with non-standard punctuation.

Tokenization edge cases — they matter in practice
  • Contractions: "don't" → ["do", "n't"] or ["don't"]? spaCy splits; NLTK splits differently. The choice affects downstream lemmatization.
  • Hyphenated words: "state-of-the-art" — one token or five? Domain conventions differ (NLP papers often keep the hyphen).
  • URLs and emails: https://example.com/page must be kept as one token, not split on slashes or dots.
  • Numbers: "3.14" and "3,000" look like punctuation-split words to a naive tokenizer.

spaCy handles these edge cases by applying language-specific prefix/suffix/infix rules, then a dictionary of special cases (contractions, abbreviations):

Python — spaCy tokenization 
import spacy

nlp = spacy.load("en_core_web_sm")
doc = nlp("Don't stop me now! I'm having such a good time.")
tokens = [token.text for token in doc]
print(tokens)
['Do', "n't", 'stop', 'me', 'now', '!', 'I', "'m", 'having', 'such', 'a', 'good', 'time', '.']

"Don't" splits at the clitic boundary ["Do", "n't"]; the sentence-final period is its own token — Penn Treebank convention ensuring clean input for downstream POS taggers.

Tokenization gives us a list of strings, but "Apple", "apple", and "APPLE" are still three distinct strings. Normalisation collapses such surface variation into a canonical form.

Text Normalization Steps

Text normalization is a pipeline of small transformations, each targeting a specific type of noise. Order matters: lowercase before punctuation removal; remove stopwords before stemming.

1
Lowercasing
Convert all characters to lowercase: "NLP" → "nlp", "Running" → "running". Collapses case variants into one vocabulary entry — a 100 000-token mixed-case vocabulary might shrink to 60 000.
When lowercasing hurts "US" (United States) and "us" (first-person plural pronoun) become identical. "Apple" (company) and "apple" (fruit) collapse. For named-entity recognition and word-sense disambiguation tasks, case is a crucial disambiguation signal. Modern neural models typically skip lowercasing and let the model learn case-sensitive representations.
2
Punctuation and special character removal
Strip punctuation marks, HTML tags, and non-alphabetic symbols: "Hello, world!" → "Hello world". Eliminates punctuation-attached variants ("end." ≠ "end" to a string matcher). Exception: punctuation is semantic in some domains — ":)" conveys sentiment; medical parentheses enclose qualifiers.
3
Number normalisation
Replace numeric tokens with a placeholder: "The cost was $3,499.99" → "The cost was NUM". Prevents vocabulary bloat from unique number strings. Alternatively normalise to a canonical form ("three thousand" → "3000"). Keep numbers intact for tasks where exact value matters (financial analysis, arithmetic reasoning).
4
Stop word removal
Remove high-frequency function words ("the", "a", "is", "in", "of") that carry little topical information. NLTK provides 179 English stopwords. Reduces feature dimensionality for classification and topic modelling.
Not all tasks benefit from stopword removal Sentiment analysis needs "not", "no", and "never" — all common stopwords. Question answering needs "who", "when", "where". Coreference resolution needs "he", "she", "they". Always consider whether your task is stopword-sensitive before removing them.

Here is a concrete NLTK-based stopword removal pipeline:

Python — NLTK stopword removal 
from nltk.corpus import stopwords
import nltk
nltk.download('stopwords')

stop_words = set(stopwords.words('english'))
tokens = ['the', 'quick', 'brown', 'fox', 'jumps',
          'over', 'the', 'lazy', 'dog']
filtered = [t for t in tokens if t.lower() not in stop_words]
print(filtered)
['quick', 'brown', 'fox', 'jumps', 'lazy', 'dog']

The function words "the" (×2) and "over" are removed; the five remaining content tokens are what a classifier needs to detect the topic.

After normalization, "cats"/"cat" and "running"/"runs"/"ran" are still distinct strings. Module 2 handles that via stemming and lemmatization — but first we need edit distance, the tool underlying spell correction, fuzzy matching, and sequence alignment.

Edit Distance — The Core Idea

Given a misspelling like "accomodation", a spell checker finds the dictionary word requiring the fewest single-character changes to match it. This count is formalised as edit distance, with applications far beyond spell checking:

Spell correction

The noisy channel model (Module 2) ranks candidates by edit distance to the typo combined with language prior probability.

DNA sequence alignment

Smith-Waterman / Needleman-Wunsch algorithms use generalised edit distance to align DNA strands; insertions, deletions, and mutations map directly to the three edit operations.

Fuzzy string matching

Autocomplete and record deduplication rank candidates by edit distance. Python's rapidfuzz library implements this efficiently.

Machine translation evaluation

TER (Translation Edit Rate) counts the word-level edits needed to convert a machine translation into the reference — edit distance at the word level.

The Three Edit Operations (Levenshtein)

The Levenshtein distance between two strings s and t is the minimum number of single-character operations needed to transform s into t. The three allowed operations are:

The Three Edit Operations

OperationCostExample
Insertion1"cat" → "cart" (insert 'r')
Deletion1"cart" → "cat" (delete 'r')
Substitution1"cat" → "cut" (substitute 'a'→'u')

A fourth operation — transposition (swapping adjacent characters) — is not included in basic Levenshtein distance but is added in the Damerau-Levenshtein variant, which handles common keyboard typos like "teh" → "the" in one operation instead of two.

Intuition: "kitten" → "sitting"

Manual walkthrough of "kitten" → "sitting":

1
kitten → sitten
Substitute 'k' → 's' at position 1. Cost: 1. Running total: 1.
2
sitten → sittin
Substitute 'e' → 'i' at position 5. Cost: 1. Running total: 2.
3
sittin → sitting
Insert 'g' at the end. Cost: 1. Running total: 3.

Two substitutions + one insertion = \text{lev}(\text{"kitten"}, \text{"sitting"}) = 3. To prove this is minimum — not just one valid sequence — we need the DP algorithm, which searches all possible operation sequences.

Levenshtein Distance — The Dynamic Programming Solution

Why Not Brute Force?

A naive approach enumerates all operation sequences — roughly O(3^{\max(m,n)}) paths. For length-20 strings that exceeds 3.5 billion paths. The blowup comes from overlapping subproblems: the same prefix-pair alignment is recomputed many times. Dynamic programming stores each subproblem once, reducing complexity to polynomial.

The Recurrence Relation

Define d[i][j] as the edit distance between the first i characters of s and the first j characters of t. The base cases are intuitive: aligning i characters with an empty string requires exactly i deletions:

d[i][0] = i \quad \text{and} \quad d[0][j] = j

For the general case, we consider what the last operation in an optimal alignment might be. There are exactly three possibilities:

Deriving the Recurrence

  1. The last operation is a deletion (we deleted s[i] from s): the cost is d[i-1][j] + 1. We had already aligned s[1..i-1] to t[1..j], then deleted one more character.
  2. The last operation is an insertion (we inserted t[j] into s): the cost is d[i][j-1] + 1. We had aligned s[1..i] to t[1..j-1], then inserted one more character.
  3. The last operation is a substitution or match: the cost is d[i-1][j-1] + \text{cost}(i,j), where \text{cost}(i,j) = 0 if s[i] = t[j] (free match), else 1 (substitution).

We take the minimum of these three possibilities, since we want the cheapest alignment.

Putting it together, the full recurrence is:

d[i][j] = \begin{cases} d[i-1][j-1] & \text{if } s[i] = t[j] \quad \text{(free match)} \\ 1 + \min\!\Big(d[i-1][j],\; d[i][j-1],\; d[i-1][j-1]\Big) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}

The final answer — the edit distance between the full strings — is stored in the bottom-right cell: d[m][n].

Worked Example: "cat" → "cut"

Let's fill the 4×4 matrix for s = \text{"cat"} (rows) and t = \text{"cut"} (columns). Indices run 0 to 3 for rows, 0 to 3 for columns.

Base cases (row 0 and column 0):

d[0][0..3] = [0, 1, 2, 3] \qquad d[0..3][0] = [0, 1, 2, 3]

Filling row 1 (s[1] = \text{'c'}):

  • d[1][1]: s[1]='c' vs t[1]='c' — match, so d[0][0] = 0.
  • d[1][2]: s[1]='c' vs t[2]='u' — mismatch, so 1 + \min(d[0][2], d[1][1], d[0][1]) = 1 + \min(2, 0, 1) = 1.
  • d[1][3]: s[1]='c' vs t[3]='t' — mismatch, so 1 + \min(d[0][3], d[1][2], d[0][2]) = 1 + \min(3, 1, 2) = 2.

Filling row 2 (s[2] = \text{'a'}):

  • d[2][1]: 'a' vs 'c' — mismatch, 1 + \min(d[1][1], d[2][0], d[1][0]) = 1 + \min(0, 2, 1) = 1.
  • d[2][2]: 'a' vs 'u' — mismatch, 1 + \min(d[1][2], d[2][1], d[1][1]) = 1 + \min(1, 1, 0) = 1.
  • d[2][3]: 'a' vs 't' — mismatch, 1 + \min(d[1][3], d[2][2], d[1][2]) = 1 + \min(2, 1, 1) = 2.

Filling row 3 (s[3] = \text{'t'}):

  • d[3][1]: 't' vs 'c' — mismatch, 1 + \min(d[2][1], d[3][0], d[2][0]) = 1 + \min(1, 3, 2) = 2.
  • d[3][2]: 't' vs 'u' — mismatch, 1 + \min(d[2][2], d[3][1], d[2][1]) = 1 + \min(1, 2, 1) = 2.
  • d[3][3]: 't' vs 't' — match, so d[2][2] = 1.

The completed matrix (path cells highlighted below) shows d[3][3] = 1: "cat" → "cut" requires exactly one substitution ('a' → 'u').

Time and space complexity The DP table has (m+1) \times (n+1) cells, each computed in O(1) time. Total time: O(mn). Space: O(mn) for the full table, or O(\min(m,n)) if we only need the final distance (not the backtrace), since we only ever look at the previous row.

Here is the full Python implementation:

Python — Levenshtein distance (DP) 
def levenshtein(s, t):
    m, n = len(s), len(t)
    dp = [[0] * (n + 1) for _ in range(m + 1)]
    for i in range(m + 1): dp[i][0] = i
    for j in range(n + 1): dp[0][j] = j
    for i in range(1, m + 1):
        for j in range(1, n + 1):
            cost = 0 if s[i - 1] == t[j - 1] else 1
            dp[i][j] = min(dp[i-1][j] + 1,      # deletion
                          dp[i][j-1] + 1,      # insertion
                          dp[i-1][j-1] + cost)  # substitution / match
    return dp[m][n]

print(levenshtein("kitten", "sitting"))   # 3
print(levenshtein("saturday", "sunday"))  # 3
3 3

Reading the Matrix and Backtrace

The DP matrix records not just the final edit distance but the full alignment between the two strings. To recover the sequence of operations, we perform a backtrace: starting at the bottom-right corner d[m][n], we follow the path backward to the top-left corner d[0][0], selecting the move that produced each cell's value.

Interpreting Each Cell and Each Move

  • Cell value d[i][j] — the minimum cost to align the first i characters of s with the first j characters of t. It says nothing about what happened after those characters.
  • Diagonal move ([i,j] \leftarrow [i-1,j-1]) — a match (if s[i]=t[j], free) or substitution (cost 1). Both characters are consumed simultaneously.
  • Up move ([i,j] \leftarrow [i-1,j]) — a deletion from s. Character s[i] is removed; no character from t is consumed.
  • Left move ([i,j] \leftarrow [i,j-1]) — an insertion into s. Character t[j] is inserted; no character from s is consumed.

At each cell [i,j], move to whichever predecessor produced the current value. Multiple predecessors with the same minimum are all valid — there may be several optimal alignments. Reading the path top-left to bottom-right gives the full edit script: diagonal = match/substitution, up = deletion, left = insertion.

Problem 1: Fill in the Base Cases

Easy

The Levenshtein DP table for comparing 'ab' (source, rows) against 'abc' (target, columns) has dimensions 3×4 (rows 0–2 for source, columns 0–3 for target).

Fill in the first row (row 0) and first column (column 0) — the base cases. What are the values, and why?

Row 0: [0, 1, 2, 3]

Column 0: [0, 1, 2]

Explanation:

  • d[0][j] = j for all j. This is the cost of converting the empty string (0 characters of source) to the first j characters of the target. The only way to do this is j insertions — one for each target character.
  • d[i][0] = i for all i. This is the cost of converting the first i characters of the source to the empty string. The only way is i deletions — one for each source character.

So row 0 = [0, 1, 2, 3] and column 0 = [0, 1, 2]. These boundary conditions anchor the entire table: every other cell is computed relative to these values.

Problem 3: Weighted Edit Distance

Hard

In OCR (optical character recognition) error correction, some character substitutions are far more likely than others — 'l' and '1' look similar, as do 'O' and '0', 'rn' and 'm'. A uniform substitution cost of 1 treats all confusable pairs equally, which is suboptimal.

Question: Describe how you would modify the Levenshtein recurrence to incorporate a cost function cost(a, b) that returns the substitution cost between characters a and b (where cost(a, a) = 0 and cost(a, b) may differ from cost(b, a) in general). How does this change affect the algorithm's time complexity?

Modification: Replace the binary cost = 0 if s[i-1] == t[j-1] else 1 with the lookup cost = cost_fn(s[i-1], t[j-1]). The recurrence becomes:

d[i][j] = \min\!\Big(d[i-1][j] + 1,\;\; d[i][j-1] + 1,\;\; d[i-1][j-1] + \text{cost}(s[i], t[j])\Big)

Deletion and insertion costs typically remain 1, but they too can be made character-dependent for even more fine-grained control (e.g., deleting a punctuation mark costs less than deleting a letter).

Time complexity: Unchanged at O(mn), assuming cost(a, b) is looked up in an O(1) precomputed table (a 2D array indexed by character code). The O(mn) structure of the DP is entirely determined by the table shape, not the cell computation.

Applications: This generalised weighted edit distance is the foundation of bioinformatics sequence alignment (Needleman-Wunsch substitution matrices like BLOSUM62 assign costs based on evolutionary substitution frequencies) and keyboard-layout-aware spell correction (characters adjacent on the keyboard get lower substitution cost).

Implementation sketch:

Python
def weighted_levenshtein(s, t, cost_fn):
    m, n = len(s), len(t)
    dp = [[0]*(n+1) for _ in range(m+1)]
    for i in range(m+1): dp[i][0] = i
    for j in range(n+1): dp[0][j] = j
    for i in range(1, m+1):
        for j in range(1, n+1):
            dp[i][j] = min(dp[i-1][j] + 1,
                           dp[i][j-1] + 1,
                           dp[i-1][j-1] + cost_fn(s[i-1], t[j-1]))
    return dp[m][n]

1. Which of the following is NOT a standard text normalization step?

A Lowercasing all tokens
B Removing stopwords
C Part-of-speech tagging
D Punctuation removal
POS tagging assigns grammatical roles to tokens — it is syntactic analysis, not normalization. Normalization reduces surface variation to a canonical form; it does not interpret or annotate.
That IS a normalization step. POS tagging is different: it assigns grammatical labels (noun, verb, adjective) to tokens. That is an analysis step that comes after normalization, not part of it.

2. The Levenshtein distance between 'abc' and 'abc' is:

A 0
B 1
C 3
D Undefined — a string cannot be compared to itself
Identical strings require zero edits — every character is a free match. The DP table fills with 0s along the diagonal, and the final cell d[3][3] = 0.
Think about it: the strings are identical, so no operations are needed. Every character aligns with itself at cost 0, and the final distance is 0.

3. Why is dynamic programming used for Levenshtein distance instead of plain recursion?

A DP can handle strings of any length; recursion is limited to short strings
B The naive recursive solution has exponential time complexity due to overlapping subproblems
C DP always uses less memory than recursion
D Recursion cannot represent the insertion operation
The naive recursion recomputes d[i][j] an exponential number of times because many different paths through the string pairs converge on the same sub-alignment. Memoizing (DP) computes each of the O(mn) subproblems exactly once, reducing time from exponential to O(mn).
Recursion can handle any length and all three operations. The problem is efficiency: the naive recursion has overlapping subproblems — d[i][j] is recomputed many times from different call paths, causing exponential runtime. DP fixes this by storing each result once.

4. If you remove the word "the" from a text as a stopword, you potentially lose information about:

A Nothing — "the" carries no semantic content whatsoever
B Definiteness and coreference (e.g., "the president" refers to a specific, contextually known president)
C Verb tense and aspect
D Sentence length only
Removing "the" destroys the definiteness distinction: "a dog" (indefinite, any dog) vs "the dog" (definite, the contextually known dog). This hurts coreference resolution and named-entity tasks where definiteness signals whether a noun phrase refers to a previously introduced entity.
"The" (the definite article) carries real information — it signals that the noun phrase refers to a specific, contextually identified entity. "A president spoke" (any president, vague) differs semantically from "The president spoke" (a specific, known president). This distinction matters for coreference resolution.
