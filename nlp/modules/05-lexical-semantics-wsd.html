Module 5: Lexical Semantics & Word Sense Disambiguation

From Syntax to Meaning

Parse trees capture grammatical structure but are silent about meaning — NP and VP labels say nothing about what concepts or actions are involved. Lexical semantics bridges this gap by formalising how individual words encode meaning and relate to each other.

The word-meaning problem Words are arbitrary symbols; meaning arises from relationships to other words and concepts. Lexical semantics makes these relationships explicit and formal.

This foundation underlies machine translation (mapping senses, not just forms), question answering (which sense of "bank"?), and information extraction (knowing "acquired", "purchased", and "bought" describe the same event type).

Semantic Relations Between Words

Linguists have catalogued a rich taxonomy of ways in which words relate to one another in meaning. Six relations are foundational.

Synonymy

Words with similar meaning: couch / sofa, begin / start. Perfect synonymy is rare — synonyms differ in connotation, register, or collocation.

Antonymy

Opposite meaning, three subtypes: Complementary (alive/dead — no middle ground), Gradable (hot/cold — admit degrees), Converse (buy/sell — two perspectives on one relation).

Hyponymy & Hypernymy

The IS-A hierarchy: "dog" is a hyponym of "animal"; "animal" is a hypernym of "dog". Transitive: poodle → dog → animal → organism → living thing.

Holonymy & Meronymy

The PART-OF relation: finger is a meronym of hand; hand is a holonym of finger. A wheel is part of a car (meronymy), not a kind of car (hyponymy).

Polysemy

One word, multiple related senses: "head" — body part, organisational leader, head of a river — all connected by a core notion of "topmost/primary".

Homonymy

One word form, completely unrelated meanings: "bank" (financial institution, Old French banque) vs. "bank" (river bank, Old Norse bakki) — converged in spelling by historical accident.

Polysemy vs. homonymy in practice The distinction matters for disambiguation. Polysemous senses share conceptual structure, so context clues that resolve one sense often help resolve related ones. Homonyms share nothing — disambiguating "bank" requires completely independent evidence for each sense.

WordNet

WordNet is a lexical database for English (Princeton, 1980s) that organises words into synsets — synonym sets — and encodes semantic relations between them. It is the backbone of knowledge-based NLP systems.

The synset: WordNet's core unit

Each synset represents a single concept and contains:

  • Lemmas: the set of synonymous word forms that express this concept
  • Gloss: a short natural-language definition (the dictionary entry)
  • Example sentences: usage examples
  • POS: noun (n), verb (v), adjective (a), adverb (r)
  • Relations: hypernyms, hyponyms, holonyms, meronyms, antonyms — pointing to other synsets

Example: the synset car.n.01 has lemmas {car, auto, automobile, motorcar} and gloss "a motor vehicle with four wheels; usually propelled by an internal combustion engine".

Naming convention: word.pos.nn where nn is the sense number — bank.n.01 is the financial institution, bank.n.09 is the river bank. Python access via NLTK:

Python — NLTK WordNet Access 
from nltk.corpus import wordnet as wn

synsets = wn.synsets('bank')
for syn in synsets:
    print(f"Synset: {syn.name()}")
    print(f"  Definition: {syn.definition()}")
    print(f"  Examples: {syn.examples()[:1]}")
    print(f"  Hypernyms: {[h.name() for h in syn.hypernyms()]}")
Synset: bank.n.01 Definition: a financial institution that accepts deposits and channels the money into lending activities Examples: ['he cashed a check at the bank'] Hypernyms: ['depository_financial_institution.n.01'] Synset: bank.n.09 Definition: sloping land (especially the slope beside a body of water) Examples: ['they sat on the bank of the river'] Hypernyms: ['slope.n.01']

Word Sense Disambiguation

Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD) is the task of determining, given a word in context, which of its dictionary senses is intended. Humans do it unconsciously, but it is one of NLP's hardest problems.

WSD is AI-complete Solving WSD perfectly requires full world knowledge and common-sense reasoning. "He saw her duck" — bird or action? — can only be resolved with physical context. No finite rule set covers all cases.

Wrong sense selection cascades through downstream systems:

  • Machine translation: Spanish "banco" can mean "bank" (financial) or "bench". Wrong sense → wrong translation.
  • Question answering: "What does the bank regulate?" depends entirely on which sense of "bank" is active.
  • Information extraction: Extracting acquisition events requires knowing that "plant" means a manufacturing facility, not vegetation.

Knowledge-Based WSD: The Lesk Algorithm

Knowledge-based methods exploit lexical resources (primarily WordNet) without annotated training data. The simplest and most influential is the Lesk algorithm (Lesk, 1986).

1
Extract context words
Take the surrounding words within a window (typically the sentence). Remove stopwords (the, a, to, …) and punctuation. What remains is the context set.
2
Retrieve candidate synsets
Look up the target word in WordNet. Each synset it belongs to is a candidate sense. For "bank" (noun), there are 10 candidate synsets.
3
Compute overlap
For each candidate synset, count how many words in its gloss (definition) also appear in the context set. This is the overlap score.
4
Select argmax
Choose the synset with the highest overlap score. In the event of a tie, apply a fallback (e.g., most frequent sense).

Example. Sentence: "He went to the bank to deposit his salary." Target word: "bank".

SynsetGloss (excerpt)Overlap with {went, deposit, salary}
bank.n.01 a financial institution that accepts deposits 1 (deposit)
bank.n.09 sloping land beside a body of water 0
bank.n.03 a supply or stock held in reserve especially for future use 0

The algorithm correctly selects bank.n.01.

Lesk's limitations Glosses are short (10–30 words), so most comparisons yield 0 overlap — results are sensitive to gloss quality and stopword list. Extended Lesk also includes definitions of context words, dramatically increasing overlap surface area.

Supervised WSD

Supervised methods train a per-word classifier on human-annotated instances (SenseVal/SemEval benchmarks).

Supervised WSD pipeline

  1. Data: Annotated corpus where every instance of the target word is labeled with its correct synset (e.g., 200 annotated sentences containing "bank").
  2. Features: Bag-of-words window (±5 words), POS tags of surrounding words, local collocations (the word immediately left/right), syntactic relations.
  3. Classifier: SVM, Naive Bayes, or neural network trained per word.
  4. Inference: For a new instance, extract features and predict the sense label.

Supervised WSD achieves the highest accuracy — typically 80–90% on standard benchmarks — but requires expensive labeled data for every ambiguous word, and doesn't generalise to new words or new senses.

Knowledge-Based Methods — Deeper: WordNet Similarity

WordNet's hierarchy also enables quantitative semantic similarity: synsets close in the hierarchy are semantically similar. Two measures formalise this.

Path Similarity

Shortest path between two synsets in the hypernym graph:

\text{path\_sim}(c_1, c_2) = \frac{1}{\text{shortest\_path\_length}(c_1, c_2) + 1}

Score 1.0 at distance 0; siblings via a shared parent score 0.5; distant concepts approach 0.

Wu-Palmer Similarity

Path length ignores ancestor depth — sharing "carnivore" implies more similarity than sharing "entity". Wu-Palmer (1994) captures this:

\text{wup}(c_1, c_2) = \frac{2 \cdot \text{depth}(\text{LCS}(c_1, c_2))}{\text{depth}(c_1) + \text{depth}(c_2)}

where LCS is the Lowest Common Subsumer — the most specific shared ancestor. Higher LCS depth = higher similarity score.

Example: dog, cat, car

  • dog.n.01 and cat.n.01 share LCS carnivore.n.01 at depth 8. Both are at depth 9. wup = 2×8/(9+9) ≈ 0.857.
  • dog.n.01 and car.n.01 share LCS entity.n.01 at depth 1. wup = 2×1/(9+7) ≈ 0.125. Much lower — as expected.
  • Path similarity: dog-cat ≈ 0.200 (5 hops apart); dog-car ≈ 0.077 (12 hops apart).
Python — WordNet Similarity (NLTK) 
from nltk.corpus import wordnet as wn

dog = wn.synset('dog.n.01')
cat = wn.synset('cat.n.01')
car = wn.synset('car.n.01')

print(f"dog-cat path similarity: {dog.path_similarity(cat):.3f}")
print(f"dog-cat wup  similarity: {dog.wup_similarity(cat):.3f}")
print(f"dog-car path similarity: {dog.path_similarity(car):.3f}")
print(f"dog-car wup  similarity: {dog.wup_similarity(car):.3f}")
dog-cat path similarity: 0.200 dog-cat wup similarity: 0.857 dog-car path similarity: 0.077 dog-car wup similarity: 0.571

from nltk.wsd import lesk
from nltk.tokenize import word_tokenize

sentence = word_tokenize("The bank can guarantee deposits will cover future tuition.")

Lesk is not always right Despite "deposits" being a strong cue for the financial sense, NLTK's Lesk selects savings_bank.n.02 (piggy bank) — sparse overlap lets gloss accidents override strong context cues. Real WSD systems augment Lesk or use supervised methods.

Problem 1: Semantic Relations

Easy

Classify each word pair as: synonymy, antonymy, hyponymy, hypernymy, or meronymy.

  1. car / vehicle
  2. happy / sad
  3. petal / flower
  4. begin / commence
  5. oak / tree
  1. car / vehicle — car IS-A vehicle, so "car" is a hyponym of "vehicle" (equivalently, "vehicle" is a hypernym of "car").
  2. happy / sad — Antonymy (gradable: there are intermediate states like "neutral" or "content").
  3. petal / flower — petal is PART-OF flower → meronymy (petal is a meronym; flower is the holonym).
  4. begin / commence — Synonymy. Both mean to start; "commence" carries a slightly more formal register.
  5. oak / tree — oak IS-A tree → hyponymy (oak is a hyponym of tree).

Problem 2: Apply Lesk Manually

Medium

Apply the Lesk algorithm to disambiguate "bass" in:

"He played a deep bass note on the piano."

Candidate synsets:

  • bass.n.01 — "the lowest part of the musical range"
  • bass.n.07 — "any of various freshwater fish typically sought by anglers"

Context words (after stopword removal): {played, deep, note, piano}

Which sense is selected? Compute overlap scores for both synsets.

Overlap computation (basic Lesk):

  • bass.n.01 gloss words: {lowest, part, musical, range} — Direct overlap with context {played, deep, note, piano}: 0 (no exact word matches).
  • bass.n.07 gloss words: {various, freshwater, fish, typically, sought, anglers} — Direct overlap: 0.

Both score 0 in basic Lesk — a common failure mode due to sparse overlap. Under Extended Lesk, we also include the definitions of context words:

  • "note" → "a notation representing the pitch and duration of a musical sound" → adds {musical, sound, pitch}
  • "piano" → "a keyboard instrument with a wooden case enclosing a soundboard and metal strings, which are struck by hammers" → adds {instrument, strings, keyboard}

Expanded context now includes: {played, deep, note, piano, musical, sound, pitch, instrument, strings, keyboard, ...}

Now bass.n.01 gloss "the lowest part of the musical range" → overlap = 1 (musical).

bass.n.07 (freshwater fish) → still 0.

Selected sense: bass.n.01 (the musical sense). The extended Lesk correctly disambiguates what basic Lesk cannot.

Problem 3: WSD Evaluation and the MFS Baseline

Hard

A WSD system is evaluated on SemEval data for the word "run" (50 test instances). Results: 35 correct, 15 wrong.

  1. Compute the accuracy of the WSD system. (In this closed-world single-label classification, precision and recall both equal accuracy.)
  2. The Most Frequent Sense (MFS) baseline assigns all instances to "run" as a verb of motion, which is correct for 28 of the 50 instances. Is our WSD system better than the baseline?
  3. Why is the MFS baseline surprisingly hard to beat?

(a) Accuracy:

\text{Accuracy} = \frac{35}{50} = 0.70 = 70\%

(b) Comparison with MFS baseline: MFS accuracy = 28/50 = 56%. Our system (70%) beats the baseline by 14 points.

(c) Why is MFS hard to beat? Sense distributions follow Zipf's law — the dominant sense may cover 50–60% of uses, giving MFS a strong free lunch. Improvements on minority senses contribute little to overall accuracy, and gains there often hurt the dominant-sense cases. WSD systems are therefore often evaluated per-sense to disentangle majority-class dominance from genuine disambiguation skill.

MCQ — Lexical Semantics & WSD

Q1. What is a synset in WordNet?

A A single dictionary entry for one word
B A set of synonymous word forms sharing a single semantic concept
C A hierarchical tree of hypernyms
D A list of antonyms for a given word
A synset is a group of words (lemmas) that are interchangeable in some contexts — they express the same concept, share a definition (gloss), and are linked by relations to other synsets.
A synset is not a single word entry but a set of synonymous words representing one concept, with a shared definition and relationships to other synsets.

Q2. Polysemy differs from homonymy in that:

A Polysemy involves unrelated meanings with different etymological origins
B Polysemous senses are related extensions of a single original meaning
C Homonymy only applies to verbs
D Polysemy is easier to disambiguate than homonymy
Polysemy: "head" — body part → head of organisation → head of river (all connected by a core notion of "topmost/primary"). Homonymy: "bank" financial vs. river bank — etymologically unrelated, converged by historical accident.
It's the opposite: homonymy = unrelated etymological origins. Polysemy = related senses all derived from one original meaning. Neither is consistently easier to disambiguate.

Q3. The Lesk WSD algorithm works by:

A Training a neural network on labeled sense examples
B Measuring the overlap between a word's synset glosses and the surrounding context words
C Always selecting the most frequent sense in a corpus
D Using the parse tree to resolve prepositional phrase attachment
Lesk is knowledge-based: it compares the definitions (glosses) of candidate synsets against context words and picks the synset with maximum overlap. No training required — it uses WordNet directly.
Lesk is knowledge-based, not learning-based. It uses dictionary definitions (glosses) compared to context — no training data, no parse tree. Option C describes the MFS baseline, not Lesk.

Q4. Which WordNet similarity measure uses the depth of the Lowest Common Subsumer (LCS)?

A Path similarity
B Wu-Palmer similarity
C Lesk similarity
D Cosine similarity
Wu-Palmer similarity = 2 × depth(LCS) / (depth(c1) + depth(c2)). It rewards shared deep ancestors — a dog and cat sharing "carnivore" as LCS scores higher than two concepts sharing only "entity".
Wu-Palmer uses depth(LCS). Path similarity uses 1/(shortest_path_length + 1) — no depth required. Lesk uses gloss overlap (not path-based at all). Cosine similarity belongs to distributional semantics (Module 6).
