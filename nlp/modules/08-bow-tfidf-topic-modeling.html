Module 8: Text Representation — Bag of Words, TF-IDF & Topic Modeling

From Text to Numbers

ML models need fixed-length numeric vectors, but text is variable-length and symbolic. This module covers three approaches that bridge that gap:

Bag of Words (BoW)

Count how many times each vocabulary word appears in a document. Simple, sparse, and surprisingly powerful — ignores word order entirely.

TF-IDF

Reweight word counts so that common words ("the", "is") are downweighted and rare, distinctive words are upweighted. The workhorse of classical information retrieval.

Topic Modeling (NMF)

Decompose the document-term matrix into latent topics. Each document is represented as a mixture of discovered themes — no labels required.

Module 7 → Module 8. NLP gives us annotations; these three approaches turn words into numbers a machine can learn from.

Bag of Words

The Bag of Words (BoW) model represents a document as a vector of word counts, ignoring word order and grammar.

Step-by-Step Construction

1
Build the vocabulary V
Collect all unique words across all documents in the corpus (after preprocessing: lowercasing, stopword removal, stemming/lemmatisation). Every unique word becomes a column in the document-term matrix. If your corpus has 20,000 unique words, every document gets a vector of length 20,000.
2
Represent each document as a count vector
For document d, create a vector of length |V|. Entry i is the number of times vocabulary word i appears in document d. If word i does not appear in d, the entry is 0.
3
Stack into a document-term matrix
With N documents and |V| vocabulary words, the result is an N × |V| matrix. Each row is a document, each column is a word. This matrix is the input to most classical ML models.

Properties and Limitations

Sparse

In a 20,000-word corpus, any document uses only a few hundred. Most vector entries are 0 — efficient to store, but distance metrics behave poorly in high-dimensional sparse spaces.

Order-independent

"Dog bites man" and "man bites dog" are identical under BoW — all syntactic and word-order information is lost.

Dominated by frequent words

"The" appears in every document and dominates count vectors without correction. TF-IDF (Section 3) fixes this.

Semantic blindness

"Car" and "automobile" are unrelated features in BoW despite being synonyms. Word embeddings (Module 9) resolve this.

Despite these limitations, BoW pairs naturally with Naive Bayes — both assume word independence — making the combination effective for text classification and spam filtering.

from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import CountVectorizer

docs = [

    "the dog chased the cat",

]

TF-IDF — The Weighting Solution

TF-IDF (Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency) down-weights words that appear everywhere ("the") and up-weights words that characterise specific documents ("quantum").

Term Frequency (TF)

TF measures how often a term appears within a single document, normalised by document length:

\text{TF}(t, d) = \frac{\text{count}(t, d)}{|d|}

where \text{count}(t, d) is the number of times term t appears in document d, and |d| is the total number of tokens in d.

Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

IDF measures how rare a term is across the entire corpus:

\text{IDF}(t) = \log\!\left(\frac{N}{\text{df}(t)}\right)

where N = total documents, \text{df}(t) = documents containing t. If a term appears in all documents, \text{IDF} = \log(1) = 0.

TF-IDF Score

\text{TF-IDF}(t, d) = \text{TF}(t, d) \times \text{IDF}(t)

A high TF-IDF score means the term appears frequently in this document but rarely in others.

Sklearn Smoothed IDF (Default)

Scikit-learn avoids division-by-zero with a smoothed variant that also prevents any weight from being exactly zero:

\text{IDF}_{\text{smooth}}(t) = \log\!\left(\frac{1 + N}{1 + \text{df}(t)}\right) + 1

This is the formula used in the live widget below.

Intuitive Examples

"the" in any document

High TF + Low IDF (df ≈ N) → Low TF-IDF. Stopword suppression emerges automatically.

"quantum" in a physics paper

Medium TF + High IDF (rare in corpus) → High TF-IDF. Strong document signal.

"neural" in a deep learning paper

High TF + Medium IDF (common in ML corpus) → Medium TF-IDF. Useful but not uniquely identifying.

"LSTM" in one specific paper

Low TF + Very High IDF → High TF-IDF. Even a single occurrence of a very rare term gets strong weight.

TF-IDF surfaces document-characterising words and suppresses common-word noise — without a handcrafted stopword list.

Cosine Similarity Between Documents

Cosine similarity ranges from 0 (orthogonal — no shared terms) to 1 (identical TF-IDF profiles). A pair with sim > 0.5 likely shares a topic.

Document Similarity with TF-IDF

A search query is treated as a short document and embedded in the same TF-IDF space. The engine returns documents with the highest cosine similarity to the query — the basis of classical IR before deep learning. Brute-force scan is O(N \cdot |V|); inverted indices make it faster.

Cosine Similarity

For two TF-IDF vectors \mathbf{u} and \mathbf{v}:

\text{cos}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \, \|\mathbf{v}\|}

Cosine similarity ignores vector magnitude — a long document and a short document on the same topic score high. This is why TF (normalized by document length) is preferable to raw counts for retrieval.

BM25 — Beyond TF-IDF

BM25 (Best Match 25) adds TF saturation (doubling term frequency doesn't double relevance) and explicit document-length normalisation. It is the default scorer in Elasticsearch and Solr, and the standard improvement over vanilla TF-IDF in production retrieval.

Topic Modeling with NMF

Topic modeling discovers latent themes in an unlabelled corpus by compressing a document's 20,000-term TF-IDF vector into a short vector over K topics, each defined by its top-weighted terms.

Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF)

NMF decomposes the document-term matrix V into two non-negative factor matrices:

V \approx W \times H \quad \text{where} \quad W, H \geq 0

The Three Matrices

MatrixShapeMeaning
Vn_docs × n_termsOriginal TF-IDF matrix — one row per document, one column per vocabulary term
Wn_docs × n_topicsDocument-topic matrix — how strongly each document belongs to each topic
Hn_topics × n_termsTopic-term matrix — each row is a topic; terms with large weights define the topic

NMF minimises reconstruction error subject to non-negativity:

\min_{W, H \geq 0} \;\|V - WH\|_F^2

where \|\cdot\|_F is the Frobenius norm. The optimisation alternates between updating W and H via a multiplicative update rule that preserves non-negativity.

Interpreting Topics

The top-k terms by weight in each row of H label the topic. For a tech corpus with 3 topics:

Topic 0 — "Machine Learning"

data, learning, model, training, accuracy

Topic 1 — "Deep Learning"

network, layer, neural, deep, activation

Topic 2 — "NLP"

text, language, word, sentence, corpus

Each row of W gives a document's topic weights. NMF supports mixed membership: a document about neural machine translation can have high weights for both Topic 1 and Topic 2.

NMF vs. LDA

LDA (Latent Dirichlet Allocation) is the other widely used topic model. Both discover K topics from unlabelled text:

NMF vs. LDA at a Glance

PropertyNMFLDA
FrameworkOptimisation (matrix factorisation)Probabilistic / Bayesian generative model
Topic weightsNon-negative reals (don't sum to 1)Probability distributions (sum to 1 per document)
Mixed membershipYes — weighted combination of topicsYes — Dirichlet prior over topic proportions
Statistical groundingNone — purely a factorisationFull generative story: topic → word distribution
Topic crispnessTends to produce crisp, coherent topicsTopics can be broader; controlled by hyperparameters
Practical useFast, simpler to tune, works well with TF-IDF inputPreferred when probability outputs are needed downstream
Python — NMF Topic Modeling 
from sklearn.decomposition import NMF
from sklearn.feature_extraction.text import TfidfVectorizer

corpus = [
    "machine learning models need data",
    "deep learning neural networks layers",
    "natural language processing text",
    "supervised learning classification regression",
    "recurrent neural networks LSTM sequences",
    "word embeddings semantic similarity"
]

tfidf = TfidfVectorizer(max_features=20, stop_words='english')
V = tfidf.fit_transform(corpus)

nmf = NMF(n_components=3, random_state=42)
W = nmf.fit_transform(V)   # document-topic matrix: (6 x 3)
H = nmf.components_         # topic-term matrix:     (3 x 20)

terms = tfidf.get_feature_names_out()
for i, topic in enumerate(H):
    top_terms = [terms[j] for j in topic.argsort()[-5:][::-1]]
    print(f"Topic {i}: {', '.join(top_terms)}")

# Topic 0: learning, neural, deep, networks, layers
# Topic 1: language, natural, processing, word, text
# Topic 2: machine, data, models, classification, supervised

Problem 2: TF-IDF Calculation

Medium

A corpus has N = 5 documents. The term "neural" appears in 2 of them (\text{df} = 2). In document D3, "neural" appears 4 times, and D3 has 100 tokens total.

(a) Compute \text{TF}(\text{neural}, D3).

(b) Compute \text{IDF}(\text{neural}) using both the standard formula \log(N/\text{df}) and the sklearn smoothed formula \log\!\left(\frac{1+N}{1+\text{df}}\right)+1.

(c) Compute \text{TF-IDF}(\text{neural}, D3) using both IDF formulas.

(a) Term Frequency:

\text{TF} = \frac{4}{100} = 0.04

(b) Inverse Document Frequency:

\text{IDF}_{\text{standard}} = \log\!\left(\frac{5}{2}\right) = \log(2.5) \approx 0.916
\text{IDF}_{\text{sklearn}} = \log\!\left(\frac{1+5}{1+2}\right) + 1 = \log(2) + 1 \approx 0.693 + 1 = 1.693

(c) TF-IDF scores:

\text{TF-IDF}_{\text{standard}} = 0.04 \times 0.916 = 0.0366
\text{TF-IDF}_{\text{sklearn}} = 0.04 \times 1.693 = 0.0677

The sklearn formula's +1 prevents any IDF from being zero. Always verify which IDF variant a library uses when comparing scores.

Problem 3: NMF vs. LDA Trade-offs

Hard

You are building a topic model for 50,000 news articles. Compare NMF and LDA on the following criteria: (a) interpretability of topics, (b) ability to assign a document to multiple topics (mixed membership), (c) runtime complexity, (d) statistical grounding. Then give a concrete recommendation: which would you use for a news recommender system that must tag articles with topic probabilities?

(a) Interpretability: Both yield human-readable topics as top-N terms. NMF topics tend to be "crisper" because the hard non-negativity constraint prevents cancellation between components. LDA topics can blend more smoothly but may be less focused without careful hyperparameter tuning.

(b) Mixed membership: Both support mixed membership. LDA gives proper probability distributions over topics per document (rows of the document-topic matrix sum to 1, by the Dirichlet prior). NMF gives non-negative weights that do not sum to 1, making comparison across documents harder.

(c) Runtime: Both scale roughly as O(n_\text{docs} \times n_\text{terms} \times n_\text{topics} \times n_\text{iter}). In practice, with sklearn on 50,000 articles and 20 topics, NMF is often faster due to simpler update rules. LDA with variational EM is competitive but can require more iterations.

(d) Statistical grounding: LDA has a generative probabilistic story (Bayesian model with Dirichlet priors). This means you can compute the probability of a new document under the model, do posterior inference, and extend the model formally. NMF is a pure optimisation problem with no probabilistic interpretation — you cannot compute likelihoods or do Bayesian inference.

Recommendation: Use LDA for the news recommender. Recommender systems need to score articles with normalised topic probabilities that integrate naturally with downstream ranking formulas (e.g., compute the probability overlap between a user's interest profile and a new article's topic distribution). LDA's Dirichlet output directly supports this. Choose NMF if engineering simplicity or faster iteration speed is the priority, or if you plan to combine topics with other non-probabilistic features in a linear model.
