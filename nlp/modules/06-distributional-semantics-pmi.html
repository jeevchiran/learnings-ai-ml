Module 6: Distributional Semantics & Pointwise Mutual Information

The Distributional Hypothesis

Bridge from Module 5. WordNet gave us a hand-crafted lexical database — rich, precise, and built by human experts over decades. But scaling to new languages, domains, or senses requires enormous labour. Can we instead learn meaning automatically from raw text, with no manual annotation at all?

The answer comes from a simple but powerful observation made by the British linguist J.R. Firth in 1957:

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps."
— J.R. Firth, A Synopsis of Linguistic Theory (1957)

This is the distributional hypothesis: words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings. It shifts the question from "what does a word mean?" (hard, requires human judgment) to "what company does a word keep?" (easy, just count co-occurrences in a corpus).

Intuition: 'dog' and 'cat'

Consider the words dog and cat. They both appear frequently near words like pet, feed, vet, furry, loyal, and bark/meow. They almost never appear near photosynthesis or transistor. Because their context distributions are so similar, the distributional hypothesis predicts — correctly — that they are semantically related.

By contrast, dog and democracy share almost no context words, predicting — again correctly — that they occupy very different regions of semantic space.

This idea is the foundation of almost all modern word representations. Word2Vec (Module 9), GloVe, FastText, and even the input layers of large language models all exploit the distributional hypothesis in one form or another. Understanding PMI and co-occurrence matrices is the prerequisite for understanding why those methods work.

No labeling required

The entire framework is unsupervised. Any raw text corpus — Wikipedia, a book, tweets — is sufficient input. There are no annotation costs.

Language-agnostic

The same algorithm works identically for English, Swahili, or ancient Greek. No language-specific rules or resources are needed.

Domain-adaptable

Train on medical literature and "discharge" means what a hospital means; train on electrical engineering texts and it means something different. The context determines the meaning.

Scalable

More data almost always helps. A trillion-word corpus gives richer co-occurrence statistics than a million-word one, producing higher-quality semantic representations.

Co-occurrence Matrices

The first concrete step toward distributional semantics is counting how often words appear together. The result is a co-occurrence matrix: rows are target words, columns are context words, and each cell holds a count of how many times the target and context appeared in each other's vicinity.

Window-Based Co-occurrence

Given a sentence and a window size k, every pair of words within distance k (positions i-k to i+k, excluding position i itself) is counted as a co-occurrence. Common choices are k = 2 (tight syntactic context) or k = 5 (broader topical context).

Building a Co-occurrence Matrix: Step by Step

Consider this small four-sentence corpus with window size k = 2:

"I like dogs and cats"
"Dogs are loyal pets"
"Cats are independent pets"
"I prefer dogs over cats"

Target vocabulary (alphabetical): and, are, cats, dogs, i, independent, like, loyal, over, pets, prefer

For sentence 1, "I like dogs and cats", with k=2:

  • dogs co-occurs with: i (dist 2), like (dist 1), and (dist 1), cats (dist 2)
  • cats co-occurs with: like (dist 2 — wait, dist 3 — outside window), dogs (dist 2), and (dist 1)

Repeat for all sentences, accumulate counts. A subset of the resulting matrix (showing key words):

Target \ Context dogs cats pets loyal independent i are
dogs 0 3 2 1 0 2 1
cats 3 0 2 0 1 1 1
pets 2 2 0 1 1 0 2
loyal 1 0 1 0 0 0 1

Notice that dogs and cats have similar row vectors — they co-occur with mostly the same words. This is the distributional hypothesis made numerical.

Problem: Raw counts are dominated by function words. Words like "the", "is", "and" appear in virtually every sentence. The co-occurrence count for "dog–the" can be enormous, even though that pairing carries almost no semantic information. Raw counts therefore misrepresent what words are truly "about". This motivates statistical weighting — specifically PMI.

Python: Building the Co-occurrence Matrix

import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

def build_cooccurrence(sentences, window=2):
    # Build vocabulary
    all_words = [w for sent in sentences for w in sent]
    vocab = sorted(set(all_words))
    word2idx = {w: i for i, w in enumerate(vocab)}

    # Count co-occurrences
    cooc = np.zeros((len(vocab), len(vocab)))
    for sent in sentences:
        for i, w in enumerate(sent):
            for j in range(max(0, i - window), min(len(sent), i + window + 1)):
                if i != j:
                    cooc[word2idx[w], word2idx[sent[j]]] += 1
    return cooc, vocab, word2idx

sentences = [
    ["i", "like", "dogs", "and", "cats"],
    ["dogs", "are", "loyal", "pets"],
    ["cats", "are", "independent", "pets"],
    ["i", "prefer", "dogs", "over", "cats"]
]
cooc, vocab, w2i = build_cooccurrence(sentences)
print(vocab)
# ['and', 'are', 'cats', 'dogs', 'i', 'independent', 'like', 'loyal', 'over', 'pets', 'prefer']

Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI)

Pointwise Mutual Information answers the question: how much more often do two words co-occur than we would expect if they were completely independent? If "dog" and "pet" appear together far more than chance predicts, they must be meaningfully related.

The PMI Formula

1
Define joint and marginal probabilities
From the co-occurrence matrix, estimate:
P(x, y) = \frac{\text{count}(x, y)}{N} \qquad P(x) = \frac{\text{count}(x)}{N} \qquad P(y) = \frac{\text{count}(y)}{N}
where N is the total number of co-occurrence tokens (sum of all cells).
2
Compute PMI
\text{PMI}(x, y) = \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x) \cdot P(y)}
If x and y are independent, P(x,y) = P(x)P(y) and PMI = 0. Positive PMI means they attract; negative means they repel.
3
Clip to PPMI
\text{PPMI}(x, y) = \max\bigl(0,\; \text{PMI}(x, y)\bigr)
Negative PMI values are unreliable for rare pairs (where P(x,y) \approx 0 gives PMI \to -\infty). Clipping at 0 — Positive PMI — discards unreliable negatives.

Interpreting the PMI Score

PMI > 0 — Attraction

The pair co-occurs more than chance predicts. Strong semantic association. Example: PMI("ice", "cream") is large and positive — they overwhelmingly appear together.

PMI = 0 — Independence

The pair co-occurs exactly as often as chance predicts. No detectable semantic relationship. Rare in practice; most word pairs are slightly positively or negatively associated.

PMI < 0 — Repulsion

The pair co-occurs less than chance predicts. Problematic for sparse data: even a single zero count drives PMI to -\infty. PPMI clips these to 0.

Key insight: PMI vs. raw counts. Consider "ice" and "the": they co-occur enormously often in any large corpus — simply because "the" appears everywhere. But their PMI is close to 0 or negative, because "the" is so frequent that it appears near every word by chance. PMI("ice", "cream"), by contrast, is large because this pairing exceeds what two individually-frequent words would produce by chance.

Python: Computing PPMI

def compute_ppmi(cooc):
    total = cooc.sum()
    row_sums = cooc.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
    col_sums = cooc.sum(axis=0, keepdims=True)

    # P(x,y), P(x), P(y)
    pxy = cooc / total
    px  = row_sums / total
    py  = col_sums / total

    # PMI = log2(P(x,y) / P(x)*P(y)); clip negatives to 0 for PPMI
    with np.errstate(divide='ignore', invalid='ignore'):
        pmi = np.log2(pxy / (px * py))
    pmi  = np.nan_to_num(pmi, nan=0.0, neginf=0.0)
    ppmi = np.maximum(0, pmi)   # PPMI
    return ppmi

ppmi = compute_ppmi(cooc)
# ppmi[w2i['dogs'], w2i['loyal']] will be positive
# ppmi[w2i['dogs'], w2i['independent']] will be 0.0 (no co-occurrence)

Building and Interpreting the PMI Matrix

With PPMI vectors in hand, each word is now represented as a high-dimensional sparse vector. Words that appear in similar contexts have similar vectors. Semantic similarity can be measured directly with cosine similarity.

Cosine Similarity

For two word vectors \mathbf{u} and \mathbf{v}:

\text{cosine\_sim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{v}\|} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i u_i v_i}{\sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i u_i^2} \cdot \sqrt{\displaystyle\sum_i v_i^2}}

Cosine similarity ranges from -1 (opposite) to +1 (identical direction). For PPMI vectors (all non-negative), the range is [0, 1]. It is preferred over Euclidean distance because it is invariant to vector magnitude — a word appearing twice as often in a corpus gets a longer PPMI vector but should not be considered semantically different from its shorter counterpart.

Finding the Most Similar Word

Given the PPMI matrix M, the most semantically similar word to "dogs" is:

\text{similar\_to}(\text{"dogs"}) = \underset{w \neq \text{"dogs"}}{\arg\max}\; \text{cosine\_sim}(M_{\text{dogs}},\, M_w)

On the small four-sentence corpus above, this reliably returns "cats" — because dogs and cats share the same high-PMI context words (pets, loyal/independent patterns, appearing near "i" and "prefer").

Limitations of Raw PPMI Vectors

  • High-dimensional: With a 50,000-word vocabulary, vectors have 50,000 dimensions — expensive to store and compare.
  • Sparse: Most entries are 0 (most word pairs never co-occur). Sparse vectors are inefficient for neural networks.
  • No generalisation: Two words can have identical meanings but never share a context word in the corpus — they'll appear unrelated.
This motivates dimensionality reduction — Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) compresses a 50,000-dimensional PPMI matrix into dense 300-dimensional vectors. And word2vec (Module 9) learns dense vectors directly by predicting context words with a neural network, implicitly discovering a structure similar to PPMI but far more scalable.

Problem 1: PMI Calculation

Easy

Given co-occurrence statistics from a corpus:

  • count(dogs, pets) = 3
  • count(dogs) = 8     count(pets) = 5
  • Total co-occurrence tokens N = 50

Compute PMI(dogs, pets). Is the value positive or negative? What does this tell you about the semantic relationship?

Compute the three probabilities:

P(\text{dogs}, \text{pets}) = \frac{3}{50} = 0.06
P(\text{dogs}) = \frac{8}{50} = 0.16 \qquad P(\text{pets}) = \frac{5}{50} = 0.10

Now apply the PMI formula:

\text{PMI}(\text{dogs}, \text{pets}) = \log_2 \frac{0.06}{0.16 \times 0.10} = \log_2 \frac{0.06}{0.016} = \log_2 3.75 \approx 1.91

The value is positive, which means "dogs" and "pets" co-occur about 3.75 times more often than chance predicts. This indicates a strong semantic association — consistent with our intuition that dogs are pets.

Problem 2: PPMI Vectors and Cosine Similarity

Medium

Two words have PPMI vectors over a 5-word vocabulary:

\mathbf{w}_1 = [0.5,\; 1.2,\; 0.0,\; 0.8,\; 0.3]
\mathbf{w}_2 = [0.4,\; 1.1,\; 0.2,\; 0.9,\; 0.0]

Compute their cosine similarity. Are these words semantically similar?

Step 1: Dot product

\mathbf{w}_1 \cdot \mathbf{w}_2 = (0.5)(0.4) + (1.2)(1.1) + (0)(0.2) + (0.8)(0.9) + (0.3)(0) = 0.20 + 1.32 + 0 + 0.72 + 0 = 2.24

Step 2: Norms

\|\mathbf{w}_1\| = \sqrt{0.25 + 1.44 + 0 + 0.64 + 0.09} = \sqrt{2.42} \approx 1.556
\|\mathbf{w}_2\| = \sqrt{0.16 + 1.21 + 0.04 + 0.81 + 0} = \sqrt{2.22} \approx 1.490

Step 3: Cosine similarity

\text{cosine\_sim}(\mathbf{w}_1, \mathbf{w}_2) = \frac{2.24}{1.556 \times 1.490} = \frac{2.24}{2.318} \approx 0.966

A cosine similarity of 0.966 is very high (maximum is 1.0). These two words are highly semantically similar — their PPMI vectors point in nearly the same direction, meaning they appear in very similar contexts.

Problem 3: Alternative Weighting Schemes Beyond PPMI

Hard

Raw co-occurrence counts are dominated by frequent function words. PPMI is one remedy — but it is not the only one. List three alternative weighting schemes beyond PPMI, explain the rationale for each, and write the mathematical formula for one of them.

1. TF-IDF applied to co-occurrence

Treat each target word as a "document" and each context word as a "term". Apply Inverse Document Frequency: context words that appear near many target words get down-weighted (like stop words). Context words that are more specific get up-weighted. The rationale mirrors document retrieval: specificity signals relevance.

2. Shifted PPMI (SPPMI)

Add a smoothing shift parameter k that penalises all PMI values, retaining only high-confidence positive associations:

\text{SPPMI}_k(x, y) = \max\!\left(0,\; \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x)\,P(y)} - \log_2 k\right)

This is directly analogous to the negative sampling objective in word2vec (with k = number of negative samples). Setting k > 1 forces the model to require stronger-than-chance association before assigning a non-zero weight.

3. Sublinear count scaling

Replace raw co-occurrence count \text{count}(x, y) with \log(1 + \text{count}(x, y)) or \sqrt{\text{count}(x, y)} before computing PMI. The rationale: the semantic information added by seeing "dogs–pets" for the 50th time is far less than seeing it for the first time. Sublinear scaling compresses the dynamic range, reducing the dominance of extremely frequent pairs without discarding them entirely.

Quiz

Q1. The distributional hypothesis states that:

Words can be distributed across documents uniformly.
Words occurring in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings.
Rare words have more specific meanings than common ones.
Every word has a unique distributional fingerprint unrelated to others.
Correct. Firth's "you shall know a word by the company it keeps" — context predicts meaning, enabling unsupervised semantic learning. The distributional hypothesis links meaning to context: similar contexts → similar meanings. This is the foundation of all distributional word representations.

Q2. PMI(x, y) < 0 means:

The two words are antonyms.
x and y co-occur less often than expected if they were independent.
The words never co-occur.
There is a data error in the co-occurrence matrix.
Correct. Negative PMI means the observed co-occurrence P(x,y) is below the expected P(x)×P(y) — the words tend to avoid each other. PMI<0 means co-occurrence is below random chance expectation — not that they never co-occur, just less often than chance predicts.

Q3. Why is PPMI preferred over raw PMI for sparse NLP data?

PPMI is faster to compute.
Negative PMI values are unreliable for rare word pairs with zero or very low counts.
PPMI eliminates all stop words.
PPMI guarantees cosine similarity equals 1 for similar words.
Correct. When two words never co-occur, PMI = −∞ (log of 0). Clipping at 0 (PPMI) prevents these unreliable negative values from dominating similarity calculations. The issue is reliability: PMI=−∞ for zero-count pairs is not meaningful. PPMI clips these to 0, making vectors more stable for downstream similarity.

Q4. Cosine similarity is preferred over Euclidean distance for comparing word vectors because:

It is faster to compute.
It is invariant to the magnitude (length) of the vectors, focusing only on direction.
It always returns a value between 0 and 1.
It works only for binary vectors.
Correct. Cosine ignores vector magnitude — a word appearing 10× more in a corpus has a longer vector but the same semantic direction. Euclidean distance would wrongly penalise this. Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors, not their length. This makes it robust to frequency differences in the corpus. Note: it can be negative for non-PPMI vectors.
