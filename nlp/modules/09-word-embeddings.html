From Sparse to Dense — Building on Module 8

Module 8 showed that TF-IDF gives each word a number — its importance in a document. But TF-IDF vectors are huge (|vocabulary| dimensions) and treat every word as orthogonal. Word embeddings solve both problems: dense low-dimensional vectors that encode semantic similarity.

TF-IDF vectors are 50,000-dimensional and treat every word as orthogonal — "dog" and "cat" have zero cosine similarity, same as "dog" and "calculus". Word embeddings replace this with dense 100–300 dimensional vectors learned from corpus statistics, where similar contexts produce similar vectors.

The Distributional Hypothesis — Recap from Module 6

Module 6 introduced the distributional hypothesis: "you shall know a word by the company it keeps" (Firth, 1957), operationalised via PMI over raw co-occurrence counts. The problem: a 50,000-word vocabulary produces a 50,000×50,000 co-occurrence matrix — enormous and sparse. Word embeddings are the learned, dense version of the same idea, compressing distributional statistics into a compact d-dimensional vector at a fraction of the cost.

Word2Vec — The Core Idea

Word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) trains a shallow neural network to predict words from context, then discards the network and keeps the weights — the input-to-hidden weight matrix IS the embedding table. Words appearing in similar contexts (e.g., "dog", "kitten", "hamster" all predicted by similar context windows) develop similar representations purely from co-occurrence patterns, with no explicit semantic rules.

1
Represent each word as a one-hot vector
Each word is a one-hot vector of length |V| — one dimension is 1, all others are 0. This is the network input.
2
Multiply by weight matrix W to get the embedding
W (shape |V|×d) maps the one-hot input to a d-dimensional hidden layer. Multiplying by a one-hot vector simply selects the corresponding row of W — that row IS the word embedding. W is what we learn.
3
Predict surrounding words (Skip-gram) or center word (CBOW)
W' (shape d×|V|) maps the hidden embedding to a probability distribution over the vocabulary. Skip-gram trains this to predict context words given a center word; CBOW averages context embeddings to predict the center. After training, W is the embedding table.

Skip-gram vs. CBOW

Skip-gram: Given a center word, predict the surrounding context words. For "sat" in "the cat sat on the mat" with window size 2, Skip-gram trains on the pairs (sat→the), (sat→cat), (sat→on), (sat→the). Because every center word generates multiple training pairs, rare words accumulate more gradient signal. Better for rare words and smaller datasets.

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words): Given the surrounding context words, predict the missing center word. Context embeddings are averaged into a single vector, then used to predict the center. Averaging smooths out noise and benefits from high-frequency co-occurrences. Faster to train and better for frequent words.

Skip-gram Objective

T = corpus positions, c = window size. Skip-gram maximises the average log-probability of each context word given its center word:

\[J(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\sum_{-c \le j \le c,\, j \ne 0} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)\]

The conditional probability of context word o given center word c uses a softmax over the full vocabulary:

\[P(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(u_o^\top v_c)}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(u_w^\top v_c)}\]

\(v_c\) is the center word vector (row of W); \(u_o\) is the context vector (row of W'). Each word has two vectors; in practice we use \(v_c\) and discard \(u_o\) after training.

Negative Sampling: Computing the softmax denominator over |V| = 50,000 words at every training step is computationally prohibitive. Negative sampling approximates the objective by replacing the softmax with a simpler binary classification task: is this (center, context) pair real, or is it a noise pair? For each real training pair, k = 5–20 "negative" words are sampled (typically proportional to their frequency raised to the 3/4 power), and the model is trained to output a high score for the real pair and a low score for the noise pairs. This reduces each update from O(|V|) to O(k) operations — a 10,000× speedup.

GloVe — Global Vectors for Word Representation

GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) pre-computes the full global co-occurrence matrix X (\(X_{ij}\) = times word i appears in context of word j), then trains vectors to predict \(\log X_{ij}\) — combining the statistical efficiency of global matrix methods (Module 6's PMI) with word2vec's compact representations. The objective:

\[J = \sum_{i,j=1}^{|V|} f(X_{ij})\left(w_i^\top \tilde{w}_j + b_i + \tilde{b}_j - \log X_{ij}\right)^2\]

\(w_i\), \(\tilde{w}_j\) are word/context vectors, \(b_i\), \(\tilde{b}_j\) are biases, and \(f(X_{ij})\) downweights very frequent pairs:

\[f(x) = \begin{cases} (x/x_{\max})^\alpha & \text{if } x < x_{\max} \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}\]

with \(x_{\max} = 100\) and \(\alpha = 3/4\) being typical choices.

GloVe vs. word2vec in practice: GloVe trains faster than word2vec because the co-occurrence matrix only needs to be computed once, after which training iterates over a much smaller number of non-zero co-occurrence pairs. Both methods typically produce comparable downstream performance. Pre-trained GloVe vectors (trained on Common Crawl, Wikipedia, or Twitter) are freely available and remain widely used as initialisation for downstream NLP tasks even in the era of transformer models.

The Embedding Space — Geometry Encodes Meaning

Well-trained embeddings encode three geometric properties consistently across word2vec and GloVe:

1. Semantic clustering. Similar words cluster together — "dog", "cat", "puppy", "kitten" form one neighbourhood; "Ferrari", "Porsche", "sedan" form another, with no explicit labels.

2. Semantic directions. Directions in embedding space encode meaningful transformations. Classic gender example:

\[\text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman} \approx \text{queen}\]

Formally: \(w^* = \underset{w \in V}{\arg\max}\;\cos(\text{emb}(w),\;\text{emb}(\text{king}) - \text{emb}(\text{man}) + \text{emb}(\text{woman}))\). The man→woman offset encodes a gender direction that generalises across domains. Similar patterns hold for capital cities (France→Paris, Germany→Berlin), verb tenses (walk→walked), and comparatives (big→bigger).

3. Cosine similarity measures semantic relatedness. \(\cos(\theta) = \frac{u \cdot v}{\|u\| \cdot \|v\|}\). Preferred over Euclidean distance because it is scale-invariant — frequent words may have larger-magnitude vectors from more gradient updates; cosine normalises this out.

Representative scores from 300-dim GloVe (Common Crawl, 840B tokens):

Word Pair Cosine Similarity Relationship
dog / cat 0.82 Closely related animals (both pets)
dog / car 0.18 Unrelated concepts
king / queen 0.75 Same domain, related by gender
France / Paris 0.71 Country / capital (frequent co-occurrence)
happy / sad 0.49 Antonyms — still related! (see below)

"happy / sad" scores higher than "dog / car" because antonyms share nearly identical contexts ("I feel ___", "she was ___"). Distributional semantics captures contextual similarity, not semantic opposition — a fundamental limitation explored in Practice Problem 3.

Python — Analogy task with word vectors 
import numpy as np

def word_analogy(nlp, word_a, word_b, word_c, top_k=5):
    """Find word_d such that word_a : word_b :: word_c : word_d"""
    target = nlp(word_b).vector - nlp(word_a).vector + nlp(word_c).vector

    # Find closest words (excluding input words)
    exclude = {word_a, word_b, word_c}
    similarities = {}
    for word in nlp.vocab:
        if word.has_vector and word.is_lower and word.text not in exclude:
            sim = np.dot(target, word.vector) / (
                np.linalg.norm(target) * np.linalg.norm(word.vector) + 1e-8
            )
            similarities[word.text] = sim

    return sorted(similarities.items(), key=lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)[:top_k]

results = word_analogy(nlp, "man", "king", "woman")
print("man:king :: woman:?", results[:3])
man:king :: woman:? [('queen', 0.7537), ('monarch', 0.7021), ('princess', 0.6891)]

Contextual Embeddings — A Forward Look

The polysemy problem: Word2vec and GloVe give every word a single, fixed vector regardless of context. But the word "bank" has at least two meanings — "financial institution" and "river bank" — that appear in radically different contexts. A static embedding averages over all usages, producing a vector that represents neither sense well.

Models like ELMo (2018), BERT (2018), and GPT (2018–) solve this by generating contextual embeddings: the same word gets a different vector depending on the sentence it appears in. "I deposited money in the bank" and "We sat on the bank of the river" produce different vectors for "bank" — vectors that encode the contextual sense. These transformer-based models are trained on massive corpora with a self-supervised objective (masked language modeling or next-token prediction) and form the foundation of virtually all modern NLP systems.

Practice

Problem 1: Cosine Similarity by Hand

Easy

Given two word vectors \(\mathbf{u} = [1, 0, 1]\) and \(\mathbf{v} = [0, 1, 1]\), calculate their cosine similarity.

Cosine similarity is defined as:

\[\cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{v}\|}\]

Step 1 — Dot product:

\[\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = (1)(0) + (0)(1) + (1)(1) = 0 + 0 + 1 = 1\]

Step 2 — Magnitudes:

\[\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{1^2 + 0^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2} \qquad \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{0^2 + 1^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{2}\]

Step 3 — Cosine similarity:

\[\cos(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2} \cdot \sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{2} = 0.5\]

Cosine similarity of 0.5 corresponds to a 60° angle — moderate relatedness (one shared context dimension out of two each).

Problem 3: Why Do Antonyms Have High Cosine Similarity?

Hard

Our similarity table shows that "happy" and "sad" have a cosine similarity of 0.49 — much higher than "happy" and "car" (roughly 0.15). Antonyms have opposite meanings, yet their vectors are relatively close. Explain this phenomenon. What does it reveal about the distributional hypothesis as a basis for word meaning? What types of semantic information cannot be recovered from distributional statistics alone?

Why antonyms cluster: "Happy" and "sad" appear in identical grammatical environments ("I feel ___", "she was ___", "he looked ___"), co-occurring with the same emotion/people vocabulary. The model assigns them nearby vectors. "Happy" and "car" share almost no context words, producing distant vectors.

The distributional hypothesis captures contextual similarity, not semantic opposition — synonyms and antonyms often appear in the same situations; the model cannot distinguish positive from negative valence.

What distributional semantics cannot recover:

  • Semantic polarity / sentiment: Whether a word is positive or negative
  • Logical negation / opposition: That "happy" and "unhappy" are antonyms, not synonyms
  • Scalar relationships: That "warm" is between "cool" and "hot" on a temperature scale
  • World knowledge not reflected in co-occurrence: That "Paris" is north of "Marseille"

These limitations motivate post-hoc retrofitting (injecting WordNet antonym/synonym constraints) and sentiment-aware training objectives.

Quiz

Q1. What does the hidden-layer weight matrix W in word2vec represent?

Correct. In the word2vec architecture, the weight matrix W of shape |V|×d connects the one-hot input to the hidden layer. Because multiplying a one-hot vector by W simply selects a row, each row of W is the embedding for the corresponding word. After training, W is the embedding table we extract and use. The training loss is a cross-entropy or negative sampling objective computed downstream of W. The second weight matrix W' produces the output distribution, and the one-hot vector is the raw input, not W itself.

Q2. Skip-gram is preferred over CBOW for rare words because:

Correct. For a center word with window size c, Skip-gram generates 2c training pairs (one per context position). A rare word appearing only 50 times in the corpus therefore contributes 50 × 2c gradient updates — much more signal than CBOW, where those 50 occurrences contribute 50 single-label predictions. Window size is an independent hyperparameter available to both architectures. Both Skip-gram and CBOW can use negative sampling. CBOW is actually faster because it averages context vectors into a single vector and makes one prediction per training step.

Q3. Why do antonyms (happy/sad) have higher cosine similarity than unrelated words (happy/car)?

Correct. "Happy" and "sad" appear in virtually identical syntactic environments — "I feel ___", "she was ___", "he seemed ___" — and co-occur with the same vocabulary of emotions, people, and events. The distributional hypothesis captures contextual similarity, not semantic polarity. "Happy" and "car" share almost no context words, so their vectors are distant. This is not a data quantity problem; it is an inherent limitation of distributional semantics. Cosine similarity measures the angle between vectors, which reflects co-occurrence patterns — not semantic opposition or grammatical category alone.

Q4. What is the main advantage of contextual embeddings (BERT) over static embeddings (word2vec)?

Correct. Word2vec and GloVe assign a single fixed vector to each word regardless of context. BERT and similar transformer models generate a different embedding for each token occurrence based on the full surrounding sentence. This means "bank" in "savings bank" and "bank" in "river bank" receive different vectors — solving the polysemy problem that static embeddings cannot address. Contextual models actually train much more slowly (BERT-base requires days on 64 TPUs). Vocabulary size is a secondary characteristic. Transformer models require far more training data than word2vec to reach good performance.

Word embeddings give us rich semantic representations — a word's meaning encoded as a point in space. But language isn't just isolated words; it's sequences.
