## From Sparse to Dense

| Property | BoW / TF-IDF | Word Embeddings |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Dimensionality | \|V\| ≈ 50,000+ | 100–300 dimensions |
| Density | Sparse (mostly zeros) | Dense (all dims populated) |
| Semantic information | None — words are orthogonal | Encoded — similar words cluster |
| Similarity of "dog" and "cat" | 0.0 (orthogonal) | ≈ 0.82 (high cosine similarity) |

## Word2Vec — The Core Idea

Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) trains a shallow neural network to predict words from context, then discards the network and keeps the weights — the input-to-hidden weight matrix IS the embedding table.

<DerivationSteps>
<DerivationStep number={1} title="Represent each word as a one-hot vector.">
Each word is a one-hot vector of length |V| — one dimension is 1, all others are 0. This is the network input.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={2} title="Multiply by weight matrix W to get the embedding.">
**W** (shape |V|×d) maps the one-hot input to a d-dimensional hidden layer. Multiplying by a one-hot vector simply *selects the corresponding row of W* — that row IS the word embedding. **W** is what we learn.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={3} title="Predict surrounding words (Skip-gram) or center word (CBOW).">
**W'** (shape d×|V|) maps the hidden embedding to a probability distribution over the vocabulary. Skip-gram trains this to predict context words given a center word; CBOW averages context embeddings to predict the center.
</DerivationStep>
</DerivationSteps>

<ConceptBox title="Skip-gram vs. CBOW">
**Skip-gram** — given a *center word*, predict surrounding context words. For "sat" in "the cat sat on the mat" (window=2), trains on pairs: (sat→the), (sat→cat), (sat→on), (sat→the). Each center word generates multiple training pairs → rare words accumulate more gradient signal. **Better for rare words and smaller datasets.**

**CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)** — given surrounding context words, predict the missing center word. Context embeddings are averaged into one vector to predict the center. Averaging smooths noise and benefits from high-frequency co-occurrences. **Faster to train, better for frequent words.**
</ConceptBox>

## Skip-gram Objective

T = corpus positions, c = window size. Skip-gram maximizes the average log-probability of each context word given its center word:

$$J(\theta) = -\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\sum_{-c \le j \le c,\, j \ne 0} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)$$

The conditional probability uses a softmax over the full vocabulary:

$$P(o \mid c) = \frac{\exp(u_o^\top v_c)}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(u_w^\top v_c)}$$

$v_c$ is the center word vector (row of **W**); $u_o$ is the context vector (row of **W'**).

<ConceptBox title="Negative Sampling">
Computing the softmax denominator over |V| = 50,000 words at every training step is prohibitive. **Negative sampling** replaces the softmax with binary classification: is this (center, context) pair real, or noise? For each real pair, k = 5–20 "negative" words are sampled (proportional to frequency^(3/4)), and the model outputs high score for real pairs and low for noise. This reduces each update from O(|V|) to O(k) — a ~10,000× speedup.
</ConceptBox>

## GloVe — Global Vectors for Word Representation

GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) pre-computes the full global co-occurrence matrix $X$ ($X_{ij}$ = times word $i$ appears in context of word $j$), then trains vectors to predict $\log X_{ij}$:

$$J = \sum_{i,j=1}^{|V|} f(X_{ij})\left(w_i^\top \tilde{w}_j + b_i + \tilde{b}_j - \log X_{ij}\right)^2$$

where $f(X_{ij})$ downweights very frequent pairs:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} (x/x_{\max})^\alpha & \text{if } x < x_{\max} \\ 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

with $x_{\max} = 100$ and $\alpha = 3/4$. GloVe trains faster than Word2Vec because the co-occurrence matrix only needs to be computed once.

## The Embedding Space — Geometry Encodes Meaning

Well-trained embeddings encode three geometric properties:

**1. Semantic clustering.** Similar words cluster together — "dog", "cat", "puppy", "kitten" form one neighborhood; "Ferrari", "Porsche", "sedan" form another.

**2. Semantic directions.**

$$\text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman} \approx \text{queen}$$

The man→woman offset encodes a gender direction that generalizes across domains. Similar patterns hold for capital cities (France→Paris, Germany→Berlin) and verb tenses (walk→walked).

**3. Cosine similarity measures semantic relatedness.**

Representative scores from 300-dim GloVe (Common Crawl, 840B tokens):

| Word Pair | Cosine Similarity | Relationship |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| dog / cat | 0.82 | Closely related animals |
| dog / car | 0.18 | Unrelated |
| king / queen | 0.73 | Royalty, different genders |
| France / Paris | 0.71 | Country / capital |

<ConceptBox title="Contextualized Embeddings">
Word2Vec and GloVe produce *static* embeddings — "bank" always has the same vector regardless of context. **Contextualized embeddings** (ELMo, BERT) produce different representations for the same word in different contexts, directly resolving polysemy. BERT's "bank" vector differs in "river bank" vs. "savings bank" contexts.

The shift from static to contextual embeddings was the key breakthrough enabling modern NLP — BERT (2018) demonstrated that pretraining on language modeling, then fine-tuning on downstream tasks, outperformed all prior approaches.
</ConceptBox>
