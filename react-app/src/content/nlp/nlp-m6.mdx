WordNet (Module 5) is hand-crafted and hard to scale. Can we instead *learn* meaning automatically from raw text? Firth (1957) answered this: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." The **distributional hypothesis** — words appearing in similar contexts have similar meanings — is the foundation of modern word representations.

## The Distributional Hypothesis

<ConceptBox title="Why Distributional Semantics Works">
*dog* and *cat* both appear near **pet**, **feed**, **vet**, **furry** — and almost never near *photosynthesis* or *transistor*. Similar context distributions correctly predict semantic relatedness. *dog* and *democracy* share almost no context words, correctly predicting semantic distance.

This hypothesis is the foundation of Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText, and LLM input layers — PMI and co-occurrence matrices are the prerequisite for understanding why they work.
</ConceptBox>

Four properties make it practical:
- **No labeling required** — fully unsupervised; any raw text corpus suffices
- **Language-agnostic** — same algorithm for English, Swahili, or ancient Greek
- **Domain-adaptable** — train on medical text and "discharge" carries a clinical meaning
- **Scalable** — more data almost always yields higher-quality representations

## Co-occurrence Matrices

A **co-occurrence matrix** has target words as rows, context words as columns, and co-occurrence counts as cells.

<ConceptBox title="Window-Based Co-occurrence">
Given a sentence and a window size $k$, every pair of words within distance $k$ (positions $i-k$ to $i+k$, excluding position $i$ itself) is counted as a co-occurrence. Common choices: $k = 2$ (tight syntactic context) or $k = 5$ (broader topical context).
</ConceptBox>

Four-sentence corpus, $k = 2$:
```
"I like dogs and cats"
"Dogs are loyal pets"
"Cats are independent pets"
"I prefer dogs over cats"
```

Subset of resulting co-occurrence matrix:

| Target \ Context | dogs | cats | pets | loyal | independent |
|-----------------|------|------|------|-------|-------------|
| **dogs** | 0 | 3 | 2 | 1 | 0 |
| **cats** | 3 | 0 | 2 | 0 | 1 |
| **pets** | 2 | 2 | 0 | 1 | 1 |

*dogs* and *cats* have similar row vectors — the distributional hypothesis made numerical.

<CodeBlock language="python">
{`import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict

def build_cooccurrence(sentences, window=2):
    all_words = [w for sent in sentences for w in sent]
    vocab = sorted(set(all_words))
    word2idx = {w: i for i, w in enumerate(vocab)}

    cooc = np.zeros((len(vocab), len(vocab)))
    for sent in sentences:
        for i, w in enumerate(sent):
            for j in range(max(0, i - window), min(len(sent), i + window + 1)):
                if i != j:
                    cooc[word2idx[w], word2idx[sent[j]]] += 1
    return cooc, vocab, word2idx

sentences = [
    ["i", "like", "dogs", "and", "cats"],
    ["dogs", "are", "loyal", "pets"],
    ["cats", "are", "independent", "pets"],
    ["i", "prefer", "dogs", "over", "cats"]
]
cooc, vocab, w2i = build_cooccurrence(sentences)`}
</CodeBlock>

**Problem with raw counts:** "the", "is", "and" appear everywhere — "dog–the" has enormous counts despite carrying no semantic information. This motivates PMI weighting.

## Pointwise Mutual Information (PMI)

PMI measures how much more often two words co-occur than expected under independence:

<DerivationSteps>
<DerivationStep number={1} title="Define joint and marginal probabilities.">
From the co-occurrence matrix, estimate:

$$P(x, y) = \frac{\text{count}(x, y)}{N} \qquad P(x) = \frac{\text{count}(x)}{N} \qquad P(y) = \frac{\text{count}(y)}{N}$$

where $N$ is the total number of co-occurrence tokens (sum of all cells).
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={2} title="Compute PMI.">
$$\text{PMI}(x, y) = \log_2 \frac{P(x, y)}{P(x) \cdot P(y)}$$

If $x$ and $y$ are independent, $P(x,y) = P(x)P(y)$ and PMI = 0. Positive PMI means they attract; negative means they repel.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={3} title="Clip to PPMI.">
$$\text{PPMI}(x, y) = \max\bigl(0,\; \text{PMI}(x, y)\bigr)$$

Negative PMI values are unreliable for rare pairs (where $P(x,y) \approx 0$ gives PMI $\to -\infty$). Clipping at 0 discards unreliable negatives.
</DerivationStep>
</DerivationSteps>

**Key insight:** "ice" and "the" co-occur enormously, but PMI("ice","the") ≈ 0 because "the" appears near every word by chance. PMI("ice","cream") is large precisely because that pairing exceeds chance expectation.

<CodeBlock language="python">
{`def compute_ppmi(cooc):
    total = cooc.sum()
    row_sums = cooc.sum(axis=1, keepdims=True)
    col_sums = cooc.sum(axis=0, keepdims=True)

    pxy = cooc / total
    px  = row_sums / total
    py  = col_sums / total

    with np.errstate(divide='ignore', invalid='ignore'):
        pmi = np.log2(pxy / (px * py))
    pmi  = np.nan_to_num(pmi, nan=0.0, neginf=0.0)
    ppmi = np.maximum(0, pmi)
    return ppmi

ppmi = compute_ppmi(cooc)
# ppmi[w2i['dogs'], w2i['loyal']]       → positive (attract)
# ppmi[w2i['dogs'], w2i['independent']] → 0.0 (no co-occurrence)`}
</CodeBlock>

## Building and Interpreting the PMI Matrix

Each word is now a **high-dimensional sparse vector**. Semantic similarity is measured with **cosine similarity**:

$$\text{cosine\_sim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{v}\|}$$

Range: $[-1, +1]$; for PPMI vectors (all non-negative), $[0, 1]$. Preferred over Euclidean distance because it is *invariant to vector magnitude* — a word appearing twice as often gets a longer vector but the same semantic direction.

On the four-sentence corpus, the most similar word to "dogs" by cosine PPMI is **"cats"** — they share the same high-PMI context words (pets, "i", "prefer").

<ConceptBox title="Limitations of Raw PPMI Vectors">
- **High-dimensional** — with a 50,000-word vocabulary, vectors have 50,000 dimensions
- **Sparse** — most entries are 0 (most word pairs never co-occur)
- **No generalization** — two words can have identical meanings but never share a context word in the corpus

SVD compresses sparse PPMI vectors into dense low-dimensional ones. Word2Vec (Module 9) learns dense vectors directly, implicitly recovering a structure similar to PPMI at much larger scale.
</ConceptBox>
