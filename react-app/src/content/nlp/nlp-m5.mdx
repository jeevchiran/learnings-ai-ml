## Semantic Relations Between Words

<ConceptBox title="Six Foundational Semantic Relations">
- **Synonymy** — similar meaning: *couch / sofa*, *begin / start*. Perfect synonymy is rare — synonyms differ in connotation, register, or collocation.
- **Antonymy** — opposite meaning, three subtypes: *complementary* (alive/dead — no middle), *gradable* (hot/cold — admit degrees), *converse* (buy/sell — two perspectives on one relation).
- **Hyponymy & Hypernymy** — the IS-A hierarchy: "dog" is a *hyponym* of "animal"; "animal" is a *hypernym* of "dog". Transitive: poodle → dog → animal → organism.
- **Holonymy & Meronymy** — the PART-OF relation: finger is a *meronym* of hand. A wheel is part of a car (meronymy), not a kind of car (hyponymy).
- **Polysemy** — one word, multiple *related* senses: "head" — body part, organizational leader, head of a river — all connected by a core notion of "topmost/primary".
- **Homonymy** — one word form, completely *unrelated* meanings: "bank" (financial institution, Old French *banque*) vs. "bank" (river bank, Old Norse *bakki*) — converged in spelling by historical accident.
</ConceptBox>

**Polysemy vs. homonymy matters for disambiguation.** Polysemous senses share conceptual structure, so context clues that resolve one sense often help others. Homonyms share nothing — disambiguating "bank" requires completely independent evidence for each sense.

## WordNet

WordNet is a lexical database for English that organizes words into **synsets** — synonym sets — and encodes semantic relations between them.

<ConceptBox title="The Synset: WordNet's Core Unit">
Each synset represents a single concept and contains:
- **Lemmas**: the set of synonymous word forms expressing this concept
- **Gloss**: a short natural-language definition
- **Example sentences**: usage examples
- **Relations**: hypernyms, hyponyms, holonyms, meronyms, antonyms — pointing to other synsets

Example: synset `car.n.01` has lemmas (car, auto, automobile, motorcar) and gloss "a motor vehicle with four wheels; usually propelled by an internal combustion engine".
</ConceptBox>

Naming convention: `word.pos.nn` where `nn` is the sense number — `bank.n.01` is the financial institution, `bank.n.09` is the river bank.

## Word Sense Disambiguation

**Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD)** is the task of determining, given a word in context, which of its dictionary senses is intended.

<ConceptBox title="WSD is AI-Complete">
Solving WSD perfectly requires full world knowledge and common-sense reasoning. "He saw her duck" — bird or action? — can only be resolved with physical context. No finite rule set covers all cases.
</ConceptBox>

Wrong sense selection cascades through downstream systems: MT maps Spanish "banco" to bank or bench depending on context; QA "What does the bank regulate?" requires the financial sense; IE needs "plant" = manufacturing facility, not vegetation.

### Knowledge-Based WSD: The Lesk Algorithm

The simplest knowledge-based method (Lesk, 1986) exploits WordNet gloss overlap:

<DerivationSteps>
<DerivationStep number={1} title="Extract context words.">
Take surrounding words within a window (typically the sentence). Remove stopwords and punctuation. What remains is the context set.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={2} title="Retrieve candidate synsets.">
Look up the target word in WordNet. Each synset it belongs to is a candidate sense. For "bank" (noun), there are 10 candidate synsets.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={3} title="Compute gloss overlap.">
For each candidate synset, count how many words in its gloss (definition) also appear in the context set. This is the overlap score.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={4} title="Select argmax.">
Choose the synset with the highest overlap score. In ties, fall back to the most frequent sense (MFS baseline).
</DerivationStep>
</DerivationSteps>

**Example.** Sentence: "He went to the bank to deposit his salary." Target: "bank".

| Synset | Gloss (excerpt) | Overlap with (went, deposit, salary) |
|--------|----------------|--------------------------------------|
| bank.n.01 | *financial institution that accepts **deposits** and channels money into lending* | 1 (**deposit**) |
| bank.n.09 | *sloping land beside a body of water* | 0 |

→ Lesk selects `bank.n.01` (financial institution). ✓

<ConceptBox title="Limitations of Lesk">
- Gloss overlap is sparse — many synsets have short glosses with few content words
- **Extended Lesk** also overlaps hyponym/hypernym glosses for richer coverage
- Knowledge-based methods fall significantly short of supervised WSD systems (which use annotated sense-tagged corpora)
- Modern systems use contextual embeddings (BERT) for WSD directly
</ConceptBox>

### Supervised WSD

With sense-tagged training data (e.g., SemEval benchmarks, SenseEval), supervised classifiers treat WSD as multi-class classification. Features include:

- Surrounding words in a context window (bag-of-words)
- POS tags of surrounding words
- Syntactic relations (subject-of, object-of)

Modern state-of-the-art: fine-tune a pre-trained transformer on sense-tagged data.

<CodeBlock language="python">
{`from nltk.corpus import wordnet as wn
from nltk.tokenize import word_tokenize
from nltk.corpus import stopwords

def lesk(word, sentence):
    """Simplified Lesk algorithm using NLTK WordNet."""
    context = set(w.lower() for w in word_tokenize(sentence) 
                  if w.lower() not in stopwords.words('english'))
    
    best_synset = None
    best_overlap = -1
    
    for synset in wn.synsets(word):
        # Tokenize the gloss (definition)
        gloss = set(w.lower() for w in word_tokenize(synset.definition()))
        overlap = len(context & gloss)
        
        if overlap > best_overlap:
            best_overlap = overlap
            best_synset = synset
    
    return best_synset

sentence = "He went to the bank to deposit his salary."
result = lesk("bank", sentence)
print(result.name(), ":", result.definition())
# bank.n.01 : a financial institution that accepts deposits ...`}
</CodeBlock>
