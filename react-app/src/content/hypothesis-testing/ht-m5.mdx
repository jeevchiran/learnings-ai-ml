**Real-world hook — blood pressure treatment.** A cardiologist wants to evaluate whether a 12-week lifestyle intervention (diet and exercise) reduces systolic blood pressure. She measures each of 15 patients before the program and again after completion. The key phrase is "each patient" — the same person is measured twice. These two measurements are not independent of each other: a patient who starts with high blood pressure is likely to have higher-than-average blood pressure at both time points. If we treated this as two independent groups (as in Module 4), we would be ignoring this built-in structure — and throwing away statistical power in the process. The **paired t-test** exploits the connection between the two measurements to give us a more sensitive and more appropriate test.

## 1. Paired vs. Independent — Why It Matters

In Module 4, the two groups consisted of entirely different people: Group A vs Group B. Each measurement belonged to one group only; there was no correspondence between an observation in Group A and any observation in Group B. In a paired design, the same subject (or a carefully matched pair) appears in **both** conditions.

Paired designs arise in three main forms:

- **Before/After:** The same subject is measured before and after a treatment, intervention, or period of time.
- **Matched pairs:** Two distinct subjects are matched on key characteristics (age, weight, baseline health) so that within each pair, one receives the treatment and the other serves as the control.
- **Crossover:** Each subject receives both treatments in sequence (with a washout period in between), and the order is randomized.

### Why pairing is powerful

Consider the cardiologist's study. Patient A might naturally have a systolic BP of 155 mmHg and patient B might naturally have 135 mmHg. This between-subject variability has nothing to do with whether the intervention works. Yet if we analyzed the pre- and post-intervention readings as two independent groups, this person-to-person noise would inflate the standard error and make it harder to detect the intervention's true effect.

The remedy: compute the difference $d_i = x_{\text{before},i} - x_{\text{after},i}$ for each subject. Because both measurements come from the same person, patient A's natural tendency toward higher BP cancels out in the subtraction. The variability that remains in the $d_i$ values reflects only within-person change — exactly what we want to measure.

This reduction to differences means the paired t-test is, at its core, nothing more than a **one-sample t-test applied to the set of differences** $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n$.

## 2. Derivation of the Test Statistic

<DerivationSteps>
<DerivationStep number={1} title="Compute the difference for each pair.">
For subject $i$, the difference is $d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i}$ (before minus after, or treatment minus control). The sign convention determines which direction of difference will be positive — choose it to match the research hypothesis.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={2} title="State the hypotheses.">
The null hypothesis asserts no average change: $H_0: \mu_d = 0$. The alternative may be two-tailed ($H_1: \mu_d \neq 0$), right-tailed ($H_1: \mu_d > 0$), or left-tailed ($H_1: \mu_d < 0$), chosen to reflect the direction of the effect the researcher expects.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={3} title="Compute the sample mean of differences.">
$$\bar{d} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i$$

This is the point estimate of $\mu_d$, the true population mean difference. It is the quantity we will compare to zero.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={4} title="Compute the sample standard deviation of differences.">
$$s_d = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(d_i - \bar{d})^2}{n - 1}}$$

This measures how variable the individual differences are. A small $s_d$ means the treatment had a very consistent effect across subjects; a large $s_d$ means the effect was heterogeneous.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={5} title="Compute the standard error of d̄.">
$$\text{SE}(\bar{d}) = \frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The standard error quantifies the precision of $\bar{d}$ as an estimate of $\mu_d$. It shrinks as $n$ increases (more pairs = more precise estimate) and as $s_d$ decreases (more consistent differences = more precise estimate).
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={6} title="Form the paired t-statistic.">
$$t = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}$$

This is exactly the one-sample t-statistic from Module 3, applied to the differences. It measures how many standard errors $\bar{d}$ lies from zero.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={7} title="Identify the null distribution.">
Under $H_0$, the statistic $t$ follows a Student's t-distribution with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom. There are $n - 1$ degrees of freedom because we have $n$ differences and we estimate one parameter ($\bar{d}$) from them before computing $s_d$.

$$t \sim t(n-1) \quad \text{under } H_0$$
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={8} title="Make a decision.">
Compare the observed $t$ to the critical value $t^*(\alpha/2,\, n-1)$ for a two-tailed test (or $t^*(\alpha,\, n-1)$ for a one-tailed test), or compute the p-value from the t-distribution and compare to $\alpha$. Reject $H_0$ if $|t| > t^*$ (two-tailed) or if p-value < $\alpha$.
</DerivationStep>
</DerivationSteps>

## 3. Assumptions of the Paired t-Test

<ConceptBox title="Assumption 1 — Random sample of pairs">
The pairs must be randomly sampled from the population of interest, or the assignment to paired conditions must be random (as in a randomized crossover trial). Non-random sampling risks selection bias.
</ConceptBox>

<ConceptBox title="Assumption 2 — Normality of differences">
The differences $d_i$ must be approximately normally distributed. For small samples ($n < 30$), this is a consequential assumption — check it with a histogram or QQ-plot of the differences. For $n \geq 30$, the Central Limit Theorem ensures that $\bar{d}$ is approximately normal regardless of the shape of the difference distribution, making the test robust.
</ConceptBox>

<ConceptBox title="Assumption 3 — Independence between pairs">
Different pairs must be independent of each other. The within-pair dependence is intentional — that's the point of the design. But pair 1 and pair 2 must not influence each other (violated if pairs are family members or treated simultaneously in a way that allows contamination across pairs).
</ConceptBox>

<ConceptBox title="Assumption 4 — No extreme outliers in differences">
Outliers in the $d_i$ values can heavily distort both $\bar{d}$ and $s_d$, invalidating the t-statistic. Inspect the differences with a boxplot before proceeding. If outliers are present, consider a non-parametric alternative such as the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
</ConceptBox>

## 4. When to Use Paired vs. Independent Tests

| Feature | Paired t-Test | Independent t-Test |
|---------|---------------|-------------------|
| Sample structure | Same subject measured twice, or matched pairs | Two unrelated groups with no correspondence |
| n per group | Must be equal (n pairs) | Can differ between groups |
| Degrees of freedom | $n - 1$ (one per pair) | Welch approximation, typically near $n_1 + n_2 - 2$ |
| Between-subject noise | Eliminated by differencing within pairs | Present in SE, reduces power |
| Typical power | Higher when data is truly paired | Lower when pairing is available but ignored |
| When to use | Before/after, matched pair studies, crossover trials | Completely separate, unrelated groups |

<ConceptBox title="Key principle: the design determines the test">
If the data were collected in a paired fashion (same subject twice, or matched pairs), use the paired t-test. Using an independent-samples test on paired data ignores structure in the data that reduces variability and increases power. You do not get to "choose" the independent test because it is simpler — the data structure mandates the paired analysis.
</ConceptBox>

## 5. Worked Example: The Blood Pressure Study

**Research context and hypotheses.** Eight patients completed a 12-week lifestyle intervention. Systolic blood pressure was recorded for each patient before and after the program. Differences are defined as $d_i = x_{\text{before},i} - x_{\text{after},i}$, so a positive difference means blood pressure decreased (improvement). The cardiologist specifies a one-sided (right-tailed) alternative:

$$H_0: \mu_d = 0 \qquad H_1: \mu_d > 0 \quad (\text{intervention reduces BP})$$

**Data:**

| Patient | Before (mmHg) | After (mmHg) | $d_i$ (Before − After) |
|---------|---------------|--------------|------------------------|
| 1 | 152 | 144 | 8 |
| 2 | 143 | 137 | 6 |
| 3 | 160 | 149 | 11 |
| 4 | 137 | 128 | 9 |
| 5 | 148 | 140 | 8 |
| 6 | 155 | 145 | 10 |
| 7 | 142 | 136 | 6 |
| 8 | 138 | 130 | 8 |

**Step-by-step calculation (α = 0.05):**

**Step 1 — n = 8 pairs.**

**Step 2 — Mean of differences:**

$$\bar{d} = \frac{8 + 6 + 11 + 9 + 8 + 10 + 6 + 8}{8} = \frac{66}{8} = 8.25 \text{ mmHg}$$

**Step 3 — Standard deviation of differences.** Deviations from $\bar{d} = 8.25$ are: −0.25, −2.25, 2.75, 0.75, −0.25, 1.75, −2.25, −0.25. Sum of squared deviations = 21.5.

$$s_d = \sqrt{\frac{21.5}{7}} = \sqrt{3.071} \approx 1.753 \text{ mmHg}$$

**Step 4 — Standard error:**

$$\text{SE}(\bar{d}) = \frac{1.753}{\sqrt{8}} = \frac{1.753}{2.828} \approx 0.620 \text{ mmHg}$$

**Step 5 — Paired t-statistic:**

$$t = \frac{8.25}{0.620} \approx 13.31$$

**Step 6 — Degrees of freedom:** $df = n - 1 = 7$.

**Step 7 — Decision.** Right-tailed critical value at α = 0.05, df = 7: $t^* = 1.895$. Observed $t = 13.31 \gg 1.895$. **Reject H₀.** p-value ≈ 0.0000012.

**Conclusion.** At the 5% significance level, there is extremely strong statistical evidence that the 12-week lifestyle intervention reduces systolic blood pressure. Estimated mean reduction: 8.25 mmHg. 95% CI:

$$8.25 \pm 2.365 \times 0.620 = [6.78,\; 9.72] \text{ mmHg}$$

## 6. Practice Problem

<ConceptBox title="Statistics Workshop Scores">
A statistics tutor works with 6 students before and after a 4-week intensive workshop. Pre-workshop scores: 62, 71, 58, 75, 68, 64. Post-workshop scores: 74, 82, 70, 84, 76, 78.

Define $d_i = \text{post} - \text{pre}$ so a positive difference means improvement.

**Hypotheses:** $H_0: \mu_d = 0$ vs $H_1: \mu_d > 0$ (right-tailed, α = 0.05)

**Differences:** 12, 11, 12, 9, 8, 14. n = 6.

**d̄** = (12+11+12+9+8+14)/6 = 66/6 = **11**

**s_d:** Deviations: 1, 0, 1, −2, −3, 3. Squared: 1, 0, 1, 4, 9, 9. SS = 24. $s_d = \sqrt{24/5} = \sqrt{4.8} \approx 2.191$

**SE** = 2.191/√6 ≈ 0.895

**t** = 11/0.895 ≈ 12.29, df = 5

**Critical value** t*(0.05, 5) = 2.015. Since 12.29 >> 2.015: **Reject H₀**.

**Conclusion:** Strong evidence the workshop improves test scores. Mean improvement ≈ 11 points.
</ConceptBox>
