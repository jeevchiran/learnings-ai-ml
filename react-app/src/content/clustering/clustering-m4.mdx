<Bridge>
**The key insight.** A good clustering places each point closer to the members of its own cluster than to the members of any other cluster — by a wide margin. The silhouette score quantifies exactly this margin, for every single point in the dataset, and then averages them into a single interpretable number. WCSS alone cannot capture this: two clusters can each be individually tight while sitting right next to each other, overlapping at their boundaries.

## 1. Why WCSS Is Not Enough

WCSS measures **cohesion** — how tightly the points within each cluster pack around their centroid. That is a good property. But it cannot see **separation**. Two concrete failure modes:

<ConceptBox title="Case A: Tight but too close">
Two elongated clusters each have very low internal scatter, so WCSS is small. But they nearly touch. A point near the boundary could plausibly belong to either cluster. Cohesion is high; separation is poor.
</ConceptBox>

<ConceptBox title="Case B: Overlapping blobs">
Two clusters overlap substantially in the middle. Points in the overlap region are equidistant from both centroids. WCSS may be similar to Case A, yet intuitively the clustering is worse — many points are ambiguously assigned.
</ConceptBox>

Both cases might produce almost identical WCSS values, yet both are poor clusterings for different reasons. We need a metric that captures **cohesion AND separation simultaneously**.

## 2. The Silhouette Score — Derivation

The silhouette coefficient was introduced by Peter Rousseeuw in 1987. It assigns every data point a score between -1 and 1 measuring how well it sits inside its assigned cluster relative to alternative clusters.

### Step 1 — Intra-cluster distance a(i)

For point $x_i$ assigned to cluster $C_k$, define the **mean distance to all other members of its own cluster**:

$$a(i) = \frac{1}{|C_k| - 1} \sum_{j \in C_k,\, j \neq i} d(x_i,\, x_j)$$

The denominator is $|C_k| - 1$, not $|C_k|$, because we exclude point $i$ itself. This quantity measures **cohesion**: how tightly point $i$ belongs to its cluster. A small $a(i)$ means the point is deep inside its cluster; a large $a(i)$ means it is on the fringe.

### Step 2 — Nearest-cluster distance b(i)

For each cluster $C_{k'}$ that does not contain point $i$, compute the mean distance from $x_i$ to all points in $C_{k'}$:

$$b(i) = \min_{k' \neq k} \frac{1}{|C_{k'}|} \sum_{j \in C_{k'}} d(x_i,\, x_j)$$

We take the *minimum* over all competing clusters — this identifies the **nearest other cluster** to point $i$. A large $b(i)$ means the nearest competitor is far away, so the current assignment is well-justified.

### Step 3 — The silhouette score for point i

$$s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i),\, b(i))}$$

The numerator $b(i) - a(i)$ is positive when the point is closer to its own cluster than to the best competitor. The denominator normalises by the larger of the two values, mapping the result into $[-1, 1]$.

### Range derivation

<DerivationSteps>
<DerivationStep number={1} title="Case a(i) < b(i) — correctly clustered.">
Here $\max(a, b) = b$, so:

$$s(i) = \frac{b - a}{b} = 1 - \frac{a}{b} \in (0, 1]$$

The closer $a$ is to 0 (perfectly tight cluster) and the larger $b$ is (far competitor), the nearer $s(i)$ is to 1.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={2} title="Case a(i) = b(i) — boundary point.">
The numerator is zero:

$$s(i) = \frac{b - a}{\max(a, b)} = \frac{0}{\max(a, a)} = 0$$

The point is equidistant from its own cluster and the nearest competitor — it sits exactly on the decision boundary.
</DerivationStep>

<DerivationStep number={3} title="Case a(i) > b(i) — possibly misclustered.">
Here $\max(a, b) = a$, so:

$$s(i) = \frac{b - a}{a} = \frac{b}{a} - 1 \in [-1, 0)$$

The point is on average closer to a competing cluster than to its own. This strongly suggests it has been assigned to the wrong cluster. The more extreme the mismatch, the closer $s(i)$ is to -1.
</DerivationStep>
</DerivationSteps>

### Overall silhouette score

$$S = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} s(i) \in [-1,\, 1]$$

| Silhouette Score | Interpretation |
|-----------------|----------------|
| 0.71 – 1.00 | Strong cluster structure |
| 0.51 – 0.70 | Reasonable cluster structure |
| 0.26 – 0.50 | Weak structure; may be artificial |
| 0.00 – 0.25 | No substantial structure |
| < 0 | Points likely in wrong cluster |

### Worked example — four points

Two clusters on a line:
- Cluster A: $x_1 = (0,0)$, $x_2 = (1,0)$
- Cluster B: $x_3 = (5,0)$, $x_4 = (6,0)$

For $x_1$:

| Step | Computation | Value |
|------|------------|-------|
| $a(x_1)$ | $d(x_1, x_2) = 1$ | 1 |
| $b(x_1)$ | $(d(x_1,x_3) + d(x_1,x_4))/2 = (5+6)/2$ | 5.5 |
| $s(x_1)$ | $(5.5-1)/\max(5.5,1) = 4.5/5.5$ | **0.818** |

Full table:

| Point | Cluster | a(i) | b(i) | s(i) |
|-------|---------|------|------|------|
| $x_1 = (0,0)$ | A | 1 | 5.5 | 0.818 |
| $x_2 = (1,0)$ | A | 1 | 4.5 | 0.778 |
| $x_3 = (5,0)$ | B | 1 | 4.5 | 0.778 |
| $x_4 = (6,0)$ | B | 1 | 5.5 | 0.818 |

Overall $S = (0.818 + 0.778 + 0.778 + 0.818)/4 = 0.798$ — two tight, well-separated clusters.

## 3. Silhouette Plots

A silhouette plot is a horizontal bar chart where each bar represents one data point. The width of each bar equals $s(i)$. Bars pointing right (positive) belong to well-clustered points; bars pointing left (negative) flag misclustered points.

<ConceptBox title="Reading a silhouette plot">
- **Cluster thickness** — the vertical extent of each cluster section shows cluster size. Balanced clustering means sections of similar thickness.
- **Bar length** — uniformly long positive bars within a section mean all points are confidently assigned. Short or uneven bars suggest the cluster is fuzzy or overlapping another.
- **Negative bars** — any bar extending left signals a point that is closer to a different cluster than its own. These are candidates for reassignment.
- **Average line** — a dashed vertical line marks the overall mean silhouette. For good clusterings, most bars exceed this line. If many bars fall below it, K is probably wrong.
</ConceptBox>

## 4. Davies-Bouldin Index

Davies and Bouldin (1979) proposed a complementary metric that measures the average "worst-case" cluster overlap. Unlike the silhouette score, it operates on cluster-level summaries rather than individual points, making it computationally cheaper.

**Scatter within cluster k:**

$$s_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|$$

**Similarity between clusters i and j:**

$$R_{ij} = \frac{s_i + s_j}{d(\mu_i,\, \mu_j)}$$

$R_{ij}$ is large when either scatter is large (fuzzy clusters) or when the centroid distance is small (close clusters) — precisely the bad clustering scenario.

**Davies-Bouldin index:**

$$DB = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \max_{j \neq k}\, R_{kj}$$

**Lower DB is better.** Unlike the silhouette score, DB is not bounded above. Only compare DB values within the same dataset.

## 5. Calinski-Harabasz Index

The Calinski-Harabasz index (1974), also called the Variance Ratio Criterion, computes the ratio of *between-cluster variance* to *within-cluster variance* — structurally identical to the F-statistic in one-way ANOVA.

$$CH(K) = \frac{\text{BCSS} / (K-1)}{\text{WCSS} / (n - K)}$$

where $\text{BCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} |C_k| \cdot \|\mu_k - \mu\|^2$ measures how far cluster centroids are from the global mean.

**Higher CH is better.** For some datasets, CH increases monotonically with K even when adding clusters is not meaningful — use it comparatively within the same dataset, not as an absolute measure.

## 6. Practical Guide: Choosing K

No single metric definitively identifies the correct K. The standard practice is to use several metrics together and look for agreement.

| Method | Measures | Optimum | Handles non-spherical? |
|--------|---------|---------|----------------------|
| WCSS (Elbow) | Cohesion only | Elbow kink | No |
| Gap Statistic | Cohesion vs. random | Max gap | Better |
| Silhouette | Cohesion + separation | Max mean s | Yes |
| Davies-Bouldin | Worst-case overlap | Min DB | Partly |
| Calinski-Harabasz | BCSS/WCSS ratio | Max CH | Partly |

**Recommended workflow:**
1. Run K-means for K = 2 to 10 with multiple restarts (n_init ≥ 10).
2. Plot the elbow curve; note the approximate elbow.
3. Compute mean silhouette for each K; note the maximum.
4. Compare with DB and CH if available.
5. Choose the K where at least 2–3 metrics agree.
6. Plot silhouette charts for your top 2–3 candidates and inspect visually.
