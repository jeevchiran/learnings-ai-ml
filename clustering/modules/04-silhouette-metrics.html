Module 4: Silhouette Score and Evaluation Metrics

Why WCSS Is Not Enough

Module 3 introduced Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) as the objective K-means minimises. WCSS measures cohesion — how tightly the points within each cluster pack around their centroid. A smaller WCSS means each cluster is internally compact. That is a good property. But it is not the only property a useful clustering should have.

Consider what WCSS cannot see: separation. Two clusters can each be individually tight while sitting right next to each other, overlapping at their boundaries. A student looking at that clustering would say it is bad — the groups are not meaningfully distinct. Yet WCSS might be low, reporting a falsely optimistic score.

The blind spot of WCSS WCSS only looks inside each cluster. It has no term that penalises clusters for being close to one another. A partition with tight clusters pressed against each other will earn a low WCSS, but it fails to answer the more important question: are the clusters well-separated from one another?

Two concrete failure modes illustrate why we need something more:

Case A: Tight but too close

Two elongated clusters each have very low internal scatter, so WCSS is small. But they nearly touch. A point near the boundary could plausibly belong to either cluster. Cohesion is high; separation is poor.

Case B: Overlapping blobs

Two clusters overlap substantially in the middle. Points in the overlap region are equidistant from both centroids. WCSS may be similar to Case A, yet intuitively the clustering is worse — many points are ambiguously assigned.

Both cases might produce almost identical WCSS values, yet both are poor clusterings for different reasons. We need a metric that captures cohesion AND separation simultaneously. That metric is the silhouette score.

The key insight. A good clustering places each point closer to the members of its own cluster than to the members of any other cluster — by a wide margin. The silhouette score quantifies exactly this margin, for every single point in the dataset, and then averages them into a single interpretable number.

The Silhouette Score — Derivation

The silhouette coefficient was introduced by Peter Rousseeuw in 1987. It assigns every data point a score between -1 and 1 measuring how well it sits inside its assigned cluster relative to alternative clusters. Here is the derivation from first principles.

Step 1 — Intra-cluster distance a(i)

For point x_i assigned to cluster C_k, define the mean distance to all other members of its own cluster:

a(i) = \frac{1}{|C_k| - 1} \sum_{j \in C_k,\, j \neq i} d(x_i,\, x_j)

The denominator is |C_k| - 1, not |C_k|, because we exclude point i itself. This quantity measures cohesion: how tightly point i belongs to its cluster. A small a(i) means the point is deep inside its cluster; a large a(i) means it is on the fringe.

Step 2 — Nearest-cluster distance b(i)

For each cluster C_{k'} that does not contain point i, compute the mean distance from x_i to all points in C_{k'}:

b(i) = \min_{k' \neq k} \frac{1}{|C_{k'}|} \sum_{j \in C_{k'}} d(x_i,\, x_j)

We take the minimum over all competing clusters — this identifies the nearest other cluster to point i. That nearest other cluster is the most plausible alternative home for point i. A large b(i) means the nearest competitor is far away, so the current assignment is well-justified.

Step 3 — The silhouette score for point i

s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i),\, b(i))}

The numerator b(i) - a(i) is positive when b(i) > a(i), meaning the point is closer to its own cluster than to the best competitor. The denominator normalises by the larger of the two values, mapping the result into [-1, 1].

Derivation of the range

1
Case a(i) < b(i) — correctly clustered
Here \max(a, b) = b, so:
s(i) = \frac{b - a}{b} = 1 - \frac{a}{b} \in (0, 1]
The closer a is to 0 (perfectly tight cluster) and the larger b is (far competitor), the nearer s(i) is to 1. Equality s(i) = 1 requires a(i) = 0, i.e., the point is at the exact centroid of a one-member cluster.
2
Case a(i) = b(i) — boundary point
The numerator is zero:
s(i) = \frac{b - a}{\max(a, b)} = \frac{0}{\max(a, a)} = 0
The point is equidistant from its own cluster and the nearest competitor. It sits exactly on the decision boundary and could reasonably belong to either cluster.
3
Case a(i) > b(i) — possibly misclustered
Here \max(a, b) = a, so:
s(i) = \frac{b - a}{a} = \frac{b}{a} - 1 \in [-1, 0)
The point is on average closer to a competing cluster than to its own. This strongly suggests it has been assigned to the wrong cluster. The more extreme the mismatch, the closer s(i) is to -1.

Overall silhouette score

The overall silhouette score for a clustering is the mean over all n points:

S = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} s(i) \in [-1,\, 1]

Higher is better. A score above 0.7 typically indicates strong cluster structure; a score below 0.2 suggests weak or no meaningful structure.

What silhouette answers that WCSS cannot The silhouette score answers a question that WCSS cannot: is this point happier in its current cluster, or would it be happier elsewhere? It combines both cohesion and separation into a single [-1, 1] number per point. A cluster where most points have high positive silhouette values is genuinely well-formed — not just internally tight, but also clearly distinct from neighbouring clusters.

Worked example — four points

Consider two clusters on the number line:

  • Cluster A: x_1 = (0,0), x_2 = (1,0)
  • Cluster B: x_3 = (5,0), x_4 = (6,0)

We compute the silhouette score for x_1 step by step:

1
Compute a(x1) — intra-cluster distance
Cluster A has only one other point, x_2 = (1,0). With a single neighbour the mean is just that one distance:
a(x_1) = d(x_1, x_2) = \sqrt{(1-0)^2 + 0^2} = 1
2
Compute b(x1) — nearest other cluster distance
The only other cluster is Cluster B = {x3, x4}:
b(x_1) = \frac{d(x_1, x_3) + d(x_1, x_4)}{2} = \frac{5 + 6}{2} = 5.5
3
Compute s(x1)
s(x_1) = \frac{b - a}{\max(a, b)} = \frac{5.5 - 1}{\max(5.5,\, 1)} = \frac{4.5}{5.5} \approx 0.818
x_1 is well-clustered: it sits much closer to its cluster partner than to Cluster B.

By symmetry, all four points produce similar values. The complete picture:

PointClustera(i)b(i)s(i)
x_1 = (0,0)A15.50.818
x_2 = (1,0)A14.50.778
x_3 = (5,0)B14.50.778
x_4 = (6,0)B15.50.818

Overall silhouette score:

S = \frac{0.818 + 0.778 + 0.778 + 0.818}{4} = 0.798

This confirms what the eye can see: two tight, well-separated clusters.

Silhouette Plots

A silhouette plot is a horizontal bar chart where each bar represents one data point. The width of each bar equals the silhouette value s(i) for that point. Bars pointing right (positive) belong to well-clustered points; bars pointing left (negative) flag misclustered points. Points are grouped by cluster and sorted within each cluster by their silhouette value, so the overall shape of each cluster section is immediately visible.

Reading a silhouette plot

  • Cluster thickness — the vertical extent of each cluster section shows cluster size. Balanced clustering means sections of similar thickness.
  • Bar length — uniformly long positive bars within a section mean all points are confidently assigned. Short or uneven bars suggest the cluster is fuzzy or overlapping another.
  • Negative bars — any bar extending left signals a point that is closer to a different cluster than its own. These are candidates for reassignment.
  • Average line — a dashed vertical line marks the overall mean silhouette. For good clusterings, most bars exceed this line. If many bars fall below it, K is probably wrong.

Using silhouette plots to choose K

The most powerful application of silhouette plots is K selection. Run the plot for several values of K and look for:

  • The K that maximises the mean silhouette score (the dashed line furthest to the right)
  • No wide negative-bar regions — even a high mean silhouette can mask a bad cluster if one cluster has many negative points
  • Cluster sections of broadly similar thickness — very unequal sizes often indicate over- or under-clustering

The K satisfying all three criteria is the preferred choice. The interactive widget below lets you explore this directly.

Davies-Bouldin Index

Davies and Bouldin (1979) proposed a complementary metric that measures the average "worst-case" cluster overlap. Unlike the silhouette score, it operates on cluster-level summaries rather than individual points, making it computationally cheaper.

Scatter within a cluster

For cluster k with centroid \mu_k, define the scatter as the mean distance from each point to the centroid:

s_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|

A small scatter means the cluster is compact. This is the cluster-level analogue of a(i) from the silhouette score.

Similarity between two clusters

The similarity between clusters i and j is:

R_{ij} = \frac{s_i + s_j}{d(\mu_i,\, \mu_j)}
Key insight: when is Rij large? R_{ij} is large when either scatter is large (fuzzy clusters) or when the centroid distance is small (close clusters) — or both. This is precisely the bad clustering scenario: scattered clusters that are close together. The Davies-Bouldin index penalises exactly the situations a human eye would call poor.

Davies-Bouldin index

For each cluster k, find the worst similarity to any other cluster. Average over all clusters:

DB = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} \max_{j \neq k}\, R_{kj}

Lower DB is better. Unlike the silhouette score, DB is not bounded above — its absolute value depends on the scale of the data. It should only be used for comparison within the same dataset (e.g., comparing K = 3 vs K = 4 on the same points). Never compare DB values across different datasets.

Calinski-Harabasz Index

The Calinski-Harabasz index (1974), also called the Variance Ratio Criterion, takes a different approach. Rather than measuring individual point-level fit or worst-case cluster pairs, it computes the ratio of between-cluster variance to within-cluster variance. This is structurally identical to the F-statistic in one-way ANOVA.

Between-cluster sum of squares (BCSS)

Let \mu be the global mean of all points. BCSS measures how far each cluster centroid is from the global mean, weighted by cluster size:

\text{BCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} |C_k| \cdot \|\mu_k - \mu\|^2

Large BCSS means the clusters are well-spread out from the global centre — i.e., good separation.

Calinski-Harabasz index

CH(K) = \frac{\text{BCSS} / (K-1)}{\text{WCSS} / (n - K)}

The denominators are degrees-of-freedom corrections (analogous to mean squares in ANOVA). Higher CH is better — it means between-cluster variance is large relative to within-cluster variance, i.e., clusters are both separated and compact.

Monotonicity caveat For some datasets, CH increases monotonically with K even when adding clusters is not meaningful. Like WCSS, use it comparatively across K values, not as an absolute measure. Silhouette score is generally more robust for K selection because it accounts for both cohesion and separation without degeneracy at large K.

Practical Guide: Choosing K

No single metric definitively identifies the correct K. The standard practice is to use several metrics together and look for agreement. Here is a comparison of the main methods:

Method Measures Optimum Cost Works for non-spherical?
WCSS (Elbow) Cohesion only Elbow kink O(K · n · iter) No
Gap Statistic Cohesion vs. random Max gap O(B · K · n · iter) Better
Silhouette Cohesion + separation Max score O(K · n²) Partial
Davies-Bouldin Cohesion + separation Min value O(K · n) Partial
Calinski-Harabasz Between vs. within Max value O(K · n) No

Recommended workflow

1
Scale features first
Euclidean distance (and therefore all these metrics) is sensitive to the scale of each feature. Standardise features to zero mean and unit variance before running K-means. A feature with range [0, 10000] will dominate a feature with range [0, 1] otherwise.
2
Run K-means for K = 1, 2, ..., Kmax
Try K from 2 to 10-15. For each K, run multiple random restarts (at least 10) and keep the solution with the lowest WCSS to avoid poor local minima.
3
Compute the Elbow curve
Plot WCSS vs K. Look for the "elbow" — the K beyond which WCSS decreases only marginally. This gives a rough plausible range (often 2-4 values).
4
Compute silhouette scores and plots
For each K in the rough range, compute the mean silhouette score and inspect the silhouette plot. Look for the K with the highest mean silhouette and no wide negative-bar regions.
5
Choose K with highest mean silhouette
Among the candidates from step 4, prefer the K that balances all three criteria: high mean silhouette, no negative outlier regions, and approximately balanced cluster sizes.
6
Validate with domain knowledge
A statistically justified K must also make sense in context. Three customer segments that map to recognisable behavioural groups are more valuable than five statistically clean segments that nobody can interpret or act on.
No metric is definitive The best K is both mathematically justified AND domain-meaningful. Metrics guide the search; domain knowledge confirms the answer. When the metrics disagree (which they often do), silhouette score is generally the most reliable starting point because it captures both cohesion and separation without degeneracy.

Practice

Problem 1: Computing Silhouette by Hand

Easy

Point x = (3, 0) belongs to Cluster A = {(2,0), (4,0)} and the nearest other cluster is Cluster B = {(8,0), (10,0)}.

(a) Compute a(x): the mean distance from x to all other points in Cluster A.

(b) Compute b(x): the mean distance from x to all points in Cluster B.

(c) Compute s(x).

(d) Interpret the result: is x well-clustered?

(a) Intra-cluster distance a(x):

a(x) = \frac{d(x,(2,0)) + d(x,(4,0))}{2} = \frac{|3-2| + |3-4|}{2} = \frac{1 + 1}{2} = 1

(b) Nearest-cluster distance b(x):

b(x) = \frac{d(x,(8,0)) + d(x,(10,0))}{2} = \frac{|3-8| + |3-10|}{2} = \frac{5 + 7}{2} = 6

(c) Silhouette score s(x):

s(x) = \frac{b - a}{\max(a,b)} = \frac{6 - 1}{\max(6, 1)} = \frac{5}{6} \approx 0.833

(d) Interpretation: s(x) = 0.833 is close to 1, placing this point in the "strong cluster structure" range. Point x is well-clustered — it sits much closer to its two cluster partners (mean distance 1) than to the nearest competing cluster (mean distance 6). The assignment is clearly correct.

Problem 2: Comparing Metrics Across Clusterings

Medium

Two competing clusterings of the same 6 points are evaluated:

  • Clustering A: K = 2, mean silhouette = 0.62, DB index = 0.8
  • Clustering B: K = 3, mean silhouette = 0.74, DB index = 0.5

(a) Which clustering does the silhouette score prefer?

(b) Which clustering does the Davies-Bouldin index prefer?

(c) Describe a realistic scenario where K = 3 is genuinely better than K = 2, even though adding a cluster increases model complexity.

(a) Silhouette prefers Clustering B (K=3). The mean silhouette is 0.74 vs 0.62. For silhouette, higher is better. Clustering B's score falls in the "strong structure" range (>0.7), while Clustering A's score falls in the "reasonable" range. The improvement is substantial — B's clusters are meaningfully better separated and more cohesive.

(b) Davies-Bouldin also prefers Clustering B (K=3). DB = 0.5 vs 0.8. For DB, lower is better. A lower DB means clusters are more compact relative to the distances between them. Both metrics agree here, which increases confidence in the K=3 solution.

(c) Scenario where K=3 is genuinely better: Suppose the 6 points form three natural groups — for example, customers with low, medium, and high spending — but two of the groups are closer to each other than either is to the third. With K=2, the algorithm merges the low and medium groups into one cluster. That cluster spans two genuinely different behaviours, so points on each side feel weakly affiliated, producing lower silhouette. With K=3, each customer type is assigned its own centroid; intra-cluster distances are short, inter-cluster distances are large, and both silhouette and DB improve. The correct K here is 3, not 2, even though 3 is "more complex".

Problem 3: Proving the Range of s(i)

Hard

Prove that s(i) \in [-1, 1] for all data points i, where s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))} and a(i), b(i) \geq 0.

(a) Show s(i) \leq 1 always. When does equality hold?

(b) Show s(i) \geq -1 always. When does equality hold?

(c) Show s(i) = 0 when a(i) = b(i). Interpret geometrically.

(d) For a perfectly clustered dataset where all points are exactly at their centroid and clusters do not overlap, what does s(i) approach as cluster size n \to \infty?

(a) Proof that s(i) ≤ 1:

We need b - a \leq \max(a, b). Since \max(a,b) \geq b always, and a \geq 0, we have b - a \leq b \leq \max(a, b). Therefore s(i) = (b-a)/\max(a,b) \leq 1.

Equality holds when a = 0 and b > 0. Geometrically, a(i) = 0 means point i is the sole member of its cluster (no other points to measure distance to, so the empty mean is defined as 0), or in the limit, all other cluster members are coincident with i.

(b) Proof that s(i) ≥ -1:

We need b - a \geq -\max(a,b), i.e., b + \max(a,b) \geq a. Since \max(a,b) \geq a, we have b + \max(a,b) \geq b + a \geq a (as b \geq 0). Therefore s(i) \geq -1.

Equality holds when b = 0 and a > 0. This means the nearest other cluster has all its points coincident with i itself (zero distance), while the point's own cluster is spread away from it. This is the worst possible misclustering: the point is sitting exactly on top of another cluster's members.

(c) When a(i) = b(i):

s(i) = \frac{b - a}{\max(a, b)} = \frac{0}{\max(a, a)} = 0

Geometrically, s(i) = 0 means point i lies on the decision boundary between its own cluster and the nearest competing cluster. It is equidistant from both in the mean sense. Reassigning it to the competing cluster would produce an equally valid (or invalid) clustering — the algorithm has no strong preference, and the point provides no evidence for either cluster assignment.

(d) Perfect clustering as n → ∞:

If all points are exactly at their centroid and clusters do not overlap, then for any point i: a(i) = 0 (all cluster partners are at the same position) and b(i) = d(\mu_k, \mu_{k'}) for the nearest centroid (positive, since clusters do not overlap). Therefore:

s(i) = \frac{b(i) - 0}{\max(0, b(i))} = \frac{b(i)}{b(i)} = 1

As n \to \infty, with points drawn independently from the same position (a degenerate distribution), a(i) \to 0 and s(i) \to 1. The silhouette score approaches its maximum value of 1 for a perfectly separated, perfectly cohesive clustering.
