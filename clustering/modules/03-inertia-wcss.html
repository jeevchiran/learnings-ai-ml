Module 3: Inertia (WCSS) and the Elbow Method

What Does K-Means Actually Optimise?

Building on Module 2: We saw that updating a centroid to the arithmetic mean of its assigned points minimises the sum of squared distances within that cluster. K-means runs this logic across all K clusters simultaneously. The single number it is driving toward zero is the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) — also called inertia in scikit-learn.

Formally, if we have K clusters C_1, \ldots, C_K with centroids \mu_1, \ldots, \mu_K, the WCSS objective is:

\text{WCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2

Each term \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2 is the squared Euclidean distance from data point x_i to its cluster centroid \mu_k. Summing across all points gives the total "spread" or compactness within clusters. We want this number to be small — tight, cohesive clusters have low WCSS.

Expanding the squared norm into individual feature dimensions gives an equivalent form:

\text{WCSS} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i \in C_k} \sum_{j=1}^{p} (x_{ij} - \mu_{kj})^2

where x_{ij} is the j-th feature of point i and \mu_{kj} is the j-th coordinate of centroid k. Both forms are identical — the first is compact notation, the second makes the feature-by-feature sum explicit.

Key Intuition

WCSS measures how "spread out" points are within their assigned clusters. A WCSS of zero would mean every point sits exactly on its centroid — perfect compactness. Real datasets always have positive WCSS. K-means tries to find the assignment and centroid positions that make WCSS as small as possible.

K-Means as Coordinate Descent

K-means alternates between two steps. The key insight is that each step is guaranteed to decrease (or leave unchanged) the WCSS. This makes the algorithm a special case of coordinate descent — optimising over two sets of variables by alternating.

Assignment Step

For fixed centroids \mu_1,\ldots,\mu_K, assign each point to its nearest centroid:

c_i = \arg\min_{k} \;\|x_i - \mu_k\|^2

This is optimal: any other assignment would place point x_i in a cluster whose centroid is farther away, increasing WCSS. The assignment step minimises WCSS with respect to the cluster labels, holding centroids fixed.

Update Step

For fixed assignments C_1,\ldots,C_K, update each centroid to the mean of its assigned points:

\mu_k = \frac{1}{|C_k|} \sum_{i \in C_k} x_i

From Module 2, the arithmetic mean is the unique minimiser of \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_k\|^2. The update step minimises WCSS with respect to the centroids, holding assignments fixed.

Why the Algorithm Converges

1
WCSS is bounded below
WCSS is a sum of squared distances, so WCSS ≥ 0 always.
2
Assignment step is non-increasing
WCSS(new assignments, old centroids) ≤ WCSS(old assignments, old centroids). Each point is reassigned to the nearest centroid, which can only reduce or maintain its contribution.
3
Update step is non-increasing
WCSS(new assignments, new centroids) ≤ WCSS(new assignments, old centroids). The mean minimises within-cluster sum of squares, so moving to the mean can only decrease WCSS.
4
Convergence is guaranteed
WCSS is non-increasing at every step and bounded below by zero. The number of distinct cluster assignments is finite (at most K^n for n points). Therefore the algorithm must visit each assignment at most once before repeating — and since WCSS is non-increasing, it can never revisit an assignment with lower WCSS. The algorithm converges in a finite number of iterations.
Important Caveat: Local Minima Coordinate descent finds a local minimum of WCSS, not the global minimum. The global optimisation of WCSS over all possible assignments is NP-hard — there is no known polynomial-time algorithm. Different random initialisations can lead to very different solutions. Multiple random restarts (running K-means several times with different starting points and keeping the run with lowest WCSS) are standard practice. K-means++ initialisation reduces but does not eliminate this problem.

Properties of WCSS

WCSS ≥ 0 always

WCSS is a sum of squared terms, so it is always non-negative. It equals zero only when every point coincides exactly with its centroid — impossible with real, distinct data.

WCSS decreases as K increases

With K = n (one cluster per point), WCSS = 0. This monotone decrease means WCSS alone cannot determine the right K — it always favours more clusters.

WCSS depends on scale

If all feature values are multiplied by 2, all squared distances increase by 4×, so WCSS increases by 4×. Always standardise features before comparing WCSS values.

Not comparable across datasets

A WCSS of 100 on dataset A and 100 on dataset B is meaningless for comparison — the datasets may have completely different scales and point densities.

Formal Argument: WCSS(K+1) ≤ WCSS(K)

Given any K-clustering, we can construct a valid (K+1)-clustering with WCSS no larger. Split any cluster C_k into two non-empty subsets C_k^L and C_k^R. The sub-cluster means \mu_{C_k^L} and \mu_{C_k^R} are better fits for their respective points than the original centroid \mu_{C_k}. Formally:

\sum_{i \in C_k^L} \|x_i - \mu_{C_k^L}\|^2 + \sum_{i \in C_k^R} \|x_i - \mu_{C_k^R}\|^2 \;\leq\; \sum_{i \in C_k} \|x_i - \mu_{C_k}\|^2

This holds because the mean minimises the sum of squared distances. The means of the sub-clusters are chosen to minimise their own within-cluster sums, whereas the original centroid \mu_{C_k} is not in general optimal for either subset alone. Summing over the remaining unchanged clusters gives WCSS(K+1) ≤ WCSS(K).

The Elbow Method

Since WCSS always decreases as K increases, plotting WCSS vs. K alone does not identify the correct number of clusters. The Elbow Method addresses this by looking at the rate of decrease: plot WCSS against K and look for a "kink" — the point where the marginal improvement from adding one more cluster drops sharply (like the bend of an arm at the elbow).

Intuition

Suppose the data has K^* natural clusters. When K < K*, we are forcing genuinely different groups to share a centroid — each new cluster we add separates a real group, producing a large drop in WCSS. When K > K*, we are splitting clusters that are already tight and well-separated — the drop in WCSS per additional cluster is small. The transition point is the elbow.

Limitations of the Elbow Method
  1. Ambiguity: The WCSS curve is often smooth without a clear kink. Many practitioners disagree on where the elbow is for the same chart.
  2. Subjectivity: The method requires a visual judgment call; it is not automated or objective.
  3. Fails for non-spherical clusters: When clusters have very different sizes or densities, the elbow can be misleading.
  4. Measures cohesion only: Clusters may be internally compact but heavily overlapping. WCSS does not measure separation between clusters.
  5. Computational cost: You must run K-means for every K from 1 to Kmax, and ideally with multiple restarts for each K.

The Gap Statistic

Tibshirani, Walther, and Hastie (2001) proposed a more principled approach: compare the observed log-WCSS to the expected log-WCSS under a null reference distribution — data with no cluster structure at all.

\text{Gap}(K) = \mathbb{E}^*\!\left[\log\bigl(\text{WCSS}_{\text{ref}}(K)\bigr)\right] - \log\bigl(\text{WCSS}(K)\bigr)

When the data has genuine cluster structure, the observed WCSS is much lower than we would expect from uniformly random data, so the gap is large. When there is no structure, both terms are similar and the gap is near zero.

Choosing K with the Gap Statistic

Choose the smallest K such that the gap at K is not substantially smaller than the gap at K+1, accounting for estimation uncertainty. Formally, choose:

K^* = \min\bigl\{K : \text{Gap}(K) \geq \text{Gap}(K+1) - s_{K+1}\bigr\}

where s_{K+1} is the standard deviation of the reference log-WCSS estimates at K+1. This "one-standard-error rule" guards against selecting an unnecessarily large K.

Implementation

  1. Run K-means on the actual data for each K; record \log(\text{WCSS}(K)).
  2. Generate B reference datasets (e.g., B = 10–20) sampled uniformly from the bounding box of the data.
  3. Run K-means on each reference dataset for each K; record \log(\text{WCSS}_b^{\text{ref}}(K)).
  4. Compute \mathbb{E}^*[\log(\text{WCSS}_\text{ref}(K))] as the mean over B reference runs, and s_{K} as the standard deviation.
  5. Evaluate the gap criterion to select K*.
Advantage Over the Elbow Method The gap statistic can detect when the data has no cluster structure at all: if Gap(K) ≈ 0 for all K, the observed WCSS is no lower than random, indicating no meaningful clusters. The elbow method gives an answer even when the data is completely uniform — the gap statistic does not.

Interactive: Within-Cluster vs. Between-Cluster

Total variance in the dataset can be decomposed into two parts:

\text{TSS} = \text{WCSS} + \text{BCSS}

where TSS (Total Sum of Squares) = \sum_i \|x_i - \mu_{\text{global}}\|^2 is the total spread of all points around the global mean — a constant that does not depend on K. BCSS (Between-Cluster Sum of Squares) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} |C_k| \cdot \|\mu_k - \mu_{\text{global}}\|^2 measures how far cluster centroids are from the global mean, weighted by cluster size.

As K increases: WCSS falls, BCSS rises, TSS stays constant. A good K simultaneously minimises WCSS (tight clusters) and maximises BCSS (clusters far from each other). The stacked bar chart below makes this trade-off visible.

Problem 1: Manual WCSS Calculation

Easy

A dataset has 6 points partitioned into 2 clusters:

  • Cluster 1: (1,2), (2,3), (3,2) — centroid = (2, 7/3)
  • Cluster 2: (7,8), (8,7), (9,8) — centroid = (8, 23/3)

Compute the WCSS. Show all squared distance calculations step by step.

Cluster 1 contributions:

Centroid \mu_1 = (2,\; 7/3).

  • d^2\bigl((1,2),\,(2,7/3)\bigr) = (1-2)^2 + (2-7/3)^2 = 1 + (-1/3)^2 = 1 + 1/9 \approx 1.111
  • d^2\bigl((2,3),\,(2,7/3)\bigr) = (2-2)^2 + (3-7/3)^2 = 0 + (2/3)^2 = 4/9 \approx 0.444
  • d^2\bigl((3,2),\,(2,7/3)\bigr) = (3-2)^2 + (2-7/3)^2 = 1 + 1/9 \approx 1.111

Cluster 1 WCSS: 1 + 1/9 + 4/9 + 1 + 1/9 = 2 + 6/9 = 2\tfrac{2}{3} \approx 2.667

Cluster 2 contributions:

Centroid \mu_2 = (8,\; 23/3). By symmetry of the point configuration, the within-cluster sum is identical: Cluster 2 WCSS ≈ 2.667.

Total WCSS:

\text{WCSS} = 2 \times \frac{8}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \approx 5.333

Problem 3: Proof that WCSS(K+1) ≤ WCSS(K)

Hard

Prove rigorously that for any dataset and any K, the optimal WCSS with K+1 clusters is no greater than the optimal WCSS with K clusters.

Hint: Given an optimal K-clustering, construct a valid (K+1)-clustering with WCSS ≤ WCSS(K) by taking any cluster and splitting it into two half-clusters. Use the following identity (parallel axis theorem for centroid optimality): for any cluster with points \{x_i\}_{i \in C} and any split into subsets C^L, C^R,

\sum_{i \in C^L} \|x_i - \mu_{C^L}\|^2 + \sum_{i \in C^R} \|x_i - \mu_{C^R}\|^2 \;\leq\; \sum_{i \in C} \|x_i - \mu_C\|^2

Then discuss: does this prove K = n always minimises WCSS? What does this imply for choosing K by WCSS alone?

Construction:

Let \mathcal{C}^* = \{C_1^*, \ldots, C_K^*\} be an optimal K-clustering with WCSS = W*. Pick any cluster, say C_1^*. Partition C_1^* into two non-empty subsets C_1^L and C_1^R (e.g., split by the median of any feature). Define a (K+1)-clustering: \{C_1^L, C_1^R, C_2^*, \ldots, C_K^*\}.

Key inequality:

For any cluster C with centroid \mu_C and split into C^L, C^R with centroids \mu_L, \mu_R:

\sum_{i \in C^L} \|x_i - \mu_L\|^2 + \sum_{i \in C^R} \|x_i - \mu_R\|^2 \;\leq\; \sum_{i \in C^L} \|x_i - \mu_C\|^2 + \sum_{i \in C^R} \|x_i - \mu_C\|^2 = \sum_{i \in C} \|x_i - \mu_C\|^2

The inequality holds because \mu_L is the minimiser of \sum_{i \in C^L}\|x_i - \mu\|^2 over all \mu, so using \mu_C instead can only give a larger or equal value. Similarly for \mu_R.

Completing the proof:

WCSS of the (K+1)-clustering satisfies:

\text{WCSS}(K+1) \;\leq\; \left(\sum_{i \in C_1^L}\|x_i-\mu_L\|^2 + \sum_{i \in C_1^R}\|x_i-\mu_R\|^2\right) + \sum_{k=2}^{K}\sum_{i \in C_k^*}\|x_i-\mu_k^*\|^2 \;\leq\; W^* = \text{WCSS}(K)

(The first inequality uses the fact that the optimal (K+1)-clustering has WCSS no larger than any specific (K+1)-clustering we construct. The second uses the key inequality above applied to the split cluster, and the remaining terms are unchanged.)

Discussion:

Yes, this proves that K = n (one point per cluster) achieves WCSS = 0 and is the global minimiser. This is precisely why WCSS alone cannot be used to choose K: the best WCSS is always achieved by the trivially useless solution where every point is its own cluster. The correct K must balance low WCSS against parsimony — using far fewer clusters than data points. Metrics like the Gap Statistic or Silhouette Score incorporate this trade-off; WCSS with the Elbow Method uses the heuristic that the rate of improvement collapses at the "right" K.
