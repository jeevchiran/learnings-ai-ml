Module 3 — One-Sample Z-Test

Factory quality control. A cereal manufacturer programs its filling machines to deliver exactly 500 g per box. The production manager knows from years of historical data that the filling process has a population standard deviation of σ = 8 g — a quantity so well-established it can be treated as known. After a recent maintenance shutdown, she wants to verify the machine is still calibrated correctly. She randomly samples 40 boxes and finds a sample mean of x̅ = 497 g. Is this 3 g shortfall just random variation, or is the machine systematically underfilling? To answer this formally — with a controlled risk of being wrong — she needs the one-sample Z-test.

1. When to Use the One-Sample Z-Test

The one-sample Z-test is the appropriate procedure whenever you are comparing a single observed sample mean against a specific claimed or target value for the population mean. Before applying any statistical test, you must verify that the conditions for its validity are satisfied. For the Z-test, four conditions must hold simultaneously.

Condition 1 — Single sample vs. claimed value

You have one sample and one claimed population mean μ0. You are not comparing two groups; you are asking whether a single group's mean equals a specific target.

Condition 2 — Known population standard deviation

The population standard deviation σ must be known from prior study or long-run process data. When σ is unknown and must be estimated from the sample, the t-test applies instead.

Condition 3 — Random and independent observations

The sample must be drawn randomly from the population. Each observation must be independent of the others — knowing one value gives no information about any other value.

Condition 4 — Approximate normality of x̅

Either the population itself is normally distributed, or the sample size is at least 30. The Central Limit Theorem then guarantees that x̅ is approximately normal regardless of the population's shape.

The Central Limit Theorem guarantee

For large n (≥ 30), regardless of the underlying population shape — whether it is skewed, bimodal, or heavy-tailed — the sampling distribution of x̅ converges to a normal distribution with mean μ and standard error σ/√n. This is what makes the Z-test so broadly applicable. In the factory example, there is no requirement that individual box weights be perfectly normally distributed; 40 boxes is sufficient for the CLT to take effect. The population standard deviation σ = 8 g is known from long-run process records, so all four conditions are satisfied.

2. The Sampling Distribution of the Mean

Before deriving the Z-test statistic, it is essential to understand the object it is built from: the sampling distribution of the sample mean. This distribution describes not the data itself, but the behaviour of x̅ across all possible samples of size n drawn from the same population.

If the population random variable X is normally distributed with mean μ and variance σ2, then the sample mean x̅ computed from n independent observations is itself normally distributed:

\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu,\, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)

The standard deviation of this sampling distribution — called the standard error of the mean — is:

\text{SE}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}

The standard error is the single most important quantity in sampling theory. It captures how much the sample mean x̅ typically varies from one sample to the next, purely due to chance. Two features deserve emphasis. First, the standard error shrinks as n increases — larger samples produce more precise estimates of μ, so deviations from H0 become easier to detect. Second, the standard error is smaller than the population standard deviation σ by a factor of 1/\sqrt{n}. A single box weight might vary by 8 g, but the mean of 40 boxes varies by only 8/\sqrt{40} \approx 1.265 g. This narrowing of variability is why averages are far more informative than individual measurements.

3. Deriving the Z Test Statistic

The Z statistic is constructed by a single principle: if we assume H0 is true, what would the sampling distribution of x̅ look like, and how unusual is our observed value within that distribution? We derive it step by step.

1
State the null distribution. Under H0 (that is, assuming μ = μ0 is true), the sampling distribution of x̅ is:
\bar{X} \sim N\!\left(\mu_0,\, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)
This is the distribution we would expect to see if the null hypothesis were correct. All subsequent probability calculations are made relative to this distribution.
2
Standardize. To convert any normal distribution to the standard normal N(0, 1), subtract the mean and divide by the standard deviation. Applying this to x̅ under H0:
Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
This is the Z test statistic. Its construction is a direct application of the standardization formula you have seen with individual observations, but here applied to the sample mean.
3
Identify the null distribution of Z. Since we subtracted the null mean and divided by the null standard error, the resulting quantity follows a standard normal distribution exactly (when the population is normal) or approximately (by the Central Limit Theorem for large n):
Z \;\sim\; N(0,\,1) \quad \text{under } H_0
This is the key step that makes the Z-test tractable. We now have a single universal reference distribution — the standard normal — whose probabilities are fully tabulated and implemented in every statistical package.
4
Interpret the magnitude of Z. A large positive Z means x̅ is far above μ0; a large negative Z means x̅ is far below. A large |Z| in either direction means our observed sample mean would be very unlikely if H0 were true.
The Z statistic is measured in standard-error units. A value of Z = 2 means the sample mean is exactly 2 standard errors away from the null hypothesis value — an event that occurs only about 4.6% of the time under H0.
5
Connect Z to the p-value. The further |Z| is from 0, the smaller the p-value, and the stronger the evidence against H0. The p-value is simply the probability, computed under H0, of observing a Z as extreme as — or more extreme than — the one we computed from our data.
This connection means that rejecting H0 is equivalent to declaring that our observed Z is too far into the tail(s) of N(0,1) to be explained by chance alone.

Understanding the components

Every part of the Z statistic carries a distinct meaning. The numerator, \bar{x} - \mu_0, is the raw difference between what we observed and what H0 predicted. In the factory example this is 497 − 500 = −3 g. A difference of 3 g sounds small, but whether it is statistically significant depends entirely on how much variability we expect in x̅. The denominator, \sigma/\sqrt{n}, is that measure of variability — it tells us how much x̅ typically moves around from sample to sample when H0 is true. The ratio therefore expresses the observed gap in units of natural sampling variation: how many standard errors away from the null is our result?

4. The Full Step-by-Step Testing Procedure

A rigorous hypothesis test follows a fixed six-step protocol that must be completed in order. The sequence matters: the significance level α must be chosen before computing the test statistic, not after seeing the data.

1
State the hypotheses. The null hypothesis always specifies an exact value:
H_0: \mu = \mu_0
The alternative hypothesis specifies the direction of the departure you want to detect. There are three options: two-tailed (H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0), left-tailed (H_1: \mu < \mu_0), or right-tailed (H_1: \mu > \mu_0). The choice must be made based on the research question, not after looking at the data.
The factory manager suspects the machine may be filling incorrectly in either direction after maintenance, so a two-tailed test is appropriate.
2
Choose α and verify assumptions. Select the significance level (commonly 0.05 or 0.01) before computing any statistics. Verify that all four conditions for the Z-test are satisfied: known σ, random sample, independence, and approximate normality of x̅.
Checking assumptions is not a formality — applying the Z-test when σ is unknown or n is small and the population is skewed leads to invalid p-values.
3
Compute the Z test statistic.
z_{\text{obs}} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}}
This is a single number computed directly from your sample data.
All numerical arithmetic should be shown explicitly in practice, as we will do in the worked example. Rounding intermediate steps can introduce errors.
4
Compute the p-value (Method A) or critical value (Method B) — both methods always give the same decision.

Method A — p-value approach: Compute the probability of observing a Z at least as extreme as zobs under H0.

  • Left-tailed: p = P(Z < z_{\text{obs}}) = \Phi(z_{\text{obs}})
  • Right-tailed: p = P(Z > z_{\text{obs}}) = 1 - \Phi(z_{\text{obs}})
  • Two-tailed: p = 2 \times P(Z < -|z_{\text{obs}}|) = 2\,\Phi(-|z_{\text{obs}}|)
Reject H0 if p < α.

Method B — Critical value approach: Find the critical value z* that cuts off exactly α (or α/2 for two-tailed) in the tail(s).

  • Two-tailed: reject if |z_{\text{obs}}| > z_{\alpha/2}
  • Left-tailed: reject if z_{\text{obs}} < -z_{\alpha}
  • Right-tailed: reject if z_{\text{obs}} > z_{\alpha}
At α = 0.05 two-tailed, the critical values are ±1.96. At α = 0.01 two-tailed, they are ±2.576. These values are fixed by the standard normal distribution and do not depend on the data.
5
State the decision. Based on the comparison above, either:
  • Reject H0 — the data provide sufficient evidence against the null hypothesis at the chosen significance level.
  • Fail to Reject H0 — the data do not provide sufficient evidence against the null hypothesis. This is not the same as proving H0 is true.
Never say "accept H0." Failing to reject is a statement about insufficient evidence, not about truth. The absence of statistical significance does not imply equivalence to the null value.
6
Write a plain-English conclusion in context. Translate the statistical decision back into the language of the original problem. Your conclusion should mention the significance level, the direction of the effect, and the practical implication for the decision-maker.
A conclusion like "Reject H0" is meaningless to a production manager. She needs to know what the test implies about her machine. Every hypothesis test must end with a sentence that a non-statistician can act on.

5. Confidence Intervals and the Hypothesis Test Duality

A profound and practically useful connection exists between two-tailed hypothesis tests and confidence intervals: they are two sides of the same coin. A two-tailed Z-test at significance level α rejects H0: μ = μ0 if and only if μ0 falls outside the corresponding (1 − α) × 100% confidence interval for μ.

The (1 − α) × 100% confidence interval for μ when σ is known is:

\bar{X} \;\pm\; z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}

Applied to the factory scenario with x̅ = 497, σ = 8, n = 40, and z0.025 = 1.96:

497 \;\pm\; 1.96 \times \frac{8}{\sqrt{40}} \;=\; 497 \;\pm\; 2.48 \;=\; [494.52,\; 499.48]

Since the hypothesized value μ0 = 500 does not fall within the interval [494.52, 499.48], we reject H0 at the 5% significance level — fully consistent with the p-value calculation. The confidence interval additionally tells us which values of the population mean are plausible: they lie between roughly 494.5 g and 499.5 g. This is more informative than a simple reject/fail-to-reject decision, because it shows the direction and magnitude of the suspected discrepancy.

This duality is not a coincidence. Both the Z-test and the confidence interval are derived from the same sampling distribution of x̅ and the same standardization. The confidence interval inverts the hypothesis test: instead of asking "is this particular μ0 plausible?", it identifies the entire set of null hypotheses that would not be rejected at the given α.

7. Worked Example: The Factory Scenario

We now apply the six-step procedure to the cereal factory problem in full detail.

Given: μ0 = 500 g, x̅ = 497 g, σ = 8 g, n = 40, two-tailed test at α = 0.05.

1
Hypotheses.
H_0: \mu = 500 \qquad H_1: \mu \neq 500
We use a two-tailed test because the manager is concerned about the machine being off in either direction after maintenance — both overfilling and underfilling are problems.
2
Significance level and assumptions. α = 0.05. Checking: σ = 8 g is known from long-run process records (yes). Sample size n = 40 ≥ 30 (CLT applies, yes). The 40 boxes were randomly selected (yes). All four conditions are satisfied.
3
Compute the test statistic.
\text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{8}{\sqrt{40}} = \frac{8}{6.3246} \approx 1.265 \text{ g}
z_{\text{obs}} = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu_0}{\text{SE}} = \frac{497 - 500}{1.265} = \frac{-3}{1.265} \approx -2.372
The sample mean is approximately 2.37 standard errors below the null hypothesis value. Whether this constitutes strong evidence against H0 will be determined by the p-value and critical value comparisons.
4
p-value and critical value.

p-value method: For a two-tailed test, p = 2 \times \Phi(-|z_{\text{obs}}|) = 2 \times \Phi(-2.372). From standard normal tables, \Phi(-2.372) \approx 0.0088, giving:

p = 2 \times 0.0088 = 0.0176
Since 0.0176 < 0.05 = α, we reject H0.

Critical value method: At α = 0.05 two-tailed, z* = z0.025 = 1.96. Since |z_{\text{obs}}| = 2.372 > 1.96 = z^*, the test statistic falls in the rejection region. We reject H0.

Both methods yield the same decision, as they always must. The p-value of 0.0176 means that, if the machine were correctly calibrated at 500 g, we would observe a sample mean at least 3 g away from 500 g in a sample of 40 boxes about 1.76% of the time — well below our 5% threshold.
5
Decision. Reject H0 at α = 0.05
6
Conclusion in context. At the 5% significance level, there is sufficient statistical evidence that the filling machine's mean output differs from 500 g. The sample mean of 497 g represents a statistically significant departure from the target. The 95% confidence interval [494.52 g, 499.48 g] does not contain 500 g, confirming the conclusion. The production manager should halt production and recalibrate the machine before resuming operations.

Statistical power is the probability of correctly rejecting a false null hypothesis — it is 1 - \beta where \beta is the Type II error rate. The widget above illustrates a central principle of study design: for a fixed true effect size (the distance between μtrue and μ0), power increases monotonically with sample size. This is the quantitative justification for why researchers conduct power analyses before collecting data. A study designed with insufficient sample size may fail to detect a real and important effect, wasting resources and potentially reaching incorrect conclusions. Conventionally, studies aim for power of at least 0.80, meaning a maximum Type II error rate of 20%.

9. Practice Problem

A city traffic department claims that the average commute time on a major highway is 25 minutes. A concerned residents' group times 64 randomly selected commute trips and finds a sample mean of 27.4 minutes. Historical records show the standard deviation is σ = 9.6 minutes. Test at α = 0.01 whether the commute time differs from the claimed 25 minutes. Use both the p-value method and the critical value method.

Show full worked solution

Step 1 — Hypotheses.

H_0: \mu = 25 \qquad H_1: \mu \neq 25 \quad \text{(two-tailed)}

Step 2 — Assumptions and α. α = 0.01. Population σ = 9.6 min is known (given). Sample size n = 64 ≥ 30. Random sample stated. All conditions satisfied.

Step 3 — Test statistic.

\text{SE} = \frac{9.6}{\sqrt{64}} = \frac{9.6}{8} = 1.2 \text{ min}
z_{\text{obs}} = \frac{27.4 - 25}{1.2} = \frac{2.4}{1.2} = 2.00

Step 4A — p-value method.

p = 2 \times P(Z > 2.00) = 2 \times (1 - \Phi(2.00)) = 2 \times 0.0228 = 0.0456

Since p = 0.0456 > α = 0.01, we fail to reject H0.

Step 4B — Critical value method. At α = 0.01 two-tailed:

z^* = z_{0.005} = 2.576

Since |z_{\text{obs}}| = 2.00 < 2.576 = z^*, the test statistic does not fall in the rejection region. Fail to reject H0. Both methods agree.

Step 5 — Decision. Fail to Reject H0 at α = 0.01

Step 6 — Conclusion in context. At the 1% significance level, there is insufficient statistical evidence that the mean commute time on this highway differs from the claimed 25 minutes.

Important note on α: If we had used α = 0.05, we would have rejected H0 (since p = 0.0456 < 0.05). This example vividly illustrates why α must be chosen before data collection. Raising α after seeing the data to achieve a significant result is a form of p-hacking that inflates the true Type I error rate far above the nominal level. The threshold for what counts as "sufficient evidence" must be decided in advance based on the consequences of being wrong.
