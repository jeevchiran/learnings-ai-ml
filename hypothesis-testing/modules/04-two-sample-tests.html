Module 4: Two-Sample Tests and A/B Testing

Why does this matter? A marketing team at an e-commerce company wants to know which of two email subject lines drives more clicks. They randomly send version A to 200 subscribers and version B to another 200. Version A achieves a 12% click rate; version B achieves 15%. The difference is 3 percentage points. But could this difference have arisen by chance alone, even if both subject lines are equally effective?

To answer rigorously, the team runs a two-sample hypothesis test — the same statistical framework used in clinical trials, quality control, and academic research whenever we compare two groups. By the end of this module, you will know exactly how to set up and execute that test, interpret its output, and understand when a statistically significant result is also practically significant.

1. The Two-Sample Framework

In Module 3 we compared a single sample mean \bar{X} to a fixed reference value \mu_0. The one-sample Z-test asked: "Is the population mean equal to some pre-specified constant?" Two-sample tests shift this question fundamentally. Rather than anchoring to a fixed external value, we now compare the means of two independent populations directly against each other. The parameter of interest is no longer a single mean, but the difference in means: \mu_1 - \mu_2.

This framework is the backbone of experimental design. In a randomized controlled trial, group 1 is the treatment arm and group 2 is the control arm. In a product experiment, group 1 might see redesigned interface A and group 2 the original interface B. In quality control, group 1 might be units from machine line 1 and group 2 from machine line 2. In every case, the scientific question reduces to: is the difference \mu_1 - \mu_2 equal to zero (no effect), or does the data provide evidence that it differs from zero?

The general two-sample hypothesis structure is:

H_0 : \mu_1 - \mu_2 = \delta_0 \quad \text{(usually } \delta_0 = 0\text{, testing for no difference)}
H_1 : \mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq \delta_0 \quad \text{(or } > \delta_0 \text{ or } < \delta_0\text{)}

In the overwhelming majority of applied settings \delta_0 = 0, meaning we test for any difference at all. The test statistic follows the general principle established in earlier modules: it is the estimator minus the hypothesized value, divided by the standard error of the estimator:

\text{Test statistic} = \dfrac{(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) - \delta_0}{\text{SE}(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2)}

The critical new ingredient is computing the standard error \text{SE}(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2), which depends on what we know or assume about the population standard deviations. The rest of this module develops that computation for three practically important cases: the Z-test when \sigma_1 and \sigma_2 are known, Welch's t-test when they are unknown (the common case), and the two-proportion Z-test for binary outcomes like click rates.

Independence Assumption

Two-sample tests require the two groups to be independent. The observations in group 1 must have nothing to do with observations in group 2: knowing the value of any observation in group 1 must give you no information about any observation in group 2. In practice, this is guaranteed by random assignment — each subject is allocated to exactly one group, and the allocation of one subject does not influence another.

If this assumption is violated — for example, because the same subjects appear in both groups (before-and-after measurements, matched pairs, repeated measures) — the two-sample t-test is invalid. The appropriate method in that case is the paired t-test, covered in Module 5. Always verify independence before applying the methods in this module.

2. Two-Population Z-Test (Known Population Standard Deviations)

If both population standard deviations \sigma_1 and \sigma_2 are known, the analysis is exact. By the properties of independent normal random variables, the sampling distribution of the difference in sample means is:

\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2 \sim N\!\left(\mu_1 - \mu_2,\; \dfrac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}\right)

This result follows from the variance addition rule for independent random variables: \text{Var}(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}_1) + \text{Var}(\bar{X}_2) = \sigma_1^2/n_1 + \sigma_2^2/n_2. Standardizing under the null hypothesis H_0 : \mu_1 - \mu_2 = \delta_0 yields the two-sample Z statistic:

Z = \dfrac{(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) - \delta_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}}}

Under H_0 (and assuming the populations are normal, or that both sample sizes are large enough to invoke the Central Limit Theorem), Z \sim N(0,1). Decision rules are identical to the one-sample Z-test: reject if |Z| > z_{\alpha/2} for a two-tailed test, or if Z > z_\alpha (respectively Z < -z_\alpha) for right-tailed (respectively left-tailed) alternatives.

In practice, the population standard deviations are almost never known precisely. The two-sample Z-test is primarily a theoretical reference point and a pedagogical stepping stone to the t-test developed in the next section. It also arises naturally when analyzing proportions, as we will see in Section 4.

3. Two-Sample Independent t-Test (Unknown Standard Deviations)

In the realistic case where \sigma_1 and \sigma_2 are unknown, we substitute the sample standard deviations s_1 and s_2. This introduces additional uncertainty and the resulting test statistic no longer follows an exact standard normal distribution. Instead, it follows (approximately) a Student's t distribution.

Standard Error

The standard error of the difference in sample means is estimated as:

\text{SE}(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) = \sqrt{\dfrac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{s_2^2}{n_2}}

Welch's t Statistic

Named after Bernard Lewis Welch, who derived the approximation in 1947, the test statistic is:

t = \dfrac{(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) - \delta_0}{\sqrt{\dfrac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{s_2^2}{n_2}}}

Welch-Satterthwaite Degrees of Freedom

The t statistic above does not follow an exact Student's t distribution for any integer degrees of freedom. Welch and Satterthwaite independently derived an approximation: the distribution is well approximated by t(\nu), where the effective degrees of freedom \nu is given by:

\nu \approx \dfrac{\left(\dfrac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{s_2^2}{n_2}\right)^2}{\dfrac{\left(s_1^2/n_1\right)^2}{n_1 - 1} + \dfrac{\left(s_2^2/n_2\right)^2}{n_2 - 1}}

In practice, \nu is rounded down to the nearest integer before looking up critical values. Note that \nu always lies between \min(n_1,n_2)-1 and n_1+n_2-2. When the two sample variances are equal, \nu achieves its maximum value n_1+n_2-2, which coincides with the pooled t-test degrees of freedom.

Welch vs. Pooled: Which Should You Use?

An older approach — the pooled two-sample t-test — assumes equal population variances (\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2) and estimates their common value by pooling the two sample variances. This yields a test statistic with an exact t distribution under normality. However, it is invalid whenever the variances are unequal, a situation that is common in practice. Welch's t-test does not require equal variances. Statistical simulation studies have demonstrated that Welch's test maintains correct Type I error rates even when variances differ substantially, while the pooled test can be severely anti-conservative. Modern statistical practice therefore recommends Welch's t-test as the default for two independent samples.

As n_1, n_2 \to \infty, the t distribution with \nu degrees of freedom approaches the standard normal distribution, and the two-sample t-test converges in behavior to the two-sample Z-test. This confirms that the additional uncertainty from estimating \sigma becomes negligible with large samples.

4. A/B Testing as a Two-Sample Hypothesis Test

A/B testing — ubiquitous in technology, marketing, and product development — is simply a two-sample hypothesis test applied to a randomized experiment. The terminology differs slightly from the academic framing, but the underlying statistical logic is identical. Group A (the control) represents the current version; group B (the treatment) represents a proposed change. The null hypothesis is that both versions perform equally; the alternative is that they differ.

When the outcome variable is binary — a user either clicks or does not, converts or does not, churns or does not — the appropriate statistic is the two-proportion Z-test. Let \hat{p}_A = x_A / n_A and \hat{p}_B = x_B / n_B be the observed click rates in groups A and B respectively. The hypotheses are:

H_0 : p_A = p_B \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : p_A \neq p_B

Under H_0, both groups share a common unknown proportion p. We estimate this pooled proportion by combining all successes across both groups:

\hat{p} = \dfrac{n_A \hat{p}_A + n_B \hat{p}_B}{n_A + n_B}

Using this pooled estimate in the standard error (as prescribed by the null hypothesis), the test statistic is:

Z = \dfrac{\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\dfrac{1}{n_A} + \dfrac{1}{n_B}\right)}}

Under H_0 and with sufficiently large samples (the normal approximation to the binomial is adequate when n_A \hat{p}, n_A (1-\hat{p}), n_B \hat{p}, n_B (1-\hat{p}) \geq 5), the statistic Z \sim N(0,1) and the same critical values as the one-sample Z-test apply.

Statistical Significance vs. Practical Significance in A/B Tests

A foundational principle that practitioners frequently violate: statistical significance is not the same as practical significance. With a sufficiently large sample, virtually any difference, no matter how negligible in magnitude, will produce a p-value below any threshold. A platform serving one million users per day may detect a 0.01 percentage point improvement in click rate at p < 0.001. Whether that improvement justifies engineering cost, deployment risk, and increased system complexity is a business and product decision entirely separate from the hypothesis test.

Conversely, a small sample may fail to detect a large and commercially important effect. Always supplement the p-value with an effect size measure (Cohen's d for means, or the raw difference in proportions for binary outcomes) and report the confidence interval for the difference. The confidence interval makes the uncertainty visible in a way that the binary reject/fail-to-reject decision obscures.

5. Effect Size: Cohen's d

Statistical significance tells you whether a difference is unlikely to be zero. It does not tell you whether the difference is large enough to matter. Effect size fills this gap by quantifying the magnitude of the difference in standardized units, allowing comparison across studies and contexts where sample sizes and measurement scales differ.

For two-sample comparisons of means, the standard effect size measure is Cohen's d, defined as the difference in means divided by a pooled within-group standard deviation:

d = \dfrac{|\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2|}{s_p} \quad \text{where } s_p = \sqrt{\dfrac{(n_1-1)s_1^2 + (n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}}

The pooled standard deviation s_p is a weighted average of the two within-group standard deviations, with larger samples contributing more weight. It provides a single scale against which to measure the raw difference in means.

Jacob Cohen (1988) proposed widely used benchmarks for interpreting d in behavioral and social science research:

S
Small effect: d \approx 0.2
The two distributions overlap substantially. The difference is real but modest — visible in aggregate data but not readily apparent in individual cases. Example: the height difference between 15- and 16-year-old males in a large population study.
M
Medium effect: d \approx 0.5
Moderate separation between groups, visible to a careful observer. This is the rough magnitude of typical treatment effects in social science research. Example: the effect of a structured reading intervention on test scores.
L
Large effect: d \approx 0.8
Clear, substantial separation between groups. The effect is obvious even to an untrained eye examining the raw data. Example: the difference in grip strength between trained athletes and sedentary adults.

Cohen himself emphasized that these benchmarks are rough guidelines, not universal standards. What constitutes a practically important effect depends on the domain: a drug that reduces stroke risk by d = 0.1 across a large population may be enormously valuable, while a training program with d = 0.5 on a low-stakes outcome may not justify its cost. Always interpret Cohen's d in the context of the specific application.

Worked Example: The Email A/B Test

We return to the marketing scenario from the opening of this module. Two email subject lines were tested on independent random samples of subscribers. The data are:

  • Version A: n_A = 200 subscribers, x_A = 24 clicks, \hat{p}_A = 24/200 = 0.12
  • Version B: n_B = 200 subscribers, x_B = 30 clicks, \hat{p}_B = 30/200 = 0.15

We test H_0 : p_A = p_B against H_1 : p_A \neq p_B at significance level \alpha = 0.05.

1
Compute the pooled proportion.
\hat{p} = \dfrac{x_A + x_B}{n_A + n_B} = \dfrac{24 + 30}{200 + 200} = \dfrac{54}{400} = 0.135
2
Compute the standard error under H_0.
\text{SE} = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})\left(\dfrac{1}{n_A} + \dfrac{1}{n_B}\right)} = \sqrt{0.135 \times 0.865 \times \left(\dfrac{1}{200} + \dfrac{1}{200}\right)}
= \sqrt{0.135 \times 0.865 \times 0.01} = \sqrt{0.001168} \approx 0.0342
3
Compute the Z statistic.
Z = \dfrac{\hat{p}_A - \hat{p}_B}{\text{SE}} = \dfrac{0.12 - 0.15}{0.0342} = \dfrac{-0.03}{0.0342} \approx -0.877
4
Compute the p-value (two-tailed).
p\text{-value} = 2 \times \Phi(-|Z|) = 2 \times \Phi(-0.877) = 2 \times 0.190 = 0.380
5
Make the decision.
Since p = 0.380 > \alpha = 0.05, we fail to reject H_0.

At the 5% significance level, there is insufficient evidence that the two email subject lines differ in click rate. The 3 percentage point difference observed in this sample is consistent with random variation between two groups of 200 subscribers drawn from the same population.

Practical note: To reliably detect a true 3 percentage point difference (with p_A = 0.12) at 80% power, a power analysis reveals that approximately 800 observations per group are required — four times the current sample size. The study was underpowered, not conclusive.

Practice Problem

A tech company runs an experiment on two versions of their checkout flow. Group A (n=80): mean checkout time \bar{X}_A = 45 s, s_A = 12 s. Group B (n=80): mean checkout time \bar{X}_B = 40 s, s_B = 11 s. Using a two-sample t-test at \alpha = 0.05 (two-tailed), test whether the checkout times differ significantly. Compute the Welch-Satterthwaite degrees of freedom, the t statistic, p-value, and Cohen's d. Interpret your results fully.

Show Solution
1
Standard Error.
\text{SE} = \sqrt{\dfrac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \dfrac{s_B^2}{n_B}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{12^2}{80} + \dfrac{11^2}{80}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{144}{80} + \dfrac{121}{80}} = \sqrt{3.3125} \approx 1.820
2
t statistic.
t = \dfrac{\bar{X}_A - \bar{X}_B}{\text{SE}} = \dfrac{45 - 40}{1.820} = \dfrac{5}{1.820} \approx 2.747
3
Welch-Satterthwaite degrees of freedom.
\nu \approx \dfrac{(3.3125)^2}{\dfrac{(1.80)^2}{79} + \dfrac{(1.5125)^2}{79}} = \dfrac{10.972}{0.041 + 0.029} \approx 156.7 \;\Rightarrow\; \nu = 156
4
Decision and p-value.
Critical value: t^*(0.025, 156) \approx 1.975. Since |t| = 2.747 > 1.975, we reject H_0.
p-value: 2 \times P(T_{156} > 2.747) \approx 0.007 < 0.05.
5
Cohen's d.
s_p = \sqrt{\dfrac{(79)(144) + (79)(121)}{158}} = \sqrt{\dfrac{11376 + 9559}{158}} = \sqrt{132.5} \approx 11.51
d = \dfrac{|45 - 40|}{11.51} = \dfrac{5}{11.51} \approx 0.434 \quad \text{(medium effect)}
6
Interpretation.
At the 5% significance level, the checkout times differ significantly between the two versions (t(156) = 2.747, p = 0.007). Group B's checkout flow is on average 5 seconds faster. The medium effect size (d = 0.43) indicates that this difference is not merely a statistical artifact of large sample size — it represents a substantively meaningful improvement that merits consideration for deployment.
