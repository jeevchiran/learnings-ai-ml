Real-world hook — blood pressure treatment: A cardiologist wants to evaluate whether a 12-week lifestyle intervention (diet and exercise) reduces systolic blood pressure. She measures each of 15 patients before the program and again after completion. The key phrase is "each patient" — the same person is measured twice. These two measurements are not independent of each other: a patient who starts with high blood pressure is likely to have higher-than-average blood pressure at both time points. If we treated this as two independent groups (as in Module 4), we would be ignoring this built-in structure — and throwing away statistical power in the process. The paired t-test exploits the connection between the two measurements to give us a more sensitive and more appropriate test.

Section 1: Paired vs Independent — Why It Matters

The core idea

In Module 4, the two groups consisted of entirely different people: Group A vs Group B. Each measurement belonged to one group and one group only; there was no correspondence between an observation in Group A and any observation in Group B. In a paired design, the same subject (or a carefully matched pair of subjects) appears in both conditions. This seemingly small structural difference has profound consequences for the analysis and its sensitivity.

Paired designs arise in three main forms in practice:

  • Before/After: The same subject is measured before and after a treatment, intervention, or period of time.
  • Matched pairs: Two distinct subjects are matched on key characteristics (age, weight, baseline health) so that within each pair, one receives the treatment and the other serves as the control.
  • Crossover: Each subject receives both treatments in sequence (with a washout period in between), and the order is randomized.

Why pairing is powerful

Consider the cardiologist's study. Patient A might naturally have a systolic BP of 155 mmHg and patient B might naturally have 135 mmHg. This between-subject variability — the variation in baseline blood pressure from person to person — has nothing to do with whether the intervention works. Yet if we analyzed the pre- and post-intervention readings as two independent groups, this person-to-person noise would inflate the standard error of our estimate and make it harder to detect the intervention's true effect.

The remedy is elegant: compute the difference d_i = x_{\text{before},i} - x_{\text{after},i} for each subject. Because both measurements come from the same person, patient A's natural tendency toward higher BP is present in both readings and cancels out in the subtraction. The variability that remains in the d_i values reflects only within-person change, which is exactly what we want to measure. By eliminating between-subject noise, we are left with a cleaner, more precise signal.

This reduction to differences means the paired t-test is, at its core, nothing more than a one-sample t-test applied to the set of differences d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n. All the machinery from Module 3 applies directly.

The mathematical reduction

Define the difference for each pair as:

d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i} \quad \text{for each subject } i = 1, 2, \ldots, n

From these n differences, compute their sample mean and standard deviation:

\bar{d} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n d_i, \qquad s_d = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (d_i - \bar{d})^2}

The null hypothesis H_0: \mu_d = 0 is then tested using a standard one-sample t-test on d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n. We have reduced a seemingly two-sample problem to a one-sample problem by a single algebraic step.

Section 2: Derivation of the Test Statistic

We now derive the paired t-test statistic step by step. Each step is logically necessary; together they give us an exact procedure for testing whether the mean difference in the population is zero.

1
Compute the difference for each pair. For subject i, the difference is d_i = x_{1i} - x_{2i} (before minus after, or treatment minus control, depending on the research question). The sign convention determines which direction of difference will be positive, so choose it to match the research hypothesis.
2
State the hypotheses. The null hypothesis asserts no average change in the population: H_0: \mu_d = 0. The alternative may be two-tailed (H_1: \mu_d \neq 0), right-tailed (H_1: \mu_d > 0), or left-tailed (H_1: \mu_d < 0), chosen to reflect the direction of the effect the researcher expects or cares about.
3
Compute the sample mean of differences.
\bar{d} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} d_i
This is the point estimate of \mu_d, the true population mean difference. It is the quantity we will compare to zero.
4
Compute the sample standard deviation of differences.
s_d = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(d_i - \bar{d})^2}{n - 1}}
This measures how variable the individual differences are. A small s_d means the treatment had a very consistent effect across subjects; a large s_d means the effect was heterogeneous.
5
Compute the standard error of \bar{d}.
\text{SE}(\bar{d}) = \frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}
The standard error quantifies the precision of \bar{d} as an estimate of \mu_d. It shrinks as n increases (more pairs = more precise estimate) and as s_d decreases (more consistent differences = more precise estimate).
6
Form the paired t-statistic.
t = \frac{\bar{d} - 0}{s_d / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\bar{d}}{s_d / \sqrt{n}}
This is exactly the one-sample t-statistic from Module 3, applied to the differences. It measures how many standard errors \bar{d} lies from zero. The null hypothesis value of \mu_d is zero, hence the numerator simplifies to \bar{d}.
7
Identify the null distribution. Under H_0, the statistic t follows a Student's t-distribution with n - 1 degrees of freedom. There are n - 1 degrees of freedom because we have n differences and we estimate one parameter (\bar{d}) from them before computing s_d.
t \sim t(n-1) \quad \text{under } H_0
8
Make a decision. Compare the observed t to the critical value t^*(\alpha/2,\, n-1) for a two-tailed test (or t^*(\alpha,\, n-1) for a one-tailed test), or equivalently compute the p-value from the t-distribution and compare to \alpha. Reject H_0 if the observed |t| > t^* (two-tailed) or if p-value < \alpha.

Section 3: Assumptions of the Paired t-Test

Like all parametric tests, the paired t-test rests on assumptions that must be checked before the results can be trusted. Violating these assumptions can lead to incorrect p-values and misleading conclusions.

1. Random sample of pairs

The pairs must be randomly sampled from the population of interest, or the assignment to paired conditions must be random (as in a randomized crossover trial). Non-random sampling risks selection bias: the sample may not represent the population, and the inferential leap from sample to population is unjustified.

2. Normality of differences

The differences d_i must be approximately normally distributed in the population. For small samples (roughly n < 30), this is a consequential assumption; check it with a histogram or QQ-plot of the differences. For n \geq 30, the Central Limit Theorem ensures that \bar{d} is approximately normal regardless of the shape of the difference distribution, making the test robust.

3. Independence between pairs

Different pairs must be independent of each other. The within-pair dependence is intentional and is the whole point of the design. But pair 1 and pair 2 must not influence each other. This is violated, for example, if pairs are family members, or if subjects are treated simultaneously in a way that allows contamination across pairs.

4. No extreme outliers in differences

Outliers in the d_i values can heavily distort both \bar{d} and s_d, invalidating the t-statistic. Inspect the differences with a boxplot before proceeding. A single large outlier can either produce a spurious rejection of H_0 or mask a real effect. If outliers are present, consider a non-parametric alternative such as the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Section 4: When to Use Paired vs Independent Tests

Choosing the correct test structure is not optional — using an independent-samples t-test on data from a paired design is a methodological error, even if it sometimes yields a numerically similar answer. The table below summarizes the key differences to guide your choice.

Feature Paired t-Test Independent t-Test
Sample structure Same subject measured twice, or matched pairs Two unrelated groups with no correspondence
n per group Must be equal (n pairs) Can differ between groups
Degrees of freedom n - 1 (one per pair) Welch approximation, typically close to n_1 + n_2 - 2
Between-subject noise Eliminated by differencing within pairs Present in SE, reduces power
Typical power Higher, when data is truly paired Lower when pairing is available but ignored
When to use Before/after designs, matched pair studies, crossover trials Completely separate, unrelated groups

Key principle: the design determines the test

If the data were collected in a paired fashion (same subject twice, or matched pairs), use the paired t-test. Using an independent-samples test on paired data ignores structure in the data that reduces variability and increases power. You do not get to "choose" the independent test because it is simpler — the data structure mandates the paired analysis.

Section 6: Worked Example

We now apply the paired t-test to the cardiologist's blood pressure study in full detail, working through every numerical step from raw data to conclusion.

Research context and hypotheses

Eight patients completed a 12-week lifestyle intervention. Systolic blood pressure was recorded for each patient immediately before the program and immediately upon completion. The differences are defined as d_i = x_{\text{before},i} - x_{\text{after},i}, so a positive difference means blood pressure decreased (improvement).

Because the cardiologist believes the intervention reduces blood pressure, she specifies a one-sided (right-tailed) alternative. The hypotheses are:

H_0: \mu_d = 0 \quad \text{(no average change in blood pressure)}
H_1: \mu_d > 0 \quad \text{(blood pressure decreases on average, so before} > \text{after)}

The significance level is \alpha = 0.05.

Step-by-step calculation

Step 1: Sample size. We have n = 8 pairs.

Step 2: Mean of differences.

\bar{d} = \frac{8 + 6 + 11 + 9 + 8 + 10 + 6 + 8}{8} = \frac{66}{8} = 8.25 \text{ mmHg}

Step 3: Standard deviation of differences. The deviations from \bar{d} = 8.25 are:

-0.25,\; -2.25,\; 2.75,\; 0.75,\; -0.25,\; 1.75,\; -2.25,\; -0.25

Squaring each: 0.0625,\; 5.0625,\; 7.5625,\; 0.5625,\; 0.0625,\; 3.0625,\; 5.0625,\; 0.0625. Sum of squared deviations = 21.5.

s_d = \sqrt{\frac{21.5}{8 - 1}} = \sqrt{\frac{21.5}{7}} = \sqrt{3.071} \approx 1.753 \text{ mmHg}

Step 4: Standard error.

\text{SE}(\bar{d}) = \frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1.753}{\sqrt{8}} = \frac{1.753}{2.828} \approx 0.620 \text{ mmHg}

Step 5: Paired t-statistic.

t = \frac{\bar{d}}{\text{SE}(\bar{d})} = \frac{8.25}{0.620} \approx 13.31

Step 6: Degrees of freedom. df = n - 1 = 8 - 1 = 7.

Step 7: Decision. For a right-tailed test at \alpha = 0.05 with df = 7, the critical value is t^*(0.05, 7) = 1.895. The observed t = 13.31 \gg 1.895, so we reject H_0 overwhelmingly. The p-value = P(t_{7} > 13.31) \approx 0.0000012, far below any conventional significance threshold.

Reject H₀ — very strong evidence that the intervention reduces systolic blood pressure

Conclusion: At the 5% significance level, there is extremely strong statistical evidence that the 12-week lifestyle intervention reduces systolic blood pressure. The estimated mean reduction is 8.25 mmHg. A 95% confidence interval for the true mean reduction is:

\bar{d} \pm t^*(0.025, 7) \times \text{SE} = 8.25 \pm 2.365 \times 0.620 = [6.78,\; 9.72] \text{ mmHg}

We are 95% confident the true average blood pressure reduction in the population lies between 6.78 and 9.72 mmHg.

At \alpha = 0.05, test whether the workshop improved scores. Define d_i = \text{Post}_i - \text{Pre}_i (positive values indicate improvement). The hypotheses are H_0: \mu_d = 0 vs H_1: \mu_d > 0 (right-tailed).

Solution

Step 1: Compute differences d_i = \text{Post}_i - \text{Pre}_i:

d = (8,\; 4,\; 7,\; -4,\; 10,\; 7)

Step 2: Mean of differences.

\bar{d} = \frac{8 + 4 + 7 + (-4) + 10 + 7}{6} = \frac{32}{6} \approx 5.333

Step 3: Standard deviation. Deviations from \bar{d} = 5.333: 2.667,\; -1.333,\; 1.667,\; -9.333,\; 4.667,\; 1.667. Squared deviations: 7.111,\; 1.778,\; 2.778,\; 87.111,\; 21.778,\; 2.778. Sum = 123.333.

s_d = \sqrt{\frac{123.333}{5}} = \sqrt{24.667} \approx 4.967

Step 4: Standard error.

\text{SE} = \frac{4.967}{\sqrt{6}} = \frac{4.967}{2.449} \approx 2.028

Step 5: t-statistic.

t = \frac{5.333}{2.028} \approx 2.630

Step 6: df = 6 - 1 = 5. Right-tailed critical value: t^*(0.05, 5) = 2.015. Since 2.630 > 2.015, we reject H_0. The one-tailed p-value = P(t_5 > 2.630) \approx 0.023 < 0.05.

Reject H₀ — sufficient evidence that the workshop improved test scores

Conclusion: At the 5% significance level, there is sufficient evidence to conclude that the 4-week intensive workshop improved student test scores. The mean improvement was 5.33 points. Note, however, that student 4's score decreased by 4 points — with only 6 students the analysis is sensitive to individual variation, and the relatively large s_d reflects this heterogeneity in outcomes.
