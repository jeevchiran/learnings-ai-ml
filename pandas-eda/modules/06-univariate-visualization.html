What, Why, and How

WHAT: Charts for a single variable

Univariate visualization means looking at one column at a time — examining its shape, spread, and any unusual features before bringing other variables into the picture. The three workhorses are:

  • Histogram — divides the value range into equal-width bins and counts how many rows fall into each. Reveals whether the data clusters, spreads, or has multiple peaks.
  • KDE curve (Kernel Density Estimate) — a smooth continuous approximation of the histogram shape. Not sensitive to bin count; instead depends on a bandwidth parameter.
  • Box plot — shows the five-number summary (min, Q1, median, Q3, max) and marks individual outliers beyond the Tukey fences. Compact enough to stack many columns side by side.

WHY: Raw numbers hide distribution structure

A column's mean and standard deviation give you a two-number summary, but they cannot tell you whether the data is symmetric, skewed, bimodal, or uniform. Two datasets can have identical means and identical variances yet look completely different when plotted. Visualization reveals structure that statistics alone hide.

In this taxi dataset the difference is dramatic:

  • fare_amount: mean ~$26, but the median is ~$11.75. A single $399 outlier drags the mean far from where most fares sit. The histogram shows a compact cluster at $3–$20 with a lone spike near $400.
  • trip_distance: median is about 3 miles, but one trip records 120 miles — a probable data error that shifts the mean and inflates the standard deviation.
  • passenger_count: only values 1–6 exist (plus some nulls). A histogram instantly reveals this is a discrete distribution with mode = 1.

HOW: Three pandas / seaborn patterns

All three chart types require import matplotlib.pyplot as plt and optionally import seaborn as sns.

1
Histogram via pandas
df['fare_amount'].hist(bins=10, figsize=(8,4)) — the bins parameter controls granularity. Fewer bins smooth over detail; more bins can make sparse regions look noisy.
2
KDE curve via pandas
df['fare_amount'].plot(kind='kde') — produces a smooth density curve. Unlike a histogram, the area under the curve integrates to 1 (it estimates probability density, not counts).
3
Box plot via pandas
df.boxplot(column='fare_amount') — whiskers sit at the IQR fences (k=1.5 by default) and individual points beyond the whiskers are plotted as circles. Instantly shows median, spread, and outliers.

Bars + overlaid smooth density curve in one call
The kde=True overlay is powerful for bin-count sensitivity. When you have a small sample, histogram shape can change dramatically as you slide the bin count up or down. Adding kde=True puts a smooth density curve on top — it is not affected by bin boundaries and shows the overall shape independently. Use both together to distinguish real structure from binning artifacts.

Practice

Problem 1: Right-skewed distribution and mean vs. median

Easy

What does a right-skewed distribution mean for the relationship between mean and median?

In a right-skewed distribution, the tail extends to the right (toward higher values). The mean is pulled in the direction of the tail, so mean > median.

Intuition: the few very large values contribute disproportionately to the arithmetic average but do not move the median (which only depends on rank order). For fare_amount, the $399 outlier drags the mean up to ~$26 while the median stays near $11.75 — a difference of ~$14, almost entirely caused by that single row.

Problem 2: Trade-off of increasing bin count

Medium

If you increase bin count from 5 to 30 for fare_amount, what trade-off do you make?

With 5 bins you get a smoother but coarser picture — structural features like the gap between $20–$399 are hidden inside wide bins, and each bin has more counts so bars look stable.

With 30 bins you get more granularity but noisier bars — in a 30-row sample, many bins will contain 0 or 1 data points, making it hard to distinguish real patterns from sampling noise. The bin near $399 will be a single isolated bar far to the right regardless, but the main cluster ($3–$25) will fragment into choppy peaks.

The practical guideline: start around bins = sqrt(n) (~5–6 for 30 rows), then adjust. Adding a KDE overlay removes sensitivity to this choice entirely.

Problem 3: Choosing the right central tendency measure for fare_amount

Hard

The fare_amount mean is ~$26 but the median is ~$11.75. Should you use mean or median to represent the "typical fare"? Why?

Use the median (~$11.75) to represent the typical fare in this sample.

The mean (~$26) is distorted by a single $399 record that is almost certainly a meter malfunction. A passenger paying a typical NYC taxi fare does not pay $26 — they pay somewhere in the $6–$20 range. The median, being the middle-ranked value, is immune to this outlier: removing or capping ID 26 barely changes the median but cuts the mean roughly in half.

Wider principle: whenever a distribution is right-skewed and the tail is produced by outliers or data errors rather than the true data-generating process, the median is a more robust measure of center. If the high values were legitimate (e.g., long-distance fares), you'd need to decide whether "typical" means "most common" (median) or "expected value for a random trip" (mean) — two valid but different questions.

Module 6 — Check Your Understanding

1. A histogram shows fare_amount is right-skewed. This means:

A Most fares are high
B A few very high fares pull the mean above the median
C The data has errors
D All fares are equal
Correct. Right skew means the distribution has a long tail to the right. The bulk of values cluster at the low end; a small number of high values drag the arithmetic mean rightward above the median. Most fares in this dataset are $3–$20.
Not quite. Right skew means the tail extends to the right (high values), so most data sits at the low end — most fares are actually low. The key consequence is mean > median, because the few large values inflate the average without moving the rank-based median.

2. What does bin count affect in a histogram?

A The total count of data points
B The granularity of the distribution view
C The mean value
D Whether outliers are included
Correct. Bin count controls how finely the value range is divided. Fewer bins smooth the picture; more bins reveal finer structure but introduce noise when sample size is small. The underlying data does not change — only how it is grouped.
Not quite. The total data count is fixed regardless of how many bins you choose. Bin count only affects how the existing values are grouped into intervals — the granularity of what you see, not what is there.

3. Which chart best shows median, IQR, and outliers simultaneously?

A Histogram
B KDE curve
C Box plot
D Bar chart
Correct. The box plot is designed precisely for this: the box spans Q1–Q3 (the IQR), the line inside the box marks the median, the whiskers extend to the Tukey fences, and individual points beyond the whiskers are plotted as outliers. All five of those facts are encoded in a single compact chart.
Not quite. Histograms show shape and count; KDE curves show shape as density. Neither encodes the five-number summary directly. Bar charts are for categorical counts. The box plot is the chart specifically built to display Q1, median, Q3, whiskers, and outliers in one view.

4. df['col'].plot(kind='kde') produces:

A A bar chart of counts
B A scatter plot
C A pie chart
D A smooth density curve estimate
Correct. KDE (Kernel Density Estimate) places a small smooth kernel function at each data point and sums them, producing a continuous curve. The y-axis is probability density (area under curve = 1), not counts. The advantage over a histogram is that the shape does not depend on arbitrary bin boundaries.
Not quite. kind='kde' specifically requests a Kernel Density Estimate — a smooth continuous curve approximating the distribution's shape. It is not a bar chart (that would be kind='bar'), not a scatter (that is kind='scatter'), and not a pie chart.
