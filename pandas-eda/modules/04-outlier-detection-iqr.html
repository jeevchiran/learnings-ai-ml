What, Why, and How

WHAT: Outliers and Tukey Fences

An outlier is a data point that sits far from the bulk of the distribution. The IQR method formalises "far" using Tukey fences — symmetric boundaries built from the spread of the middle 50% of values.

Key quantities:

IQR = Q3 - Q1
\text{Lower fence} = Q1 - k \cdot IQR
\text{Upper fence} = Q3 + k \cdot IQR

The default multiplier is k = 1.5 (Tukey's original choice). A stricter alternative, k = 3.0, is sometimes called the "far outlier" threshold. Any point outside the fences is flagged as an outlier.

WHY: Outliers distort summaries and models

Outliers skew the arithmetic mean, inflate variance, and drag regression lines off-track. In this dataset, fare_amount ID 26 records a $399.00 fare for a 6.7-mile trip — almost certainly a meter-malfunction error. If left in, it pulls the column mean from ~$14 to ~$26 and would dominate any mean-based model. But not every extreme value is an error: IDs 6 and 18 record fares of $45 and $52 — plausible for a JFK airport flat-rate (RatecodeID=2). Detecting outliers is only step one; deciding what to do with them requires domain knowledge.

For reference, a z-score framing makes the same point:

z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}

A z-score beyond ±3 roughly corresponds to k=3 IQR fences on normal data — but the IQR method is more robust because Q1 and Q3 themselves are unaffected by the very outliers you are trying to find.

HOW: Four-step workflow

1
Compute IQR
Sort the column, find Q1 (25th percentile) and Q3 (75th percentile), subtract. For fare_amount: Q1 = 6.125, Q3 = 19.125, IQR = 13.0.
2
Apply Tukey fences
At k = 1.5: lower = 6.125 − 1.5 × 13.0 = −13.375, upper = 19.125 + 1.5 × 13.0 = 38.625. Any fare outside this range is flagged.
3
Visualise
A boxplot is the natural companion to IQR — its whiskers sit exactly at the Tukey fences, and points beyond the whiskers are individually plotted, making outliers visible at a glance.
4
Choose a treatment: remove or cap
Remove: drop outlier rows entirely. Best when the outlier is a confirmed error. Cap (Winsorize): replace values beyond the fence with the fence value itself. Best when extreme values are real but should not dominate — you keep all rows, just limit their leverage.

Code Walkthrough

Python — compute IQR bounds and flag outliers 
Q1 = df['fare_amount'].quantile(0.25)   # 6.125
Q3 = df['fare_amount'].quantile(0.75)   # 19.125
IQR = Q3 - Q1                                 # 13.0

k = 1.5
lower = Q1 - k * IQR                          # -13.375
upper = Q3 + k * IQR                          # 38.625

outliers = df[(df.fare_amount < lower) | (df.fare_amount > upper)]
print(f"Outliers: {len(outliers)} rows")      # 3 rows
Outliers: 3 rows (ids 6, 18, 26)
Python — treatment: remove vs. cap (Winsorize) 
# Option A: Remove outlier rows
df_clean = df[(df.fare_amount >= lower) & (df.fare_amount <= upper)]
print(f"Rows after removal: {len(df_clean)}")   # 27

# Option B: Cap (Winsorize) — replace with fence value
df_capped = df.copy()
df_capped['fare_amount'] = df_capped['fare_amount'].clip(lower, upper)
print(df_capped['fare_amount'].max())              # 38.625 (was 399.0)
Rows after removal: 27 38.625
Statistical outlier ≠ data error — always investigate before removing IDs 6 ($45) and 18 ($52) are flagged by the IQR method at k=1.5, but their RatecodeID=2 marks them as JFK airport flat-rate fares — a documented, legitimate fare structure. Blindly deleting them would remove valid data about a real trip type. Only ID 26 ($399, standard RatecodeID=1, 6.7-mile trip) has no legitimate explanation — that is the true error most likely caused by a meter malfunction.

Practice

Problem 1: Compute IQR for fare_amount

Easy

What is the IQR of fare_amount in this 30-row sample?

IQR = Q3 − Q1 = 19.125 − 6.125 = 13.0

Q1 is the 25th percentile (the median of the lower half) and Q3 is the 75th percentile (the median of the upper half). IQR measures the spread of the middle 50% of data, making it robust to extremes: adding or removing a single very large value does not change Q1 or Q3 at all, so the IQR stays stable.

Problem 2: Fences at k=2.0 — how many outliers survive?

Medium

At k=2.0, what are the Tukey fences for fare_amount? How many outliers does k=2.0 flag compared with k=1.5?

With IQR = 13.0:

  • Lower fence = 6.125 − 2.0 × 13.0 = −19.875
  • Upper fence = 19.125 + 2.0 × 13.0 = 45.125

At k=1.5 the upper fence was 38.625, which flagged IDs 6 ($45), 18 ($52), and 26 ($399) — three outliers. At k=2.0 the fence rises to 45.125: ID 6 ($45.00) is now just inside the fence and ID 18 ($52.00) is still outside; only ID 26 ($399.00) is unambiguously outside — so one or two outliers depending on exact fence comparison (45.00 < 45.125, so actually IDs 18 and 26). Verify: $45.00 < $45.125 → inside; $52.00 > $45.125 → outside; $399.00 > $45.125 → outside. Result: 2 outliers (IDs 18, 26) at k=2.0.

Problem 3: Why choose k=2.0 over k=1.5?

Hard

Why might you choose k=2.0 over k=1.5 for fare_amount? What does the difference tell you about the role of domain knowledge in outlier detection?

At k=1.5, the IQR method flags ID 6 ($45) as an outlier — but domain knowledge reveals it is a legitimate JFK airport flat-rate fare (RatecodeID=2). Raising k to 2.0 widens the fence enough that $45 is no longer flagged, avoiding a false positive.

This illustrates the core tension: the IQR threshold is statistical, but the decision of where to draw the line is a domain decision. A stricter k catches more edge cases but risks removing structurally valid data. A looser k is more tolerant of legitimate extremes but may miss borderline errors. The right k is the one that minimises false positives (legitimate data wrongly removed) and false negatives (actual errors wrongly kept) — and that calibration requires understanding the data-generating process, not just running an algorithm.

In practice: always plot the flagged rows, check their other fields, and decide case-by-case before deleting anything.

Quiz

Module 4 — Check Your Understanding

1. What does IQR measure?

A Distance between mean and median
B Spread of the middle 50% of data
C Standard deviation squared
D Range of the entire dataset
Correct. IQR = Q3 − Q1 captures how wide the middle 50% of the distribution is, ignoring the top and bottom quarters entirely. That's why it's robust to outliers — the extremes are excluded by definition.
Not quite. IQR = Q3 − Q1, the width of the interquartile range. It measures spread across the middle 50% of data, not the full range, not the deviation from the mean, and not variance.

2. Which fare_amount value is MOST LIKELY a data error (not a legitimate fare)?

A $45.00 (RatecodeID=2, airport flat rate)
B $52.00 (RatecodeID=2, airport flat rate)
C $399.00 (RatecodeID=1, standard meter, 6.7-mile trip — likely meter malfunction)
D All three are errors
Correct. $399 on a 6.7-mile standard-meter trip is implausible — at roughly $3/mile that trip should cost around $20–25. The $45 and $52 fares carry RatecodeID=2 which documents them as JFK flat-rate fares, a structured and legitimate pricing tier.
Not quite. The key is RatecodeID: IDs 6 and 18 both carry RatecodeID=2, the documented NYC TLC code for JFK flat-rate fares. Those prices are expected. ID 26's $399 on a standard meter for 6.7 miles has no legitimate explanation — that is the error.

3. Winsorization (capping) vs. removal — when should you prefer capping?

A When you want fewer rows in the dataset
B When the outlier represents a legitimate extreme value, not an error
C When the dataset is large enough that losing rows doesn't matter
D Never — always remove outliers to keep the data clean
Correct. Capping preserves the information that an extreme value occurred while reducing its leverage on means and models. If the $45 and $52 JFK fares are real events you want to keep, capping them to 38.625 retains the observation (these trips happened, they cost a lot) without letting them dominate a regression.
Not quite. Capping doesn't change row count, large sample size isn't the criterion, and "always remove" is a myth — removing legitimate extreme values introduces its own bias. Capping is right when the extreme is real but should not dominate: you preserve the fact it happened while limiting its influence.

4. If you increase k from 1.5 to 3.0, the fence width:

A Narrows — more points are flagged as outliers
B Widens — fewer points are flagged as outliers
C Stays the same — k only affects the lower fence
D Becomes undefined when k exceeds 2.0
Correct. Upper fence = Q3 + k × IQR; lower fence = Q1 − k × IQR. A larger k pushes both fences further out, so the band of "acceptable" values grows wider and fewer points fall outside it — fewer outliers are flagged.
Not quite. A larger k multiplies a larger amount away from Q1 and Q3: fences move outward, the acceptable band widens, and fewer points are flagged. Think of k as a tolerance dial — higher k means you tolerate more extreme values before calling them outliers.
