Module 2: Impurity Measures and the Training Process

Why Do We Need a Splitting Criterion?

A decision tree works by recursively partitioning data. At each node we must answer: which feature, and which threshold, gives us the best split? Without a mathematical measure of "good," we have no way to compare one candidate split against another.

Consider a clinical dataset with 10 patients — 6 with a disease (positive) and 4 without (negative). Two candidate features both produce a binary split:

Split Left child Right child
Feature A 9 positive, 1 negative 1 positive, 9 negative (wait — only 1+0 pos left if 6 total)
Feature A (corrected) 5 positive, 1 negative 1 positive, 3 negative
Feature B 5 positive, 5 negative 1 positive, 3 negative (wait — but 5+1=6 pos, 5+3=8 neg total, doesn't match)

Let us use a cleaner illustration. The parent has 6 positives and 4 negatives (10 total).

Split Left child Right child Intuitive quality
Feature A 5 pos, 1 neg 1 pos, 3 neg Both children are reasonably homogeneous
Feature B 3 pos, 3 neg 3 pos, 1 neg Left child is perfectly mixed — useless

Feature A is clearly better: its left child is almost all positive; its right child is majority negative. Feature B's left child is a coin flip. The tree cannot make a useful prediction there. We say Feature A produces purer children. But "intuition" is not an algorithm — we need a number.

From intuition to formula. We need a scalar function I(t) defined on a node t such that I(t) = 0 when the node is perfectly pure (all one class) and I(t) is maximised when classes are equally mixed. Then the gain of a split is I(parent) minus weighted sum of I(children). We pick whichever split maximises that gain. The three standard choices are Gini impurity, Shannon entropy, and misclassification rate.

Gini Impurity

Start from a probabilistic thought experiment. Pick two samples at random (with replacement) from a node. What is the probability they belong to different classes? That probability is the Gini impurity.

For class k, the probability of picking it first is p_k, and the probability the next pick is not class k is 1 - p_k. Summing over all classes:

G(t) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k(1 - p_k) = 1 - \sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k^2

The equivalence of the two forms follows from \sum_k p_k = 1:

\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k(1 - p_k) = \sum_k p_k - \sum_k p_k^2 = 1 - \sum_k p_k^2
Key properties of Gini impurity
  • G = 0 when the node is pure (all samples belong to one class, so one p_k = 1 and the rest are 0).
  • G = 1 − 1/K at maximum, achieved when all K classes are equally represented (p_k = 1/K for all k).
  • For binary classification: G = 2p(1-p), which reaches its maximum of 0.5 at p = 0.5.

Worked example

A node has 6 positives and 4 negatives (10 samples total). p_+ = 0.6, p_- = 0.4.

1
Compute squared proportions
p_+^2 + p_-^2 = 0.6^2 + 0.4^2 = 0.36 + 0.16 = 0.52
Square each class proportion and sum them.
2
Apply the Gini formula
G = 1 - \sum_k p_k^2 = 1 - 0.52 = 0.48
Subtract from 1. A value of 0.48 is close to the maximum of 0.5, confirming the node is nearly balanced.

Shannon Entropy

Information theory asks: how many bits on average are needed to encode the class label of a randomly drawn sample? If the node is pure, you already know the label — zero bits needed. If classes are equally mixed, you need the most bits. This quantity is Shannon entropy:

H(t) = -\sum_{k=1}^{K} p_k \log_2 p_k

By convention, 0 \cdot \log_2 0 = 0 (the limit as p \to 0^+ is 0).

Key properties of Shannon entropy
  • H = 0 when the node is pure.
  • H = log2(K) at maximum, when all classes are equally likely.
  • For binary classification: H = -p\log_2 p - (1-p)\log_2(1-p), reaching a maximum of 1 bit at p = 0.5.
  • Entropy is slightly more sensitive than Gini near the extremes (pure nodes), which can make it prefer splits that isolate small pure subsets.

Worked example (same node: 6 positives, 4 negatives)

1
Compute each term
0.6 \cdot \log_2(0.6) = 0.6 \cdot (-0.737) = -0.442
0.4 \cdot \log_2(0.4) = 0.4 \cdot (-1.322) = -0.529
Recall log2(0.6) = ln(0.6)/ln(2) ≈ −0.737 and log2(0.4) ≈ −1.322.
2
Sum and negate
H = -(-0.442 - 0.529) = -(-0.971) = 0.971 \text{ bits}
Close to 1 bit (maximum for binary), again confirming the node is nearly balanced.

Misclassification Rate

The simplest impurity measure: if we assign every sample in the node to the majority class, what fraction do we misclassify?

E(t) = 1 - \max_k \, p_k

For the 6/4 node: E = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4. For a pure node: E = 0. For the balanced binary case (p = 0.5): E = 0.5.

Why misclassification rate is not used for splitting in practice

Consider a parent with 10 samples: 6 positive, 4 negative. E(\text{parent}) = 0.4.

Split X: left = {4+, 4−}, right = {2+, 0−}.
Weighted E = (8/10)(1 − 0.5) + (2/10)(0) = 0.4 + 0 = 0.4. Gain = 0.4 − 0.4 = 0.

Split Y: left = {4+, 1−}, right = {2+, 3−}.
Weighted E = (5/10)(1 − 0.8) + (5/10)(1 − 0.6) = 0.10 + 0.20 = 0.30. Gain = 0.4 − 0.30 = 0.10.

Split Y is clearly better: its right child has a clear majority. Yet for Split X, misclassification rate reports zero gain because the majority class in the left child did not change. Entropy and Gini both correctly reward Split Y with a positive gain. This insensitivity makes misclassification rate a poor criterion for choosing splits.

Information Gain and Gini Gain

To evaluate a split that sends |t_L| samples left and |t_R| samples right (from a parent with |t| samples), we compute how much the weighted child impurity is below the parent impurity.

\text{IG}(t, A) = H(t) - \frac{|t_L|}{|t|} H(t_L) - \frac{|t_R|}{|t|} H(t_R)
\text{GG}(t, A) = G(t) - \frac{|t_L|}{|t|} G(t_L) - \frac{|t_R|}{|t|} G(t_R)

The term \frac{|t_L|}{|t|} I(t_L) + \frac{|t_R|}{|t|} I(t_R) is the weighted child impurity. It weights each child by its fraction of the parent's data so that larger children matter more. We choose the feature and threshold that maximises the gain.

Worked example: step-by-step Information Gain

Parent: 6 positive, 4 negative (10 total). Split A sends 5+/1− left and 1+/3− right.

1
Parent entropy
H(\text{parent}) = -(0.6 \log_2 0.6 + 0.4 \log_2 0.4) = 0.971 \text{ bits}
Already computed in Section 3.
2
Left child entropy (5+, 1−; n=6)
H_L = -\tfrac{5}{6}\log_2\tfrac{5}{6} - \tfrac{1}{6}\log_2\tfrac{1}{6} = -(0.833 \cdot (-0.263) + 0.167 \cdot (-2.585)) = 0.650 \text{ bits}
The left child is mostly positive, so entropy is below 1 bit.
3
Right child entropy (1+, 3−; n=4)
H_R = -\tfrac{1}{4}\log_2\tfrac{1}{4} - \tfrac{3}{4}\log_2\tfrac{3}{4} = -(0.25 \cdot (-2) + 0.75 \cdot (-0.415)) = 0.811 \text{ bits}
The right child is 75% negative — still some mixed-ness.
4
Weighted child impurity
\frac{6}{10}(0.650) + \frac{4}{10}(0.811) = 0.390 + 0.324 = 0.714 \text{ bits}
Weight each child by its share of the parent.
5
Information Gain
\text{IG} = 0.971 - 0.714 = \mathbf{0.257} \text{ bits}
This split reduces uncertainty by 0.257 bits — a substantial improvement for just 10 samples.

The CART Algorithm

CART (Classification and Regression Trees, Breiman et al. 1984) finds the best split at each node by exhaustive search: try every feature, try every threshold, compute the Gini gain, keep the best. Then recurse on each child until a stopping criterion is met.

BuildTree(data, depth, maxDepth, minSamples): if depth == maxDepth or |data| < minSamples or all labels same: return Leaf(majority_class(data)) bestGain = -INF bestFeature, bestThreshold = None, None for each feature j: for each unique value t in data[j]: left = data where x_j <= t right = data where x_j > t gain = GiniGain(data, left, right) if gain > bestGain: bestGain, bestFeature, bestThreshold = gain, j, t return Node(bestFeature, bestThreshold, left = BuildTree(leftData, depth+1, ...), right = BuildTree(rightData, depth+1, ...))

Stopping criteria

Recursion terminates when any of these conditions hold:

  1. Maximum depth reached — the max_depth hyperparameter. Prevents the tree from growing arbitrarily deep and overfitting.
  2. Too few samples in the node — the min_samples_split (or min_samples_leaf) hyperparameter. A split that leaves a child with only 1 sample usually overfits.
  3. Gain below a threshold — the min_impurity_decrease parameter in scikit-learn. A split must improve impurity by at least this amount to be accepted; otherwise the node becomes a leaf.
  4. All samples have the same label — the node is already pure; splitting cannot improve things further.

Computational complexity

At a single node with n samples and p features, we evaluate up to n thresholds for each of the p features, giving O(n \cdot p) gain computations. Each computation touches all n samples, so the cost per node is O(n^2 \cdot p) in the naive case. With up to n nodes per depth level and a tree of depth d:

\text{Total} = O(n^2 \cdot p \cdot d)

For n = 1,000, p = 10, d = 10 this is roughly 108 operations — manageable but not negligible. In practice scikit-learn pre-sorts each feature column once before fitting, which reduces the per-node cost to O(n \log n \cdot p), making moderately large datasets tractable.

Practice

Problem 1: Computing impurity measures by hand

Easy

A node contains 8 samples: 5 belong to class A and 3 to class B.

Compute each of the following:

  1. Gini impurity G(t)
  2. Shannon entropy H(t) (base 2)
  3. Misclassification rate E(t)

(a) Gini impurity. p_A = 5/8 = 0.625, p_B = 3/8 = 0.375.

G = 1 - (p_A^2 + p_B^2) = 1 - \left(\frac{25}{64} + \frac{9}{64}\right) = 1 - \frac{34}{64} = \frac{30}{64} \approx 0.469

(b) Shannon entropy. \log_2(0.625) \approx -0.678, \log_2(0.375) \approx -1.415.

H = -(0.625 \cdot (-0.678) + 0.375 \cdot (-1.415)) = -(−0.424 − 0.531) = 0.954 \text{ bits}

(c) Misclassification rate. The majority class is A with 5/8 samples.

E = 1 - \max_k p_k = 1 - \frac{5}{8} = 0.375

Problem 2: Comparing Gini Gain for two candidate splits

Medium

A parent node has 10 samples: 6 positive (+) and 4 negative (−). Two candidate splits are proposed:

  • Split A: left child = {4+, 0−}, right child = {2+, 4−}
  • Split B: left child = {3+, 1−}, right child = {3+, 3−}

Compute the Gini Gain for both splits. Which split is better?

Parent Gini.

G(\text{parent}) = 1 - (0.6^2 + 0.4^2) = 1 - 0.52 = 0.480

Split A.

Left child (4+, 0−; n=4): pure, so G_L = 0.

Right child (2+, 4−; n=6): p_+ = 2/6, p_- = 4/6.

G_R = 1 - \left(\tfrac{4}{36} + \tfrac{16}{36}\right) = 1 - \tfrac{20}{36} = \tfrac{16}{36} \approx 0.444
\text{GG}_A = 0.480 - \tfrac{4}{10}(0) - \tfrac{6}{10}(0.444) = 0.480 - 0.267 = \mathbf{0.213}

Split B.

Left child (3+, 1−; n=4): p_+ = 3/4, p_- = 1/4.

G_L = 1 - \left(\tfrac{9}{16} + \tfrac{1}{16}\right) = 1 - \tfrac{10}{16} = 0.375

Right child (3+, 3−; n=6): p_+ = p_- = 0.5.

G_R = 2(0.5)(0.5) = 0.500
\text{GG}_B = 0.480 - \tfrac{4}{10}(0.375) - \tfrac{6}{10}(0.500) = 0.480 - 0.150 - 0.300 = \mathbf{0.030}

Conclusion: Split A (Gini Gain = 0.213) is far superior to Split B (Gini Gain = 0.030). Split A isolates all 4 positives in a pure left child. Split B leaves its right child perfectly balanced — useless for prediction.

Problem 3: Proving the maximum of Gini and bounding it by misclassification rate

Hard

For binary classification with class proportion p (probability of positive):

  1. Prove, using calculus, that G(p) = 2p(1-p) attains its maximum at p = 0.5.
  2. Prove that Gini impurity is always at most twice the misclassification rate, i.e. G(t) \leq 2 E(t) for any p \in [0,1].

Part 1: Maximum via calculus.

Differentiate with respect to p:

\frac{d}{dp}[2p(1-p)] = \frac{d}{dp}[2p - 2p^2] = 2 - 4p

Set the derivative to zero:

2 - 4p = 0 \implies p = \tfrac{1}{2}

The second derivative is -4 < 0, confirming this is a maximum. At p = 0.5: G = 2(0.5)(0.5) = 0.5.

Part 2: Bounding Gini by 2E.

For binary classification, without loss of generality assume p \leq 0.5 (the positive class is not the majority). Then E = p (misclassification rate equals the minority class proportion) and G = 2p(1-p).

We need to show 2p(1-p) \leq 2p:

2p(1-p) \leq 2p \iff 1-p \leq 1 \iff p \geq 0

This holds for all p \in [0,1]. By symmetry the same argument applies when p > 0.5 (replace p with 1-p as the minority proportion). More cleanly: E = \min(p, 1-p), so

G = 2p(1-p) \leq 2\min(p,1-p) = 2E

because p(1-p) \leq \min(p,1-p) for p \in [0,1] (the product of two numbers in [0,1] is at most each factor individually).
