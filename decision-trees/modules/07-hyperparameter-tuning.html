The Tuning Mindset

Hyperparameter tuning is not a matter of randomly turning knobs until validation accuracy improves. Every hyperparameter exists for a reason that is grounded in the bias-variance framework, and understanding that reason is what separates principled tuning from trial-and-error. Before adjusting any hyperparameter, ask yourself a single question: "Am I trying to reduce bias, reduce variance, or reduce computation?"

The organizing principle of hyperparameter tuning Hyperparameters that control model complexity control the bias-variance tradeoff. Hyperparameters that control randomness reduce variance. Hyperparameters that control computation are secondary — they affect training cost, not the fundamental quality of the model.

This distinction immediately organizes the landscape. When your model underfits (high train error, high test error), you need to reduce bias — reach for parameters that increase model complexity. When your model overfits (low train error, high test error), you need to reduce variance — reach for regularization, sampling, or depth controls. When your model is too slow to train, reach for computational shortcuts.

The modules preceding this one established the mathematical foundation: decision trees define piecewise-constant functions on axis-aligned partitions; random forests reduce variance by averaging decorrelated trees; gradient boosting reduces bias iteratively through additive fitting of residuals. Each algorithm's hyperparameters map directly onto these mechanisms. Understanding the mechanism is the prerequisite for principled tuning.

From algorithms to parameters. Modules 5 and 6 derived the Random Forest variance formula \text{Var}(F) = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{(1-\rho)\sigma^2}{B} and the GB update rule F_m = F_{m-1} + \eta h_m. Every major hyperparameter in both algorithms corresponds directly to a term in one of these formulas. This module makes those correspondences explicit and translates them into practical tuning recipes.

Random Forest Hyperparameters

Random forests have a relatively small set of important hyperparameters. Because the ensemble mechanism (bagging + feature randomness) is the primary tool for variance reduction, individual trees are allowed to grow without aggressive regularization. This makes RF more forgiving to misconfiguration than gradient boosting.

n_estimators (B): The Ensemble Size

The variance formula from Module 5 is the direct justification for this parameter. For an ensemble of B trees each with variance \sigma^2 and pairwise correlation \rho:

\text{Var}(F_B) = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{(1-\rho)\sigma^2}{B}

Increasing B reduces the second term \frac{(1-\rho)\sigma^2}{B}, which represents the variance reducible by averaging. The first term \rho\sigma^2 is the irreducible correlation floor — no amount of additional trees eliminates it. This is why returns diminish after roughly 100-200 trees: the second term becomes negligible relative to the first.

Practical guidance: use OOB error to diagnose convergence. If OOB error is still decreasing at B=100, add more trees. The recommended starting point is 100-500. Computational cost scales linearly with B, so there is no benefit to overshooting. A common efficient protocol: start at B=200, plot OOB error vs B, stop at the elbow.

max_features (m_try): The Correlation Dial

max_features is the primary dial for controlling tree correlation \rho. At each split, only a random subset of m out of p features is considered. Smaller m means the trees are forced to use different features, reducing correlation but also increasing individual tree bias. The ensemble variance formula shows the tension: lower \rho is always good, but lower m also raises \sigma^2 (individual tree variance increases because some trees use suboptimal features).

For classification, \sqrt{p} is the empirically validated default. For regression, p/3. If your features are highly correlated (e.g., many redundant or collinear inputs), use smaller m to force diversification. Cross-validate over the set \{\lfloor\sqrt{p}/2\rfloor,\, \lfloor\sqrt{p}\rfloor,\, \lfloor 2\sqrt{p}\rfloor\}. This is usually the single most impactful hyperparameter to tune in a random forest.

max_depth: Tree Depth Control

In random forests, it is common practice to grow full trees (max_depth=None) because averaging across many trees controls the ensemble variance even when individual trees are deep. Limiting depth adds bias to individual trees, and since the ensemble's bias equals the average tree bias, this directly hurts performance. The exception arises when n is very large and full trees become computationally expensive. Common cross-validation values: {None, 5, 10, 20}. For most datasets, None is the best choice.

min_samples_leaf: Smooth Regularization

min_samples_leaf controls tree depth more gracefully than max_depth by preventing splits when either resulting leaf would have fewer than the specified number of samples. With the default of 1, trees can create single-sample leaves that perfectly memorize individual training points. Increasing min_samples_leaf forces leaves to represent broader regions of the feature space, yielding smoother predictions. The effect is analogous to kernel smoothing: larger leaf size averages more training points, reducing variance at the cost of resolution. Try values {1, 5, 10, 20}. Larger values are beneficial when the dataset is noisy or when features are measured with error.

Gradient Boosting Hyperparameters

Gradient boosting is substantially more sensitive to hyperparameter choices than random forests. The additive construction means overfitting compounds across iterations — one misconfigured hyperparameter can lead to severe overfitting. The flip side is that GB is also capable of higher peak performance when tuned correctly.

The Critical Interaction: learning_rate x n_estimators

The GB ensemble is:

F_M(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} \eta \cdot h_m(\mathbf{x})

The learning rate \eta shrinks each tree's contribution. The total "capacity" of the model to fit the data is proportional to the product:

\text{Total contribution} \approx \eta \cdot M \cdot \bar{h}

where \bar{h} is the average tree magnitude. If you halve \eta from 0.1 to 0.05, you need roughly double M to reach the same fitting level. This creates a fundamental trade-off: small \eta with large M generally generalizes better (the model corrects errors in smaller, more careful steps) but requires more computation. The practical recipe is to fix the "budget" \eta \times M first, then tune other parameters, then optionally reduce \eta and scale M proportionally for a final accuracy boost.

Common mistake: tuning learning_rate and n_estimators independently Never treat these as separate parameters. Their product determines effective model capacity. Setting a small learning_rate without increasing n_estimators produces an underfitted model. Setting a large learning_rate without decreasing n_estimators produces an overfitted one. Always adjust them together.

n_estimators (M): Iterations with Early Stopping

Unlike random forests where more trees always helps, more gradient boosting iterations eventually causes overfitting. The model progressively memorizes training noise. The correct approach is not to grid-search M directly, but to use early stopping: train for M_max iterations and stop when validation loss stops improving. This effectively treats M as a result of the optimization process rather than a hyperparameter. Practical range: 100-2000. With a small learning rate and early stopping, using a large M_max (e.g., 2000) is safe.

max_depth: Keep Trees Shallow

Unlike random forests where full trees are standard, gradient boosting works best with shallow trees (depth 2-6). The reason is that in GB, trees accumulate — deeper trees fit high-order interaction effects early, leaving less structured signal for subsequent trees and accelerating overfitting. Shallow trees (also called "stumps" at depth 1-2, or "weak learners" at depth 2-4) force the ensemble to build up complexity gradually across iterations. This creates a more regularized, interpretable model. The interaction with n_estimators: shallower trees need more iterations to achieve the same fit. Typical cross-validation range: {2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

subsample: Row Sampling per Iteration

At each boosting iteration, use a random fraction s \in (0, 1] of training samples (without replacement) to fit the current tree. This is the "stochastic" in stochastic gradient boosting. Lower subsample introduces randomness analogous to bagging, reducing the variance of each update and the tendency to overfit. Values below 0.5 tend to introduce too much noise. The sweet spot is typically 0.6-0.8. Note: unlike random forests, subsample without replacement is used here, so the theory is slightly different from bootstrap sampling.

colsample_bytree / max_features: Column Sampling

At each iteration (XGBoost) or at each split (sklearn), sample a random fraction of features. This is the gradient boosting analog of max_features in random forests. It serves the same variance-reducing role: forcing different trees to use different feature subsets, reducing correlation. In XGBoost, colsample_bytree controls sampling at the tree level, colsample_bylevel at the depth level, and colsample_bynode at the split level. Start with colsample_bytree in {0.5, 0.8, 1.0}.

min_child_weight / min_samples_leaf

The minimum sum of instance weights (or equivalently, number of samples) required in a leaf. This controls the minimum support needed before a region is considered stable enough to split further. Larger values prevent splits that are based on too few samples, providing regularization. In XGBoost, min_child_weight also accounts for the Hessian sum, making it more principled than a simple sample count. Try values {1, 3, 5, 10, 20}.

L1/L2 Regularization: reg_alpha and reg_lambda

XGBoost adds L1 (Lasso) and L2 (Ridge) penalties on the leaf weight magnitudes to the split criterion. The modified leaf weight optimization becomes:

w_j^* = -\frac{\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i}{\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda}, \quad \text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{(\sum g_i)^2}{\sum h_i + \lambda} + \cdots\right] - \gamma

where \lambda is the L2 regularization weight and \gamma is the minimum gain required for a split. The default \lambda = 1 already provides meaningful regularization. Start with \alpha = 0 (no L1) and tune \lambda in {0.1, 1, 10, 100}. Add L1 only when you want sparse trees (fewer splits used).

Practical Tuning Strategy

A principled tuning workflow starts from a reasonable baseline, checks for the most impactful parameters first, and uses validation evidence at each step rather than blind grid search. The following recipes have been validated across many competition datasets and real-world applications.

For Random Forests

1
Establish a baseline
Start with: n_estimators=200, max_features='sqrt', max_depth=None, min_samples_leaf=1. This is a strong default for most datasets. Compute OOB error and test error to characterize the bias-variance situation.
2
Diagnose n_estimators via OOB curve
Plot OOB error as a function of B from 10 to 300. Stop adding trees at the elbow where improvement becomes marginal (typically <0.1% per 50 additional trees). This is more reliable than a fixed default.
3
Grid search max_features
With p features, search over: {max(1, floor(√p/2)), floor(√p), floor(2√p)}. For regression also try p/3. This is usually the single most impactful change. Use cross-validation or OOB error.
4
Grid search min_samples_leaf
Try {1, 5, 10, 20}. If OOB error improves with min_samples_leaf > 1, the data is noisy or the features have measurement error. Larger values also speed up training by preventing very deep trees.
5
Optionally tune max_depth
Try {None, 10, 20} only if full trees are computationally prohibitive or if OOB error suggests variance is still the dominant problem. For most problems, max_depth=None with proper min_samples_leaf is sufficient.

For Gradient Boosting

1
Set robust regularization defaults
Start with: subsample=0.8, colsample_bytree=0.8, max_depth=4, learning_rate=0.1. These provide a moderate level of regularization that prevents severe overfitting across most datasets.
2
Find n_estimators via early stopping
For the fixed learning_rate=0.1, use early stopping on a held-out 15-20% validation set (or cross-validate). Run up to M_max=1000 iterations. Record the optimal M* at the validation loss minimum.
3
Test the budget equivalence with lower learning_rate
Try learning_rate=0.05 with approximately 2 × M* iterations. Compare validation performance. Smaller learning rates often generalize better at the cost of computation. This is the highest-leverage single change for competition settings.
4
Grid search max_depth
Search {2, 3, 4, 5, 6} with early stopping re-run for each value. Shallower trees often win on tabular data. Depth 3-4 is the most common optimum.
5
Fine-tune regularization parameters last
Only after max_depth and the learning_rate/n_estimators budget are settled, fine-tune min_child_weight, subsample, colsample_bytree, and reg_lambda. Their marginal impact is smaller than the structural choices above, but cumulatively significant.
Never tune n_estimators and learning_rate independently Their interaction is what matters. A small learning_rate with the original n_estimators is an underfitted model. A large learning_rate with large n_estimators is an overfitted one. Always use early stopping to find the right M for any given learning_rate, or adjust them proportionally.

Early Stopping

Early stopping is the most practical tool for preventing overfitting in gradient boosting. It avoids the computational cost of an exhaustive grid search over n_estimators by training the model only as long as it continues to improve on held-out data.

The algorithm is:

1
Partition the data
Reserve 10-20% of training data as a validation set. This data is not used for fitting the trees — only for monitoring generalization.
2
Train iteratively and monitor
Train for up to M_max boosting iterations. After each iteration m, compute validation loss L_val(m). Track the running minimum and record which iteration achieved it.
3
Apply the patience criterion
Stop when validation loss has not improved for n_rounds consecutive iterations (the "patience"). A larger patience allows temporary plateaus and local fluctuations before declaring convergence.
4
Restore the best model
Use the model from iteration m* that achieved the minimum validation loss — not the final model at the stopping iteration. This is the critical step that many practitioners miss.
5
Report the optimal iteration
Record M* as the hyperparameter for final retraining on the full dataset (if desired). This makes the early stopping a form of model selection rather than just regularization.

Formally, let L_\text{val}(m) be the validation loss at iteration m. Early stopping identifies:

m^* = \arg\min_{m \in \{1,\ldots,M_\text{max}\}} L_\text{val}(m)

The training loss L_\text{train}(m) is monotonically non-increasing by construction (each tree is fit to reduce the current residuals). The validation loss follows a U-shape: it decreases as the model learns genuine structure, reaches a minimum at m^*, then increases as the model begins to memorize training noise. Early stopping finds and uses this minimum.

Practice

Problem 1: Diagnosing Random Forest Overfitting

Easy

You train a random forest with n_estimators=50, max_features='sqrt' (p=100 features so m=10), max_depth=None, and observe: train accuracy=99%, OOB accuracy=72%, test accuracy=70%.

(a) What is the likely diagnosis based on the bias-variance framework?

(b) List two specific hyperparameter changes you would make and explain the expected effect on bias and variance for each change.

(a) Diagnosis: The large gap between train accuracy (99%) and OOB/test accuracy (72%) indicates high variance / overfitting. The model fits training data nearly perfectly but generalizes poorly. The bias is low (the model has sufficient capacity), but variance is the dominant problem. This is a classic overfit signature.

(b) Two interventions:

Change 1: Increase n_estimators from 50 to 200-500. From the RF variance formula \text{Var}(F_B) = \rho\sigma^2 + (1-\rho)\sigma^2/B, increasing B directly reduces the second term. At B=50 the ensemble variance is still substantially higher than the \rho\sigma^2 floor. Effect: lower variance, train accuracy may decrease slightly as the ensemble stops memorizing, OOB and test accuracy should improve.

Change 2: Increase min_samples_leaf from 1 to 5 or 10. With the default min_samples_leaf=1, trees can form single-sample leaves that perfectly memorize individual training points. Forcing leaves to contain at least 5-10 samples creates smoother, less overfit predictions. Effect: lower variance (leaves represent broader regions), slightly higher bias (cannot represent fine-grained structure), net improvement in OOB/test error expected.

Either of these changes targets variance specifically, which is the diagnosed problem. Changing max_depth or max_features could also help, but the above two are the most direct levers for this symptom pattern.

Problem 2: The Budget Theory in Practice

Medium

A gradient boosting model uses learning_rate=0.1 and n_estimators=500. The validation MSE is 0.12 at the early stopping point (iteration 320). A colleague suggests switching to learning_rate=0.01 and n_estimators=5000.

(a) According to the budget theory \eta \times M, should these two configurations produce similar results?

(b) What additional hyperparameter(s) might need adjustment alongside the colleague's suggestion?

(c) What is the main practical disadvantage of the colleague's suggestion?

(a) Budget comparison:

Original budget: \eta \times M = 0.1 \times 320 = 32 (at early stopping point).

Colleague's budget: \eta \times M = 0.01 \times 5000 = 50.

These are not equal. The colleague's configuration actually has a larger effective budget, which might produce slightly better results — or it might overfit if M_max=5000 without early stopping. However, if we apply early stopping to the 0.01/5000 configuration, the model would likely stop around iteration 3200 (to match the original budget of 32), and performance should be comparable or slightly better. So yes, the budget theory predicts similar results if early stopping is properly applied.

(b) Required companion adjustment: early stopping patience. With learning_rate=0.01, the validation curve changes much more slowly per iteration. A patience of (for example) 10 iterations that worked for learning_rate=0.1 would need to be increased to roughly 100 iterations to cover the same "effective progress." The patience parameter must scale inversely with the learning rate to detect the same amount of improvement.

(c) Practical disadvantage: computational cost. Training 5000 trees versus 320 trees requires approximately 15x more computation (assuming the early stopping properly fires at ~3200 for 0.01 rate). For large datasets this can be prohibitive. Additionally, storing and serializing a 3200+ tree model requires significantly more memory. Unless there is a measurable accuracy improvement, the original configuration with 320 trees at learning_rate=0.1 is more efficient.

Problem 3: Variance Bound Under Subsampling

Hard

For a stochastic gradient boosting model with subsample rate s and M iterations:

(a) Derive qualitatively that the variance of ensemble predictions is bounded by:

\text{Var}(F_M) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{M} + C\frac{1-s}{s}

for some constant C depending on the training data. (Hint: use the independence introduced by subsampling to decompose the variance.)

(b) Why does subsampling introduce an approximate unbiasedness guarantee even though each individual tree is fit on a biased (partial) sample?

(c) Given the bound in (a), find the value of s that minimizes total expected error if bias^2 \propto 1/M and variance \propto (1-s)/s + 1/M. (Treat M as fixed.)

(a) Qualitative derivation:

At each iteration m, the tree h_m is fit on a subsample S_m of size \lfloor sn \rfloor, drawn without replacement. The key observation: because different iterations use different random subsamples, h_m and h_{m'} are (approximately) uncorrelated when m \neq m', subject to the correlation from shared training data.

Write F_M = \sum_{m=1}^M \eta h_m. If we treat the h_m as approximately independent (valid when subsamples have low overlap):

\text{Var}(F_M) \approx M\eta^2 \text{Var}(h_m) = M\eta^2 \left[\text{Var}(\bar{h}) + \text{Var}(h_m - \bar{h})\right]

The first term \text{Var}(\bar{h}) is the variance of the expected tree (systematic component), which is O(1/M) by averaging. The second term represents the sampling variance introduced by using only a fraction s of data. By finite population sampling theory, the variance of a subsample estimate scales as (1-s)/s (the finite population correction factor). Putting these together:

\text{Var}(F_M) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{M} + C\frac{1-s}{s}

where the first term captures the benefit of averaging M estimates and the second captures the cost of using partial data at each step. The constant C depends on the variability of training labels and tree structure.

(b) Approximate unbiasedness by symmetry:

Even though each tree h_m is fit on a biased subsample (it only sees a fraction of the data), the bias is not directional. Each training point i is included in any given subsample with probability approximately s, and across the M iterations, each training point is expected to appear in approximately sM iterations. For large M, the ensemble effectively "sees" each training point multiple times with roughly equal representation. The expected contribution of each point to the final model is therefore proportional to its weight in the full dataset: \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_m \mathbf{1}[i \in S_m]\right] = sM, which is uniform across all i. This uniform coverage in expectation is the source of approximate unbiasedness.

(c) Optimal subsample rate:

The total expected error (holding M fixed) is proportional to:

E(s) = \underbrace{\frac{a}{M}}_{\text{bias}^2} + \underbrace{\frac{b(1-s)}{s} + \frac{c}{M}}_{\text{variance}}

where a, b, c are positive constants. Differentiating with respect to s:

\frac{dE}{ds} = b\frac{d}{ds}\left(\frac{1-s}{s}\right) = b\cdot\frac{-s - (1-s)}{s^2} = -\frac{b}{s^2} < 0

The derivative is strictly negative for all s \in (0,1). This means the total error is a strictly decreasing function of s — the minimum is achieved at s = 1 (no subsampling). However, this is the theoretical minimum under the assumption that the bias does not depend on s. In practice, subsampling introduces decorrelation between trees that reduces the effective correlation term (analogous to \rho in the RF formula), which the bound does not capture. The practical optimum is therefore an interior solution, empirically found around s = 0.6-0.8, where the decorrelation benefit outweighs the sampling cost.
